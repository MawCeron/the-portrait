# PROLOGUE — The Letter (1519)

## Florence

The letter arrived just after dawn, slipped under our door as quietly as a guilty thought. On the back, pressed deep into ruby wax—broken now, but unmistakable even after all these years: his mark.

Leonardo's.

I held it a long moment before calling for my husband, turning it over in my hands. The paper was thick, expensive, travel-worn at the edges. Through the broken seal I could see the careful script inside, though I didn't try to read it. Not yet. The house was still half-asleep—the servants moving quietly downstairs, our daughter-in-law humming something in the kitchen. Francesco had taken to rising late these days. He'd been back from France for weeks now, and still moved through the house like a guest in someone else's home.

"Francesco," I called softly, pushing open the door to the small room he'd claimed as his place to sulk. It had once been a storage room, which felt appropriate somehow. He was exactly where he'd been for days: hunched in the chair by the window, staring at nothing in particular, pretending he wasn't aging by holding perfectly still. The morning light caught the silver in his hair—when had it become so much? And the lines around his mouth had deepened into permanent grooves, as if his face had forgotten how to arrange itself any other way.

I stepped inside, my footsteps loud against his silence. "A letter came for you."

No reaction. Not even a flicker.

"It's from France."

Still nothing. He might have been carved from wood.

I sighed and held it out anyway, letting the broken seal catch the light. "It bears Leonardo's mark."

_That_ got him. His head jerked toward me, eyes sharp for a moment—the old Francesco, the one who used to light up at the mere mention of that name—but as soon as he recognized the symbol pressed into the wax, he recoiled as if I'd offered him a live coal.

"No." Just that. Flat and low, like a door slamming.

I crossed the room, my patience wearing thin. Sixteen years I'd watched this obsession, and now he wouldn't even look at what might be its conclusion? "At least open it."

"I said no." He pushed himself up from the chair with visible effort—his movements stiffer than they'd been even a year ago, his shoulders carrying a weight that hadn't been there before—suddenly needing to move, to pace, as if the walls were closing in. His hands opened and closed at his sides—merchant's hands, I'd always thought, built for measuring cloth and counting coins, not for whatever grief he was wrestling now.

"I don't want it," he said again, louder this time. "What's the point now?"

His voice cracked on that last word—and that frightened me more than any shouting would have. Francesco didn't crack. Not in business, not in the lean years, not even when we'd buried our first son. But now, over a piece of paper?

He kept moving, unable or unwilling to slow down, wearing a path in the floor I'd have to explain to the servants later. "He's dead, Lisa. _Dead_. Gone. And what am I supposed to do with a letter from a dead man? Read it out loud like some final blessing? Frame it next to all the other promises he never kept?"

He stopped at the far wall, one hand braced against it. Then he laughed—bitter, hollow, nothing like the sound he used to make when he'd come home from visiting Leonardo's workshop, full of stories about flying machines and anatomical studies and brilliant, impossible ideas.

"Or maybe it's an apology," he said to the wall. He let out a sharp, jagged sound—not a laugh, but the ghost of one. "Wouldn't that be something? After all these years... a neat little note from France. 'Sorry about your portrait, Francesco.' As if..." He stopped, swallowing hard. "As if a few words on paper could fix..."

I watched him struggle with words he couldn't quite say. Watched the frustration pull at the corners of his mouth, watched bitterness settle on him like dust from a road he'd traveled too many times. He had returned from France looking twenty years older, and for once it wasn't just the travel. Something in him had folded inward, like a letter sealed too tight to ever open cleanly.

He turned toward the shuttered window—morning sunlight trying to leak around the edges, promising a beautiful day he wouldn't see—and shook his head slowly. "I wasted so much time. Years, Lisa. _Years_ chasing him across Italy. Milan, Rome, back to Florence. Waiting outside workshops. Making excuses to merchants about why I was never in the office. Spending money we needed on journeys to places I wasn't wanted."

His voice dropped lower. "And for what? A painting I'll never have? A promise he forgot the moment he made it? The chance to stand in rooms where I didn't belong, pretending I understood what genius looked like?"

He pressed both palms flat against the windowsill, and I watched his shoulders sag under the weight of something I couldn't quite name. Not just disappointment. Something heavier.

"And now it belongs to a king," he said, almost spitting the word—and Francesco never spit words; he was too careful for that, always had been. "King Francis of France. A _king_. It sits in some palace with a hundred other paintings, and I'm supposed to what? Write a polite letter requesting its return? Show up at court and explain that actually, I paid for that one, and if His Majesty wouldn't mind terribly, I'd like it back?"

He gave a small, hollow laugh that hurt to hear.

"As if I would dare upset a king by asking for what was mine." He stared at the shutters without seeing them. "As if I would be upsetting a king for a portrait of my own wife..."

His voice trailed off into something too soft to catch, words dissolving into the morning air like smoke.

The silence that followed felt heavier than any of his words.

For a long moment, he didn't move. Just stood there bent at the window, a man defeated not by kings or genius or even death, but by something smaller and more devastating: the realization that perhaps none of it had ever mattered at all.

I stood there with the unopened letter in my hands, feeling the weight of it—not the paper, but everything wrapped inside the silence between us. My husband looked small. Smaller than I'd ever thought he could be. Not the man who'd built a successful silk business from his father's modest inheritance. Not the man who'd negotiated with the Medici and held his ground. Not even the man who'd spent sixteen years chasing a dream across half of Italy with the stubborn optimism of someone who believed the world owed him something if he just worked hard enough.

Just a man worn down by years of chasing someone who'd delighted the world and disappointed him.

I pressed the letter to my chest and made a decision. Not yet. Whatever was inside could wait until I understood whether my husband could bear to hear it.

The letter would wait. I understood what Francesco could not yet see—that those sixteen years hadn't been wasted at all. But to understand that, you had to go back to where it began.
