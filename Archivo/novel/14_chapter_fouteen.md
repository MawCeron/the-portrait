# CHAPTER 14 – Too Late

## May 2, 1519 – Amboise, France

Francesco always insisted he arrived in France already half-victorious.

He told me this later, much later, when the worst of the grief had dulled enough to speak about it, when he could form words without his voice breaking apart midsentence, when he'd found some way to live with what had happened. He said the morning light on the Loire Valley made everything look like a promise kept rather than a promise pending.

Soft rolling hills covered in spring green. Clean air that tasted different from Italian air—sweeter somehow, cleaner, full of possibility. Vineyards that looked as though they'd been tended by angels rather than mortal hands, the vines trained with geometric precision, the earth between them rich and dark.

He rode through all of it sitting straighter in the saddle than he had in years, he claimed. Feeling younger than his age. Feeling like the journey itself had already justified itself, regardless of what waited at its end.

He believed—and this is what broke my heart when he told me—that he was close. Closer than ever before. Closer than all those other attempts across all those other years. That this time, finally, he would end the chase on his own terms. With dignity. With resolution. With the portrait under his arm or at least with answers, with truth, with something solid to show for sixteen years of pursuing shadows.

"Imagine it, Lisa," he told me one night, months after his return, sitting in our courtyard under stars that seemed dimmer than they used to be. "I thought I would ride into Amboise like it was any other city. Find my way to the château. Greet Leonardo like an old friend—because that's what we were, weren't we? Friends. I'd settle the matter once and for all. We'd talk, we'd laugh about the years, about the chase, about his inability to finish anything. Maybe we'd share wine. And then... resolution. One way or another."

His voice was steady when he said this, but his hands shook slightly. They did that now, sometimes.

"I wasn't nervous. Not this time. Not after all the other times. I felt... ready. Prepared. Like I'd finally learned the right questions to ask, the right way to approach it all."

And I believed him. I truly did. I could picture it perfectly—my merchant husband transformed once more into that eager pilgrim I'd watched leave Florence so many times, trotting down the French road like a merchant-knight on a holy quest. Rehearsing what he'd say, adjusting his phrasing, imagining Leonardo's face lighting up with recognition. The surprise. The warmth. That particular laugh Leonardo had. The inevitable, "Ah, yes! Francesco! Your portrait! I was just thinking about it. Let me fetch it—it's around here somewhere..."

He reached the town of Amboise just after late morning. The sun was high but not yet harsh. Market day, with people moving through the streets carrying bread and vegetables and fish from the river. Normal life continuing as it always did, oblivious to the significance of moments happening within it.

He stopped a baker to ask for directions to the Château du Clos Lucé, where Leonardo lived. Used his careful French, the phrases he'd been practicing during the long journey north.

The baker blinked at him strangely, Francesco said. As if he'd asked where to find the moon, or how to reach yesterday, or some other impossible thing. Not hostile. Not confused by the question itself. Just... odd. Surprised.

Francesco repeated himself, thinking perhaps his accent had been unclear.

The baker pointed up the road, gave brief directions, and turned away quickly. Too quickly. As if not wanting to say something, or not knowing what to say.

Strange, Francesco thought. But the French were foreign. Perhaps they were always like this.

He continued on, leading his horse through the town. Stopped a pair of women drawing water from a well near the market square. The same question, more carefully pronounced this time.

The same look—surprise flickering into something else. Something that looked almost like pity. One woman's hand went to her mouth. The other just shook her head slightly and pointed the same direction the baker had indicated.

Francesco didn't understand. Not yet. Not fully. He simply thought the French had peculiar habits of staring at strangers. Or perhaps news of Leonardo's famous Italian visitor had somehow preceded him—unlikely, but he'd learned that gossip traveled in strange ways.

So he kept moving forward, following the directions, still mounted but riding slowly through the town streets, saving his questions, saving his energy for what lay ahead.

The path to the château wound gently uphill, away from the town proper, between trees heavy with new spring leaves. Birds were singing—he remembered this specifically, told me about it. The particular sweetness of their songs. The way the light filtered green through the canopy. The warmth of the sun on his shoulders after the cool morning.

Francesco said it felt like riding toward a reunion. One he'd earned. One he deserved through fifteen years of stubborn, perhaps foolish, but undeniable faith.

The château came into view as he rounded a curve in the tree-lined path—more elegant than imposing, he said. Beautiful in its proportions with its pale stone walls catching the light. Red brick accents and decorative chimneys rising against the blue sky. Conical tower roofs that looked almost whimsical. Windows reflecting the morning sun like welcoming eyes.

Not as grand as the royal château visible in the distance, but perfect in its way. A gentleman's residence fit for honoring a genius.

He reached the gate, dismounted with the careful movements of a man who'd been riding for days, and tied his horse to a post there. Brushed the dust from his coat with careful attention, the way one does before an important meeting. Adjusted his hat. Smoothed his beard. Checked his breath—still fresh enough from the bread and cheese he'd eaten that morning.

I imagine him straightening his posture then, pulling his shoulders back the way he used to before entering important negotiations. Preparing to greet the greatest mind of their age not as a supplicant, not as a merchant pestering an artist, but as an equal. As a friend.

A servant appeared in the courtyard—a young man, perhaps thirty, dressed in simple but clean livery. Not French, Francesco thought. Italian, by his look.

Francesco smiled. Confident. Almost triumphant. This was it. The moment sixteen years had been leading toward.

"Buongiorno," he said, then switching to his practiced French: "I'm here to see Maestro Leonardo da Vinci. My name is Francesco del Giocondo, from Florence. The Maestro will remember me—I'm an old friend. I've come a long way to visit him."

The servant stopped. Went very still.

Just stood there, mouth slightly open, as if Francesco had said something in a language that needed translating not through words but through reality itself.

Francesco felt something shift then. Later, he told me it was like sensing a change in air pressure before a storm, or the way you know something is wrong before anyone tells you, before you have proof, just a coldness that spreads through your chest and down your limbs.

The servant's eyes softened. His shoulders dropped.

"Pardon, monsieur..."

A pause. A breath.

And then, gently, as gently as one tells any terrible truth:

"I'm sorry, monsieur. The Maestro died this morning."

---

Francesco never tried to describe that moment to me in detail. Never found words adequate to the experience of hearing those words. He only said it felt like the ground had been pulled out from under him—silently, completely, with no warning—leaving him standing in the air with nothing to hold on to, waiting for the fall that must come but hasn't yet arrived.

He didn't speak. Couldn't. His mouth opened but no sound came out.

The servant stood there, patient and sad, probably used to delivering this news by now. It had been hours since Leonardo's death. Others had surely come. Others had surely received this same gentle devastation.

"When?" Francesco finally managed. One word. Barely a whisper.

"This morning, monsieur. Early. Peacefully, they say. The king was with him."

This morning.

Francesco had been on the road this morning. Rising with the dawn, eating his simple breakfast, mounting his horse with that sense of anticipation. Riding through the beautiful French countryside thinking about reunions and resolutions. Stopping to ask directions, receiving those strange looks he hadn't understood.

All while Leonardo was dying. Or already dead. Or dying while Francesco trotted past those vineyards thinking about what he'd say, how he'd smile, how satisfying it would be to finally, finally reach the end of this impossible quest.

The birds kept singing. He remembered this with bitter clarity. How the birds just continued their songs as if nothing had changed. As if the world hadn't just fundamentally rearranged itself.

The sun kept shining, warm on his shoulders, pleasant on his face.

The château windows glowed peacefully in the light, beautiful and indifferent.

And my husband—after sixteen years of chasing a man who never stayed still, who never remained in one place long enough to be caught, who always had somewhere else to be or something else to do—had finally, finally arrived.

On the only day Leonardo would never move again.

Too late to speak to him. Too late to be seen, to be remembered, to matter in whatever final thoughts might have crossed that extraordinary mind.

Too late to ask about the portrait, though Francesco told me later that thought didn't even occur to him in that moment. The portrait was suddenly, completely irrelevant.

Too late to simply sit together one more time. To share wine and conversation. To tell Leonardo what these sixteen years had meant. To thank him, perhaps, for the gift Francesco hadn't realized he'd been given.

Too late to understand what he'd really been seeking, though perhaps he was beginning to now, standing in that French courtyard with the news still echoing in his head.

He stood there, he told me, for a long time after the servant had stepped aside with a murmured invitation to come inside if he wished, to pay his respects, to see the place where the Maestro had lived and worked in his final years.

Just stood at that gate, staring at nothing in particular. The château. The trees. The sky. His own hands gripping the iron bars as if they were the only solid thing left in a world that had suddenly become unmoored.

Feeling—for the first time in his adult life, perhaps the first time ever—not ambition or frustration or excitement or any of the emotions that had driven him forward for so long.

Just emptiness.

A vast, echoing emptiness where something had been. Where Leonardo had been, even if only as an idea, a possibility, a destination that gave direction to Francesco's motion through the world.

And now that destination was gone.

And Francesco was just a man standing at a gate in France, having arrived too late for everything except grief.
