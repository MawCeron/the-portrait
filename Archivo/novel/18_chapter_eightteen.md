# CHAPTER 18 – The Reading

## Late June 1519 – Florence, Night

I waited until the house was completely still—the kind of deep quiet that settles only after a day spent holding a broken man together, the kind that comes when exhaustion finally defeats grief just long enough for sleep to claim its victims.

Francesco had gone to bed hours ago without eating, without speaking, without even pretending to try. I'd brought him broth that went cold on the nightstand. Offered wine he didn't touch. Suggested he might feel better if he ate something, anything, knowing even as I said it that food wasn't what he needed.

He'd just turned his face to the wall.

After he'd been still for an hour, I'd gone up to check on him. Found him lying on his back, fully clothed, staring at the ceiling with dry, exhausted eyes. Not sleeping. Not crying. Just existing in whatever grey space he'd retreated to.

"Rest, my love," I'd whispered, covering him with a blanket he didn't acknowledge.

Back downstairs, I'd listened for his footsteps, for pacing, for the restless muttering he sometimes did when he thought I couldn't hear him working through problems out loud.

Nothing.

Just silence pressing down on our home like a weight.

It was enough. It was time.

I lit a single candle—just one, intimate and warm—and sat at my writing table. Pulled the chair close. Placed the letter before me with careful hands, as if it were made of something more fragile than paper.

The seal had been broken when it arrived—not by us, but by the servant who'd carried it from France, checking perhaps that it contained nothing dangerous or heretical, or simply acting on the casual authority servants sometimes take with correspondence. Yet even broken, it felt significant. Leonardo's mark, pressed into wax that had been soft in his workshop, perhaps warmed near his hand.

I hesitated, my fingers touching the folded edge.

Opening it now might unleash something I wasn't ready to face. Might contain words that would hurt Francesco more than help him. Might be cruel, or dismissive, or simply too late to matter.

Then I thought of Francesco's face when he'd first seen the seal, that morning that felt like years ago now. The way hope and grief had collided in his expression and collapsed in the span of a single heartbeat. The way he'd recoiled from it as if burned.

I owed it to him to know what was inside. To read it first, to determine if these were words he needed to hear or words I needed to protect him from.

And I owed it to Leonardo, perhaps. To witness whatever last thing he'd wanted to say.

I owed it to both of them.

I unfolded the letter carefully, smoothing the creases where it had been pressed closed.

The handwriting was unmistakable—looping, elegant, slightly chaotic, slanting dramatically in places as though the words had been chasing each other across the page, racing to get out before they were forgotten. Leonardo's hand. Leonardo's voice. Leonardo still alive in ink and paper.

And then I read.

---

_"My dear Francesco,_

_If you are reading this, then I am gone, and you have finally stopped chasing me. I confess I always knew you would chase me to the end—it was who you were, who we both were. Men who could not let go._

_I should have finished your wife's portrait. I should have delivered it years ago, in 1504, 1508, 1510. I had a thousand opportunities."_

---

I felt my throat tighten. He had. Oh, he had. A thousand opportunities across a thousand days, and he'd taken none of them.

---

_"But I kept seeing something new in it, something I hadn't quite captured. Her smile, yes, but also something else—the mystery of a woman who watched her husband chase a dream and loved him anyway."_

---

My breath hitched. I had to stop reading for a moment, had to press my free hand against my chest where something had suddenly gone tight and painful.

I had never expected Leonardo to notice me at all, much less... that. Much less to see me clearly enough to understand what I'd been doing all those years. The watching. The loving anyway. The letting Francesco be who he needed to be even when it made no sense.

I blinked hard and kept reading, though the words were blurring slightly now.

---

_"But that's an excuse. The truth is I kept it because it kept you coming back. You were never just a patron to me, Francesco. You were a friend. One of the few who understood what it meant to chase something you could never quite catch."_

---

A hot tear slipped down my cheek and fell onto the page. I watched it land, horrified, and quickly brushed it away with my sleeve before it could smudge his words into nothing. These words. These precious, impossible, heartbreaking words.

Francesco had been right. They had been friends. Not patron and artist. Not merchant and genius. Just two men who'd understood something about each other that perhaps no one else could understand.

---

_"I have left instructions with Francesco Melzi and with King Francis. The portrait is yours. It was always meant to be yours. You need only ask for it, and it will be returned. I have vouched for your claim personally."_

---

My hands began to tremble. He had prepared for this. Had thought about it carefully. Hadn't just abandoned Francesco to uncertainty and loss. Had chosen, deliberately, to set things right—or as right as they could be—even if it took death to bring him the clarity or courage to do it.

The portrait could still come home. Could still hang on our walls. Could still be the thing Francesco had paid for, waited for, chased for sixteen years.

But then—

---

_"But I suspect—I hope—that you won't ask for it. Not because you can't upset a king, but because you understand now what I understood years ago: some things are more valuable in the chasing than in the catching."_

---

I pressed a hand to my mouth to keep the sob from escaping, but it came anyway—a small, strangled sound in the quiet room.

He'd known. Leonardo had known. Had understood what Francesco hadn't been able to understand about himself. That the portrait wasn't the point. That it had never been the point. That the value was in the pursuit, in the connection, in the story they'd created together across Italian cities and decades.

---

_"Thank you, my friend, for the chase. It made me feel less alone._

_Your faithful procrastinator,  
Leonardo"_

---

I read it again immediately. Then a third time. Slowly, carefully, as though the words might vanish if I blinked or breathed wrong. As though this letter might be the only proof that any of it had been real.

When I finally lowered the letter to the table, the candle had burned low, wax pooling at its base, spilling warm, flickering light over a surface that suddenly felt far too large for just one person to occupy.

Francesco had not been a fool.

That's what the letter said, in every line, in every careful word. He had not been a fool to chase. Had not been wrong to pursue. Had not wasted sixteen years on a one-sided obsession with someone who barely noticed him.

Leonardo had not been mocking him. Had not been cruel. Had not been simply using him or avoiding him or treating him as an inconvenience to be dodged.

There had been friendship there. A strange one, yes. Wildly uneven. Complicated by genius and social class and geography and Leonardo's complete inability to finish anything. But real. Real enough that in his last days, when he must have had a thousand more important things to consider, Leonardo da Vinci had taken the time to write to a silk merchant from Florence—a man most of the world would never consider important enough to remember.

The years of wandering, the frustrations, the missed meetings, the elaborate dodges—even being walked into the Pope's chambers to avoid a conversation about deadlines—they had meant something. Not just to my husband, who had built his identity around the chase, but to the man he'd been chasing.

_"Thank you, my friend, for the chase. It made me feel less alone."_

Leonardo had been lonely too. In all his brilliance, surrounded by admirers and patrons and students and hangers-on, he'd been lonely. And Francesco—persistent, stubborn, merchant Francesco who kept showing up—had somehow become the friend who understood. Who shared the understanding of what it meant to chase something impossible, to never be satisfied, to always see something more just beyond reach.

I folded the letter carefully, precisely, along its original creases. Held it against my chest, against my heart, and let the grief wash over me in waves.

Not just grief for Leonardo, though I mourned him too—mourned the loss of that extraordinary mind, that unique way of seeing the world.

Not just grief for Francesco, though my heart ached for my husband upstairs, lying in the dark, believing he'd wasted his life.

But grief for the strange, beautiful, impossible thing they'd created together. For the final page of a story that had always been half-comedy, half-tragedy. For a friendship neither of them had fully understood while it was happening. For all the words they'd never said to each other directly but had somehow communicated anyway across workshops and journeys and years.

I let myself cry—quietly, so as not to wake Francesco, but fully. Let the tears come until the candle guttered and went out, leaving me in darkness with Leonardo's letter pressed against my chest.

Tomorrow, I would tell my husband.

Would read him these words that would break his heart and heal it simultaneously. Would show him that the ending he thought was tragic was actually something else—something more complex, more meaningful, more true.

Would help him understand that he hadn't arrived too late for everything. That Leonardo had given him something in the end after all. Not the portrait—or not just the portrait. But proof. Confirmation. Validation that everything he'd felt, everything he'd pursued, had been real and mutual and valued.

Tonight, I simply let myself cry for both of them.

For two men who had spent sixteen years chasing each other in circles, neither one quite realizing they'd already caught something worth keeping.

For the merchant who'd wanted to be more than a merchant.

For the genius who'd wanted to be less alone.

For the woman between them, watching, loving, understanding.

For all of us, really, who chase things we can't quite name, hoping that someone will see us chasing and understand, and maybe chase alongside us for a while, even if neither of us ever catches what we're running toward.

The letter stayed pressed against my heart until I could breathe normally again.

Until I could think about tomorrow without breaking apart.

Until I was ready to give Francesco what Leonardo had left for him: not just a letter, but a gift.

The gift of knowing he hadn't been alone in the chase either.
