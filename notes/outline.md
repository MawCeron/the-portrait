# THE PORTRAIT

## A Novel about Francesco del Giocondo and Leonardo da Vinci

**Narrator:** Lisa del Giocondo (third person, through her perspective and voice)  
**Timespan:** 1503-1519  
**Tone:** Grounded historical fiction, comedic but warm, laughing _with_ Francesco

---

## PROLOGUE: The Letter (1519)

**Setting:** Del Giocondo home in Florence, weeks after Francesco's return from France

- Lisa receives a letter from France, sealed with Leonardo's mark
- Francesco is home, sulking since his return from France
- He's been withdrawn, sad, bitter - unusual for him
- Lisa tries to give him the letter - he sees Leonardo's seal
- Francesco refuses: "I don't want it. What's the point now?"
- His outburst: He wasted his life chasing a man who's now dead, a painting that belongs to a king
- "As if I would be upsetting a King" - said with bitterness, defeat
- Lisa looks at her husband - old, broken, diminished
- She holds the unopened letter
- **Lisa's pivot to reader:** _"To understand how we arrived here - at this moment, with my husband broken and a dead man's letter in my hands - I must take you back to the beginning. Back to when Francesco del Giocondo believed a portrait could change his life."_

---

## PART ONE: The Commission (1503-1504)

### "How It Began"

### Chapter 1: A Merchant's Ambition

- Establish Francesco and Lisa's life: prosperous silk merchants, comfortable but not noble
- Francesco's restlessness - he wants more than wealth, he wants _recognition_
- The social hierarchy of Florence: merchants vs. old families vs. artists/intellectuals
- Francesco's idea: commission a portrait from the greatest artist in Florence
- Lisa's practicality vs. Francesco's dreams

### Chapter 2: The Maestro

- Francesco seeks Leonardo without success, after all, he's not important just wealty
- Francesco approaches Leonardo - the negotiation, the price (significant for them)
- Francesco pays upfront (establishing the merchant's honor theme)
- Leonardo accepts - Francesco is thrilled, feels elevated already
- Lisa sits for the portrait - her perspective on Leonardo, finding him charming but distant
- Leonardo's distracted quality even then - talking about other projects while painting

### Chapter 3: The First Delays

- Weeks pass, Francesco visits the workshop
- Leonardo is there, working, but always "not quite finished"
- "Just a few more sessions, Signor Francesco"
- Francesco sees Leonardo's other work: sketches, inventions, ideas
- Leonardo involves, unwittingly, Francesco in his projects
- First seeds of admiration beyond just "the painter I hired"

### Chapter 4: Florence - The Republic's Painter

- Francesco's early attempts in Florence before Leonardo left
- Leonardo juggling multiple commissions: Battle of Anghiari, engineering for the Republic
- Francesco arrives annoyed about portrait, leaves starstruck
- Sees Leonardo consult with Machiavelli, solve engineering problems
- **The transformation:** Francesco discovers Leonardo is more than a painter
- Lisa notices the change in how Francesco talks about these visits
- _Lisa's narration:_ "He would return empty-handed but full of stories. I had married a practical man who dealt in wool and silk. I was now living with a boy who had discovered that gods walked among us."

---

## PART TWO: The Chase Begins (1504-1510)

### "The Education of Francesco del Giocondo"

### Chapter 5: The Departure

- 1504: Leonardo leaves Florence (historically: went to Milan)
- Takes the unfinished portrait with him
- Francesco's shock and confusion - this wasn't the agreement
- Lisa: "Perhaps he'll finish it in Milan and send it"
- Francesco: "Perhaps"
- But he's already planning to follow

### Chapter 6: Milan - The First Journey

- Francesco travels to Milan (expensive, time from business)
- Leonardo is working for French governor Charles d'Amboise
- Francesco tracks him down - Leonardo is painting, designing, performing
- Another charming dodge - Leonardo introduces him to important people
- Francesco returns empty-handed but full of stories
- Lisa's gentle mockery, but also sees Francesco is happier

### Chapter 7: The Pattern Establishes

- Montage of several years, several attempts
- Francesco pretends, and swear, to be furious.
- Francesco himself insist his honor is in stakes, and that now the portrait is his priority
- Leonardo moves around - back to Florence, to Rome
- Each time Francesco tracks him down
- Each dodge becomes more elaborate
- Lisa narrates the toll: expenses, time away, but also Francesco's joy in the chase
- Their children grow up with "Father is visiting Maestro Leonardo again"

### Chapter 8: Lisa's Reckoning

- Lisa's chapter - her perspective on the obsession
- Lisa suggets Raphael to comission a new portrait, Francesco refuses
- She realizes it's not about the portrait anymore
- Francesco is Leonardo's "groupie" - this is his passion
- Her decision to stop fighting it
- The comedy: it's better than his social-climbing obsession
- At least he comes home happy (eventually)
- _Lisa's reflection:_ "He didn't see it, of course. That the chase had already given him what the portrait was supposed to. But if I told him that, he might stop chasing. And I was beginning to understand that my husband needed this the way some men need wine or dice. At least Leonardo da Vinci was a relatively harmless vice."

---

## PART THREE: The Grand Tour (1513-1516)

### "Following Genius Across Italy"

### Chapter 9: Rome - The Pope Dodge (1513)

- Francesco hears Leonardo is in Rome, working for Pope Leo X
- He's older now, been chasing for 10 years
- Decides: this is it, final attempt
- The street corner encounter - Leonardo agrees to get the painting
- "You're right. Come, let's fetch your painting."
- The walk to the "workshop" - Francesco's triumph, already imagining success
- Francesco notices they're heading toward Vatican Hill - growing confusion
- Entering the Apostolic Palace - Francesco's growing dread
- They're suddenly in papal chambers
- **"Have you met His Holiness Pope Leo X?"**
- Leonardo's innocent introduction: "Your Holiness, may I present Francesco del Giocondo, a distinguished merchant of Florence"
- Pope Leo X (Giovanni de' Medici) lights up: "A Florentine! We are practically neighbors..."
- Francesco cannot be rude to the Pope, cannot demand his painting
- He can barely speak, overwhelmed
- Twenty minutes of conversation, Francesco looks around... Leonardo is gone
- Francesco emerges onto the street, dazed

### Chapter 10: The Conversation

- Francesco returns to Florence, tells Lisa everything (probably multiple times)
- She waits for the right moment, tries one more time to convince him to give up
- The gentle moment between them - she takes his hand
- "It's been ten years. Ten years of traveling, of expenses, of time away from home, from the business, from—"
- "From you," he finishes quietly
- She suggests another painter - Raphael is in Rome
- "It wouldn't be the same." "No. It would be finished."
- The pause. Francesco can't explain it, doesn't fully understand it himself
- Lisa's resignation, then lighter: **"Well, at least you can say you've met the Pope."**
- She continues: "And the Medici have been sending orders. More than before. It seems your name was mentioned favorably."
- Francesco's realization: the chase has helped his business, given him recognition
- Lisa's unspoken thought: The chase has already given him what the portrait was supposed to

### Chapter 11: The Rumors

- Years pass, less frequent attempts
- Leonardo is in Rome, then France (1516)
- Letters, rumors, reports from travelers
- Francesco aging, business thriving, but still listening for Leonardo news
- Lisa watches her husband become someone who tells Leonardo stories
- Has he become defined by this chase?

### Chapter 12: The Quiet Years

- 1517-1518: Leonardo in France, at Château du Clos Lucé, King Francis's honored guest
- Francesco knows where he is but doesn't go immediately
- Is it age? Expense? Or has something shifted?
- Lisa wonders if he's finally letting go
- But she sees him sometimes, staring at nothing, and knows he's thinking of France
- Francesco quieter, more reflective

---

## PART FOUR: The End of the Chase (1519)

### "What Was Really Painted"

### Chapter 13: The Final Journey

- Early 1519: Francesco hears Leonardo is still in France, settled at Clos Lucé
- He's been there for three years now
- Francesco (now in his 50s) makes a decision: this is it. Final attempt.
- Maybe Leonardo is ready to let go of the portrait
- Maybe Francesco just needs to see him one last time
- Lisa worried: it's far, expensive, he's not young anymore
- But Francesco is determined - something driving him
- **The journey:** Long, difficult travel to France
- Lisa narrates what Francesco told her later about his thoughts during travel
- Maybe he rehearsed what he'd say
- Maybe he imagined finally bringing the portrait home
- Or maybe he just wanted to see Leonardo again

### Chapter 14: Too Late

- **May 2, 1519:** Francesco arrives at Amboise
- Asks for directions to Château du Clos Lucé
- People's responses are strange - odd looks, sympathy
- He reaches the château
- Someone (a servant? Francesco Melzi?) meets him at the gate
- "I'm here to see Maestro Leonardo da Vinci"
- The pause. The sympathetic look.
- **"I'm sorry, signore. The Maestro died this morning."**
- The devastating timing: Francesco traveled for weeks, arrived the very day Leonardo died
- Never got to speak to him one final time
- Never got to say... what? He doesn't even know anymore

### Chapter 15: The Portrait, At Last

- Francesco in shock, grief, disbelief, the servant let them pass
- Francesco Melzi (Leonardo's devoted student and heir) is grieving but kind
- Offers to let Francesco pay respects
- Shows him where Leonardo worked
- Shows him some of the paintings
- Among them: **Lisa's portrait**, carefully set apart from the rest
- Francesco sees it, finally, after 16 years
- It's beautiful. Unfinished. Perfect. Heartbreaking.
- Melzi: "This is yours, is it not? The portrait of your wife?"
- Francesco could claim it right then and there
- Legal right, moral right, paid for it
- But he just stands there, looking at it
- For now it belongs to the king
- "It belongs here now," he says himself quietly
- And leaves France

### Chapter 16: The Return

- Francesco returns to Florence devastated
- He arrived the day Leonardo died - he was _so close_
- If he'd left a week earlier, if the journey had been faster...
- He never got to say goodbye, never got one final conversation
- And he saw the portrait - could have claimed it - and didn't
- Why? He doesn't understand his own choice
- **His bitterness:** "I wasted my life chasing a dead man"
- Spent 16 years chasing, and missed him by hours
- The portrait right there, and he walked away
- Was he a fool? Was any of it real?
- Lisa tries to comfort him, reach him - he's unreachable
- Days pass, Francesco withdrawn, bitter, aged

### Chapter 17: The Letter Arrives

- **Back to prologue moment:** The letter comes, Leonardo's seal
- Francesco refuses to read it: "What could he possibly have to say now?"
- Lisa realizes: Leonardo wrote it before he died, maybe in his final days
- Francesco: "I don't want his apologies. I don't want his excuses."
- "As if I would be upsetting a King" - hollow, defeated
- Lisa keeps the letter, unopened
- Days pass, Francesco still withdrawn
- Lisa decides she must read it

### Chapter 18: The Reading

- Lisa opens the letter, reads it alone
- **Leonardo's letter (finally revealed):**

_"My dear Francesco,_

_If you are reading this, then I am gone, and you have finally stopped chasing me. I confess I always knew you would chase me to the end - it was who you were, who we both were. Men who could not let go._

_I should have finished your wife's portrait. I should have delivered it years ago, in 1504, 1508, 1510. I had a thousand opportunities. But I kept seeing something new in it, something I hadn't quite captured. Her smile, yes, but also something else - the mystery of a woman who watched her husband chase a dream and loved him anyway._

_But that's an excuse. The truth is I kept it because it kept you coming back. You were never just a patron to me, Francesco. You were a friend. One of the few who understood what it meant to chase something you could never quite catch._

_I have left instructions with Francesco Melzi and with King Francis. The portrait is yours. It was always meant to be yours. You need only ask for it, and it will be returned. I have vouched for your claim personally._

_But I suspect - I hope - that you won't ask for it. Not because you can't upset a king, but because you understand now what I understood years ago: some things are more valuable in the chasing than in the catching._

_Thank you, my friend, for the chase. It made me feel less alone._

_Your faithful procrastinator,  
Leonardo"_

- Lisa's tears reading this
- She understands: Francesco wasn't a fool. Leonardo valued their strange friendship.
- The chase was real. The connection was real.

### Chapter 19: The Revelation

- Lisa brings the letter to Francesco: "You need to read this"
- He resists, she insists
- He reads Leonardo's words
- His reaction: tears, then slowly... that smile
- Lisa: "You could still claim it. The letter gives you authority."
- Francesco: **"As if I would be upsetting a King"** - but THIS time with the smile, with peace
- Lisa: "You saw it, didn't you? In France. You could have taken it then."
- Francesco nods
- Their conversation: why he didn't, what it meant, what Leonardo understood
- Francesco: "He called me his friend, Lisa. All these years... I was his friend."
- The acceptance, the peace

---

## EPILOGUE: The Portrait

**Lisa narrating from further in the future:**

- Francesco lived happily after reading the letter, the bitterness gone
- He told Leonardo stories until his death, but differently now - with warmth, not longing
- The letter proved what he needed to know: he wasn't a fool, he was a friend
- The portrait stayed in France, became increasingly famous
- Lisa reflects on what was really painted: not just her face, but the story of two men
- One who couldn't finish, one who couldn't let go
- Both of them perfectionists and procrastinators
- Both of them chasing something just out of reach
- The portrait immortalized her, yes, but it also immortalized their strange friendship
- **Final reflection:** "Perhaps that's as it should be. Some things are better unfinished. Some chases are better than their conclusions. And some portraits capture more than just a face - they capture the moment when a merchant and a genius understood each other, however briefly, across the distance that separated their worlds."
- Final image: Lisa imagining the portrait hanging in France, still not quite finished, still mysterious
- "It made him feel less alone," Leonardo wrote. And in the end, that's what Francesco needed too - to know he wasn't alone in his chasing, in his longing for something beyond reach.

## THEMES TO WEAVE THROUGHOUT

- **Class and aspiration:** Francesco trying to transcend merchant status
- **Completion vs. perfection:** Both men struggle with finishing things
- **What we chase vs. what we need:** Francesco chasing recognition, finding friendship
- **Marriage and understanding:** Lisa's patient wisdom
- **Art vs. commerce:** Different value systems colliding (merchant's honor vs. artist's process)
- **Fame and legacy:** Ironic - Francesco wanted recognition, became a footnote; Lisa wanted nothing, became immortal
- **Loneliness of ambition:** Both men lonely in their pursuits, finding connection through the chase
- **The value of the journey:** The chase itself was the gift, not the destination

- The actual timeline: portrait commissioned 1503, Leonardo died 1519
- Francesco Melzi as Leonardo's devoted pupil and heir

## TONAL NOTES

- Warm, affectionate comedy in the chase sequences
- Genuine admiration for both men's struggles
- Lisa's voice: intelligent, observant, loving but clear-eyed
- Never mock Francesco - laugh WITH him
- Leonardo not a villain or a mystical genius - just a brilliant, distracted procrastinator
- The tragedy is gentle, the comedy is kind
- Ending is bittersweet but ultimately hopeful
