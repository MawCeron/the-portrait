**PROLOGUE – The Letter (1519)**
_Florence_

The letter arrived just after dawn, slipped under our door as quietly as a guilty thought. I found it lying there like it had wandered in on its own — cream paper, heavy, foreign, smelling faintly of travel and cold northern air. And on the back, pressed deep into the wax, unmistakable even after all these years: his mark.

Leonardo’s.

I held it a long moment before calling for my husband. The house was still half-asleep, and he had taken to rising late. Too much time in France, he said. Different habits there. But really, ever since his return, he moved like a man carrying invisible luggage — heavy, unnecessary, impossible to set down.

“Francesco,” I called softly, pushing open the door to the room he’d claimed as his place to sulk. He was exactly where he’d been for days: hunched in the chair by the window, staring at nothing in particular, pretending he wasn’t aging by holding perfectly still.

I stepped inside. “A letter came for you.”

No reaction.

“It’s from France.”

Still nothing.

I sighed and held it out anyway. “It bears Leonardo’s seal.”

That got him. His head jerked toward me, eyes sharp for a moment — the old spark — but as soon as he recognized the symbol, he recoiled as if I’d offered him a live coal.

“No.”
Just that. Flat and low.

I crossed the room. “At least look at it.”

“I said no.” He pushed himself up from the chair, pacing like the walls were suddenly too close. “I don’t want it. What’s the point now?”

His voice cracked on that last word — and that frightened me more than any shouting would have.

He kept going, unable or unwilling to slow down. “He’s dead, Lisa. Dead. And what am I supposed to do with a letter from a dead man? Read it out loud like some final blessing?” He laughed, bitter and humorless. “Or maybe it’s an apology. Wouldn’t that be something? After all these years, a neat little apology from France.”

I watched him pace, watched the frustration pull at the corners of his mouth, watched bitterness settle on him like dust. He had returned from France looking twenty years older, and for once it wasn’t just the travel. Something in him had folded inward.

He turned toward the shuttered window — sunlight trying to leak around the edges — and shook his head. “I wasted so much time. Years chasing him. Waiting. Hoping. For what? A painting I'll never have? A promise he forgot as soon as he made it?”

He pressed his palms into the windowsill, shoulders sagging.

“And now it belongs to a king,” he said, almost spitting the word. “A king. As if I would dare upset a king by asking for what was mine.” He gave a small, hollow laugh. “As if I would be upsetting a king…” His voice trailed off into something too soft to catch.

For a long moment, he didn’t move. Just a man bent by something he could finally name.

I stood there with the unopened letter in my hands, feeling the weight of it — not the paper, but everything wrapped inside the silence between us. My husband looked small, smaller than I ever thought he could be, worn down by the years of chasing a man who delighted the world and disappointed him.

I pressed the letter to my chest.

To understand how we arrived here — at this moment, with my husband broken and a dead man’s letter in my hands — I must take you back to the beginning. Back to when Francesco del Giocondo believed a portrait could change his life.
