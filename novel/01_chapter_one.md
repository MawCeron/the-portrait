**CHAPTER 1 – A Merchant’s Ambition**
_1503 – Florence_

My husband was born with a talent for two things: selling silk and wanting more. The first kept our household fed, clothed, and respectably comfortable. The second kept him pacing across every room we ever owned, muttering about opportunity like it was a living creature that had slipped out the window whenever he blinked.

Don’t misunderstand — we were doing well. Exceptionally well, if you asked the neighbors (and they did, often, with that particular nosiness Florence had perfected). Our shop carried the finest threads, the softest satins, the kind of velvets you could drown in. People recognized our name. They nodded to us in the street. Some even smiled.

But we were merchants. Always merchants.

And in a city like Florence, that meant we stood comfortably on the staircase, but not nearly high enough to see over the heads of the old families. Wealth was acceptable — admired, even — but lineage? That was unpurchasable. And nothing tortures a man like my husband more than the idea of something he _cannot_ buy.

So while I managed the household and the children, and made sure no moth ever dared settle near our fabrics, Francesco chased… recognition. The kind that slipped through his fingers because he wanted it too desperately.

He tried joining certain circles — guild dinners where the food was terrible but the handshakes were political, gatherings where men boasted about who their great-grandfathers had feuded with. I remember one evening he came home glowing because someone had mistaken him for a cousin of a moderately notable family. He spent the next month repeating that story like it was scripture.

I listened, because I loved him. But I also rolled my eyes, because I loved him.

One afternoon, while we were having a perfectly peaceful moment with a plate of figs between us, Francesco cleared his throat — always the first sign peace was ending.

“Lisa,” he said, “I’ve been thinking.”

I set the fig down. “Should I brace myself?”

He ignored that. “We need something visible,” he continued. “Something that shows who we are. That we belong in a higher circle.”

“For example?” I asked, though I already knew the answer would be extravagant and mildly ridiculous.

He leaned forward, eyes bright — the same eyes he had when he spotted good silk. “A portrait. And not from just any painter.”

“Ah,” I said. “Here it comes.”

“From the greatest artist in Florence.”

I waited. He wanted me to ask who. It would have fed his excitement. Instead, I folded my hands and tilted my head like a patient tutor waiting for a child to finish the sentence.

He finally burst. “Leonardo da Vinci.”

I blinked. “Leonardo.”

“Yes!”

“Leonardo.” I repeated it because sometimes repetition is all that keeps a marriage steady. “The man who paints when he feels like it, finishes when the stars align, and charges as if he’s painting with powdered gemstones?”

Francesco waved that away. “Genius is eccentric.”

“Genius is expensive,” I corrected.

He stood, pacing again — his true vocation. “Think of what it would mean. People would see it and know we’re connected to greatness. We’d be associated with his brilliance.”

“Or with his inability to deliver things on time,” I said, but gently.

He stopped pacing and faced me with that determined tilt of his chin — the one that meant arguing was pointless and I might as well save my breath. “This is how families rise, Lisa. Patronage. Art. Everyone remembers who commissioned what. Florence breathes art. This is how we move upward.”

“We’re not moving upward,” I said. “We’re putting a painting on a wall.”

“Exactly!” he said triumphantly, as if I’d agreed with him.

And that — right there — was my husband in full: a dreamer in a merchant’s body, climbing ladders only he could see, convinced the next rung was just within reach if he stretched a little farther.

I looked at him, glowing with ambition, and knew there was no stopping him. Not when he got like this. I could guide him, soften the landing, keep him from tripping outright, but I couldn’t turn him away from this idea.

“Fine,” I sighed. “Go ask him.”

Francesco beamed as if I’d handed him a crown.

I didn’t know it then — how could I? — but that moment, that simple capitulation over figs and foolishness, was the beginning of everything. The chase, the frustration, the years of waiting. All of it began with a merchant who wanted more than gold could buy, and a wife who knew better but loved him all the same.
