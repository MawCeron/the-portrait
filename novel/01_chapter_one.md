# CHAPTER 1 – A Merchant's Ambition

## 1503 – Florence

My husband was born with a talent for two things: selling silk and wanting more.

The first kept our household fed, clothed, and respectably comfortable. Francesco had an eye for quality that bordered on supernatural—he could touch a bolt of fabric in dim light and tell you which region it came from, which loom had woven it, whether the dye would hold through a Florentine summer. Merchants from Pisa to Venice knew his name meant honest dealing and fair prices, which in our world was worth more than gold. Well, almost.

The second talent—the wanting—kept him pacing across every room we ever owned, muttering about opportunity like it was a living creature that had slipped out the window whenever he blinked.

Don't misunderstand me. We were doing well. Exceptionally well, if you asked the neighbors (and they did ask, often, with that particular nosiness Florence had perfected into an art form). Our shop on Via Porta Rossa carried the finest threads, the softest satins, velvets you could drown in if you weren't careful. The kind of fabrics that made wealthy women sigh and reach for their purses before they'd even asked the price. People recognized our name. They nodded to Francesco in the street—not just polite acknowledgment, but real recognition, the kind that meant respect. Some even smiled.

But we were merchants. Always merchants. And in a city like Florence, that meant something very specific.

We stood comfortably on the staircase of society, high enough to see the view, but not nearly high enough to see over the heads of the old families who'd been perched at the top since before anyone could remember. The Strozzi, the Pazzi, the Medici when they were in favor—families whose names were woven into the city's history like gold thread through brocade. They didn't just have money. They had _lineage_. Ancestors. Stories that stretched back centuries, full of condottieri and Cardinals and carefully arranged marriages that kept the power circulating like blood.

Wealth was acceptable—admired, even, in a city built on banking and trade. But lineage? That ancient, unshakeable certainty of belonging? That was unpurchasable. And nothing tortures a man like my husband more than the idea of something he _cannot_ buy.

I understood it, even if I didn't share it. I'd married Francesco when I was fifteen, more concerned with whether I'd like my new household than whether it came with a coat of arms. My father had been a merchant too—a good one—and he'd taught me that dignity came from honest work, not dusty parchments listing dead relatives. But Francesco had inherited his father's business along with something else: an hunger for more. Not more money—we had enough of that. More _meaning_. More weight. More permanence.

He wanted to matter in a way that would outlast him.

I remember the evening that crystallized this for both of us with a clarity that has never softened at the edges, no matter how many years have passed.

We had been invited—_finally_ invited, after years of Francesco cultivating connections with the patience of a gardener coaxing life from stubborn soil—to a dinner at the Palazzo Strozzi. An evening with old families. The kind who spoke of ancestors the way merchants speak of warehouses: as assets inherited rather than earned, appreciated rather than built. Families who could trace their bloodlines back to Charlemagne, or at least claimed they could with enough confidence that no one bothered to question the details.

Francesco prepared as though he were going to war, or perhaps to court—which, in its way, was both things at once. For days he consulted me on everything, turning over each small choice as if it were a strategic calculation: which doublet struck the right balance between prosperous and presumptuous? Which ring to wear—the simple gold band or the one with the small ruby that showed success without shouting it? Which stories would demonstrate ambition without sounding desperate or crass?

He rehearsed anecdotes aloud, pacing our courtyard, adjusting his phrasing when I raised an eyebrow or shook my head.

"No one wants to hear about profit margins," I told him gently after one particularly detailed story about a successful negotiation.

"But they should," he replied, earnest and slightly wounded. "It's intelligence. Strategy. It's no different from military tactics or political maneuvering."

"It's commerce," I corrected. "And they prefer intelligence that appears effortless. As if brilliant thoughts simply arrive unbidden, rather than being earned through work."

He laughed at that, but with an edge of bitterness. Still, he remembered it. Adjusted his stories accordingly.

When the evening finally came, he looked handsome—polished, confident, carrying himself with the bearing of a successful man who knew his worth. But underneath all that careful presentation, I could see hope shining through. Bright, almost childlike hope.

That last quality worried me most. Hope makes men careless. Makes them vulnerable in ways success never does.

We arrived exactly on time—not a moment too early, not a moment too late. Francesco had calculated it carefully, the way he calculated everything: not early enough to seem overeager, like merchants arriving first to a market stall; not late enough to appear disrespectful or arrogant, as if our time mattered more than theirs.

The Palazzo Strozzi rose before us like a statement carved in stone and self-assurance. Each window glowed with warm light. Each column stood with geometric perfection. The building itself seemed to announce: _We have always been here. We will always be here. And you?_

Inside, the architecture answered questions we hadn't yet asked. Ceilings soared overhead, covered in frescoes of gods and heroes who had never worried about money or reputation because such things were beneath them—or so the paintings suggested. The marble floors caught our footsteps and threw them back at us, as if even sound were required to announce itself properly in such a space.

Our home was comfortable. Warm. Filled with good furniture and fine fabrics and the evidence of successful work. But standing there, surrounded by casual magnificence, I saw it through new eyes: practical, yes. Prosperous, certainly. But unmistakably _recent_. You could smell the newness on it, the effort, the climbing.

Francesco was thirty-eight that year. At twenty-four—barely more than a boy—he'd been elected Consul of the Silk Guild, the youngest man to hold that position in living memory. His business acumen had been recognized early, his ambition matched by genuine skill. He'd built on his father's modest success and transformed it into something substantial. He negotiated with the Medici themselves. He advised other merchants. Younger men sought his counsel on contracts and trade routes.

In the world of commerce, Francesco del Giocondo was respected. Sought after. His opinion carried weight.

And yet, standing in the Palazzo Strozzi, watching these families whose wealth came from inheritance rather than effort, whose names meant more than any achievement...

None of it mattered.

Not the consulship he'd earned so young. Not the successful years of building and negotiating and proving himself. Not the respect of his peers or the trust of his clients.

Here, he was simply: merchant. New money. A man who worked.

The other guests were already mingling when we entered, and their ease was what struck me first. They moved through the space with the unselfconscious grace of people who'd been in rooms like this their entire lives. They formed clusters that had known themselves across generations—this family had married into that one three times over the past century, these two had been allies since before living memory, those three had feuded and reconciled so many times it had become a kind of tradition.

Laughter passed among them easily, like inherited silver being polished and passed down. Their rings bore family crests that meant something to everyone present. Their gestures carried the confidence of people who'd never had to prove they belonged anywhere.

No one was trying. That was the entire point.

Dinner revealed what the architecture had already suggested, but with the precision of a map marking territories.

Francesco and I were seated at the far end of the table—not insultingly so. The Strozzi were too refined for obvious slights, too conscious of their own reputation to commit actual rudeness. But far enough to establish a clear boundary. To create what they might call "appropriate placement."

Near us sat others of our kind: merchants dealing in wool and leather, a banker from a family that had made its fortune in the last generation rather than the last century, a lawyer whose father had managed to purchase a small estate and was now hoping time and patient cultivation would grow it a pedigree the way one grows grapes.

We were the new money section. The earned-rather-than-inherited section. The people-who-work-for-what-they-have section.

At the other end of the table—the end that mattered—sat the Tornabuoni, the Pitti, the Pazzi cousins, families whose names needed no introduction because they _were_ the introduction. Their names were Florence. Their voices carried down the length of the table without effort. Their laughter had weight.

Francesco tried. God, how desperately he tried.

He engaged the man beside him—a Tornabuoni cousin, I think, or perhaps a Pazzi—in conversation about trade with the Levant. Shipping routes, tariff negotiations, the delicate dance of Eastern markets and Western merchants. This was his territory, and in it he was brilliant: articulate, insightful, genuinely knowledgeable in ways that had made him Consul of the Silk Guild at an age when most men were still learning the trade from their fathers.

He'd negotiated contracts that shaped Florence's textile commerce. He'd advised the Signoria itself on matters of trade policy. Other merchants—successful, experienced men—came to him for guidance.

But here, discussing these same topics that demonstrated his intelligence and expertise in any other context, he might as well have been describing how to properly load a cart.

The conversation kept dying. Not rudely—these were educated people, well-mannered people. They responded with polite noises, asked cursory follow-up questions that showed they were listening just enough to satisfy courtesy. But their eyes wandered. Their attention drifted. And after a few exchanges, they would find reasons to turn to someone else, to let the conversation lapse into the kind of silence that feels like dismissal even when nothing explicit has been said.

The message was clear as spoken words: _We are not interested in trade talk at dinner. We do not care that you were Consul at twenty-four or that you advise the Medici on silk contracts. This is not the place. You are not reading the room._

I watched Francesco adjust, recalibrate, pivot to different topics. He tried humor—a story about a mishap at the port that was genuinely funny, told well. It earned polite smiles but no real laughter. He tried asking questions about their interests, their properties, their family histories. The answers were brief, courteous, and firmly closed the door on further inquiry.

Each attempt was met with civility that somehow felt worse than open rejection. At least rejection is honest. This was the social equivalent of being patted on the head and told to run along.

I watched him try softer tones, broader topics, anything to find common ground. Watched the small defeats accumulate. Watched him work harder and harder to belong in a space where everyone else's belonging was effortless.

Then, midway through the meal—between the fish course and the meat, I think—someone at the far end of the table made a joke.

I couldn't hear it. The distance was too great, and they weren't projecting their voices the way Francesco had been forced to do. Neither could Francesco, though I saw him tilt his head slightly, trying to catch it.

But whatever it was—some reference, some shared memory, some allusion that required years of mutual context to understand—it landed perfectly with its intended audience.

That end of the table erupted in genuine laughter. Not polite dinner chuckles. Real laughter, rich and unguarded and intimate. The kind that comes from truly shared understanding, from jokes that don't need explanation, from the comfort of people who know each other's histories so well that a single word can conjure entire stories.

The sound rolled down the long table like a wave of warmth and belonging, washing over those who understood and stopping—with almost audible precision—just short of where we sat.

Francesco looked up instinctively, drawn by the sound of joy he wasn't part of. He caught the moment—caught the tail end of whatever had caused such genuine mirth—and smiled. A real smile, hopeful and open, as if understanding might be contagious. As if by smiling he could retroactively become part of the joke, part of the group, part of the laughter that had somehow excluded him by simple virtue of distance and context.

One of the Strozzi cousins noticed him smiling. A young woman, perhaps twenty, beautiful in the careless way of people who've never had to think about it. She caught his eyes across the length of the table.

And she smiled back.

It was a kind smile. I want to be clear about that. There was no malice in it, no deliberate cruelty. She was not a cruel person.

But it was also utterly, devastatingly clear.

_You weren't meant to understand. This wasn't for you. We see you trying, and it's sweet, but you're not one of us and smiling doesn't change that._

All of that, communicated in a single expression. A gentle, almost apologetic smile that somehow managed to be kind while simultaneously drawing a line neither of them would ever cross.

Francesco's smile froze on his face. Held there for a moment suspended in time, neither fading nor staying, just caught between what he'd hoped to convey and what had actually been received.

Then it faded. Not all at once, but with a precision that broke my heart to watch—starting at his eyes, then his cheeks, finally his mouth, until his face was simply neutral again. Carefully, deliberately neutral.

He lowered his gaze to his plate and returned his attention to his food, cutting his meat with focused precision. Slowly. Deliberately. As though control over something—anything, even just the way he cut his meat—still mattered. As though by controlling this small thing he could prove he hadn't been affected by the larger thing he couldn't control.

I watched his hands. They didn't shake. He didn't let them.

I watched something settle inside him in that moment. Something that would shape the rest of our lives together, though I didn't know it yet.

Not anger. Anger would have been cleaner. Anger burns hot and fast and then exhausts itself, leaving space for other things.

This was quieter. More dangerous. Humiliation braided tightly together with resolve. Shame transformed into determination. The setting of a decision that felt, in that moment, like survival.

_I will not feel this again,_ his shoulders said. _I will find a way to belong. Or at least to matter._

The rest of the dinner passed in a blur of courses I barely tasted. Francesco was polite, responsive when spoken to, but the light had gone out of him. He'd stopped trying. Not in an obvious way—he still smiled when appropriate, still nodded at the right moments—but the reaching had ended.

He'd learned the lesson the evening had been designed to teach: You are here by invitation, not by right. We are kind to you because we are kind people. But do not mistake kindness for acceptance.

The walk home was long and silent. Florence at night wrapped around us—familiar streets that were ours in a way the Palazzo Strozzi would never be, shadows and stones we knew, buildings we'd walked past a thousand times. Our city. Our home. And yet somehow, after that dinner, it felt smaller. Less significant.

We were nearly to our door when Francesco finally spoke.

"We need something they can't ignore," he said quietly. Not to me, exactly. More like he was speaking to the night itself, testing the words aloud. "Something that marks us as people of culture, not just commerce."

I didn't ask what he meant. I knew exactly what he meant.

He meant transformation. Alchemy. The dream of turning gold—which we had—into something rarer and more valuable than gold.

Recognition. Respect. The ability to walk into a room and have people see something other than a merchant who'd worked his way up from nothing.

He wanted to transcend what we were. To become, somehow, the kind of people who belonged at that other end of the table. Who were part of the jokes instead of smiling hopefully at them from too far away.

"How?" I asked, though I wasn't sure I wanted to know.

"I don't know yet," he said. But his voice carried certainty despite the words. "But there has to be a way. Art, perhaps. Patronage. Something that shows we understand more than profit and loss."

That dinner—that single moment of laughter he wasn't meant to share, that one kind smile that had somehow cut deeper than any insult could have—was where the portrait truly began.

Oh, he wouldn't say the words for months yet. Wouldn't explicitly announce his intention to commission Leonardo da Vinci. The idea needed time to grow, to solidify, to transform from wounded pride into what he could frame as rational ambition.

But the seed was planted that night. Watered by hope and wounded pride in equal measure. Fed by the memory of how it felt to smile at a joke you didn't understand, to try so hard and still be gently, kindly, definitively excluded.

And once planted, once it had taken root in the particular soil of Francesco's need to matter in ways that transcended mere success...

Well. It was only a matter of time before it grew into something neither of us could control.

Something that would define the rest of his life, and in its own way, mine as well.

All because of one dinner. One moment. One smile that said: _You don't belong here._

And a husband who decided, in response: _Then I will create a place where I do._

So while I managed the household and the children—three living, two lost, and another on the way that spring—and made sure no moth ever dared settle near our fabrics, Francesco chased recognition. The kind that slipped through his fingers precisely because he wanted it too desperately, like trying to catch smoke.

He tried joining certain circles. Guild dinners where the food was terrible but the handshakes were political. Gatherings where men with important names stood around boasting about which condottiere their great-grandfathers had hired, or which pope had once stayed at their villa, as if proximity to power was heritable. Francesco would come home from these events vibrating with carefully controlled disappointment, trying to convince both of us that the conversations had been stimulating, the connections valuable.

I listened, because I loved him. But I also rolled my eyes when he wasn't looking, because I loved him enough to be honest with myself about his foolishness.

One afternoon in late spring—the kind of day that makes Florence feel like God's favorite city, all golden light and flower-scented air—we were having a perfectly peaceful moment in our courtyard. The children were with their tutor, the shop was closed for the day, and we'd settled with a plate of figs between us, purple-ripe and sweet. Francesco had been quiet all through our meal, which should have warned me. He was never quiet unless he was planning something.

He cleared his throat—always the first sign that peace was ending.

"Lisa," he said carefully, in that tone that meant he'd rehearsed this conversation in his head at least a dozen times, "I've been thinking."

I set my fig down and wiped my fingers on my napkin, preparing myself. "Should I brace myself, or will this be relatively painless?"

He ignored my levity with the focused determination of a man on a mission. "We need something visible," he continued, leaning forward with the intensity he usually reserved for negotiating contracts. "Something that shows who we are. That demonstrates we belong in a higher sphere."

"A higher sphere," I repeated. "Francesco, we live very comfortably. We have a lovely home, healthy children—"

"That's not what I mean." He waved this away impatiently, though I noticed he had the grace to look slightly embarrassed. "I mean culturally. Intellectually. We need to be associated with something greater than commerce."

"Greater than the commerce that pays for everything we have?"

"You know what I mean."

I did, unfortunately. "What did you have in mind?"

He took a breath, and I watched the idea settle over his face like sunlight—the same expression he got when he spotted an exceptional bolt of silk. "A portrait. Not just any portrait—a proper commission from a master artist. Something people will see and know immediately: these are people of refinement. People who understand beauty. People who belong."

I waited, because I already knew this performance had another act. Francesco loved building to his conclusions.

"And not from just any painter," he continued, unable to contain his excitement any longer. "From the greatest artist in Florence."

"Ah," I said neutrally, reaching for another fig. "Here it comes."

"From Leonardo da Vinci."

I stopped mid-reach. "Leonardo."

"Yes!" His face was bright with the possibility of it all.

"Leonardo da Vinci." I repeated it slowly because sometimes repetition is all that keeps a marriage steady, like counting to ten before responding to your children's more creative suggestions. "The man who paints when he feels like it, finishes when the stars align favorably, and charges as if he's painting with powdered gemstones and crushed unicorn horns?"

Francesco waved this objection away like he was shooing a persistent fly. "Genius is eccentric. Everyone knows that."

"Genius is expensive," I corrected, "and frequently unreliable."

"But imagine it, Lisa." He stood, unable to contain his enthusiasm while seated, and began pacing—his true vocation, really. The man could have made a career out of wearing paths in floors. "Think of what it would mean. A portrait of you, painted by Leonardo. People would see it and know immediately that we're connected to greatness. We'd be associated with his brilliance, his innovation. The Del Giocondo name would be spoken in the same breath as the greatest artist of our age."

"Or associated with his inability to deliver things on time," I pointed out, but gently, because I could see he'd already made up his mind and my role now was just to soften the landing when reality struck. "Everyone knows he left _The Adoration of the Magi_ unfinished for the monks of San Donato. And that was for the Church, Francesco. The _Church_. If he won't finish work for God, what makes you think he'll finish it for a silk merchant?"

He stopped pacing and faced me with that determined tilt of his chin—the one that meant arguing was pointless and I might as well save my breath for more productive activities, like talking to walls or reasoning with the cat. "This is how families rise, Lisa. Patronage. Art. Culture. Everyone remembers who commissioned what. The Medici didn't become the Medici just through banking—they became who they are by supporting artists. Botticelli, Michelangelo, all of them. Florence breathes art. A city built on beauty. This is how we move upward."

"We're not moving upward," I said, trying one last time to inject reality into his dream. "We're putting a painting on a wall."

"Exactly!" he said triumphantly, spreading his hands as if I'd just proven his entire argument. "A painting that will hang in our home for generations. That our children's children will see and know: our ancestors had vision. They understood what mattered."

I looked at him standing there in our courtyard, glowing with ambition like he'd swallowed the sun, and I knew there was no stopping him. Not when he got like this. When Francesco decided he wanted something—truly wanted it, not just the casual wanting that flavored his everyday thoughts—he became a force of nature. I could guide him, perhaps. Soften the eventual landing. Keep him from tripping over his own enthusiasm. But I couldn't turn him away from this idea any more than I could convince the Arno to flow backwards.

Besides, there was something almost sweet about it. My practical husband, who could calculate margins in his head faster than most men could count their fingers, who negotiated every contract with the precision of a banker and the patience of a saint, suddenly transformed into a dreamer by the thought of art. Of beauty. Of leaving some mark on the world that wasn't measured in florins and fabric.

And if I'm being honest—which I suppose I must be, telling you this story—there was a small, vain part of me that warmed to the idea. To be painted by Leonardo da Vinci. To have my face studied by those eyes that saw the world differently than anyone else. To be made immortal, in a way, by genius.

Not that I'd ever admit that to Francesco. A wife must maintain some mystery.

"Fine," I sighed, making it sound like a grand concession. "Go ask him. But when he tells you he's too busy designing war machines or studying bird flight or whatever project currently has his attention, don't say I didn't warn you."

Francesco beamed at me as if I'd handed him a crown and scepter. He crossed the courtyard in three strides, kissed my forehead with the enthusiasm of a much younger man, and practically ran toward the door.

"I'll go tomorrow," he called back. "First thing. Before he can get caught up in something else."

I watched him disappear into the house, already planning his approach, already imagining the conversation, already halfway to Leonardo's workshop in his mind.

I didn't know it then—how could I? No one sees the future clearly, no matter how much they think they understand their husband's nature. But that moment, that simple capitulation over figs and foolishness in the spring sunshine, was the beginning of everything.

The beginning of the chase. The beginning of the frustration. The beginning of sixteen years of waiting, hoping, and slowly understanding that what we chase and what we need are often entirely different things.

All of it began with a merchant who wanted something gold couldn't buy, and a wife who knew better but loved him too much to stand in his way.

God help us both.
