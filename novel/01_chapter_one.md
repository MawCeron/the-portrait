# CHAPTER 1 — A Merchant's Ambition

## 1503 — Florence

My husband was born with a talent for two things: selling silk and wanting more.

The first kept our household fed, clothed, and respectably comfortable. Francesco had an eye for quality that bordered on supernatural—he could touch a bolt of fabric in dim light and tell you which region it came from, which loom had woven it, whether the dye would hold through a Florentine summer. Merchants from Pisa to Venice knew his name meant honest dealing and fair prices, which in our world was worth more than gold.

The second talent—the wanting—kept him pacing across every room we ever owned, muttering about opportunity like it was a living creature that had slipped out the window whenever he blinked.

We were doing well. Exceptionally well, if you asked the neighbors (and they did ask, often, with that particular nosiness Florence had perfected into an art form). Our shop on Via Porta Rossa carried the finest fabrics. People recognized our name. They nodded to Francesco in the street with real respect.

But we were merchants. And in a city like Florence, that meant something very specific.

We stood on the staircase of society, high enough to see the view, but not nearly high enough to see over the heads of the old families perched at the top. The Strozzi, the Pazzi, the Medici—families whose names were woven into the city's history like gold thread through brocade. They had lineage. Ancestors. Stories that stretched back centuries.

Wealth was acceptable. But lineage? That ancient, unshakeable certainty of belonging? That was unpurchasable. And nothing tortures a man like my husband more than the idea of something he cannot buy.

I remember the evening that crystallized this for both of us with a clarity that has never softened, no matter how many years have passed.

We had been invited—_finally_ invited, after years of Francesco cultivating connections with the patience of a gardener coaxing life from stubborn soil—to a dinner at the Palazzo Strozzi.

Francesco prepared as though he were going to war. For days he consulted me on everything: which doublet struck the right balance between prosperous and presumptuous? Which ring showed success without shouting it? Which stories would demonstrate ambition without sounding desperate?

"No one wants to hear about profit margins," I told him after one particularly detailed anecdote about a successful negotiation.

"But it's intelligence," he replied, earnest and wounded. "Strategy. It's no different from military tactics."

"It's commerce," I corrected. "And they prefer intelligence that appears effortless."

He laughed, but with an edge of bitterness. Still, he adjusted his stories accordingly.

When the evening came, he looked handsome—polished, confident, carrying himself with the bearing of a successful man who knew his worth. But underneath all that careful presentation, I could see hope shining through. Bright, almost childlike hope.

That quality worried me most. Hope makes men careless. Makes them vulnerable in ways success never does.

The Palazzo Strozzi rose before us like a statement carved in stone. Each window glowed. Each column stood with geometric perfection. The building itself seemed to announce: _We have always been here. We will always be here. And you?_

Inside, ceilings soared overhead, covered in frescoes of gods and heroes who had never worried about money because such things were beneath them. The marble floors caught our footsteps and threw them back at us, as if even sound were required to announce itself properly.

Our home was comfortable. Warm. Filled with good furniture and fine fabrics and the evidence of successful work. But standing there, surrounded by casual magnificence, I saw it through new eyes: practical, yes. Prosperous, certainly. But unmistakably _recent_.

Francesco was thirty-eight that year. At twenty-four, he'd been elected Consul of the Silk Guild—the youngest man to hold that position in living memory. His business acumen had been recognized early, his ambition matched by genuine skill. He'd transformed his father's modest success into something substantial. He negotiated with the Medici themselves. Younger men sought his counsel.

In the world of commerce, Francesco del Giocondo was respected. His opinion carried weight.

And yet, standing in the Palazzo Strozzi, watching families whose wealth came from inheritance rather than effort...

None of it mattered.

Not the consulship he'd earned so young. Not the years of building and proving himself. Not the respect of his peers.

Here, he was simply: merchant. New money. A man who worked.

Dinner revealed what the architecture had already suggested, but with the precision of a map marking territories.

Francesco and I were seated at the far end of the table—not insultingly so. The Strozzi were too refined for obvious slights. But far enough to establish a clear boundary.

Near us sat others of our kind: merchants in wool and leather, a banker whose father had made his fortune in the last generation, a lawyer hoping time would grow his small estate a pedigree the way one grows grapes.

We were the new money section. The earned-rather-than-inherited section.

At the other end—the end that mattered—sat the Tornabuoni, the Pitti, the Pazzi cousins. Their voices carried down the table without effort. Their laughter had weight.

Francesco tried.

He engaged the man beside him in conversation about trade with the Levant. This was his territory, and in it he was brilliant: articulate, insightful, genuinely knowledgeable in ways that had made him Consul at an age when most men were still learning from their fathers.

But here, he might as well have been describing how to properly load a cart.

The conversation kept dying. Not rudely—these were well-mannered people. They responded with polite noises, asked cursory questions. But their eyes wandered. Their attention drifted. And after a few exchanges, they would turn to someone else.

The message was clear: _We are not interested in trade talk at dinner. This is not the place. You are not reading the room._

I watched Francesco adjust, pivot to different topics. He tried humor—a genuinely funny story about a mishap at the port. It earned polite smiles but no real laughter. He asked about their properties, their family histories. The answers were brief, courteous, and firmly closed.

Each attempt was met with civility that somehow felt worse than open rejection. At least rejection is honest.

Then, midway through the meal, someone at the far end of the table made a joke.

I couldn't hear it. Neither could Francesco, though I saw him tilt his head, trying to catch it.

But whatever it was—some reference requiring years of mutual context—it landed perfectly.

That end of the table erupted in genuine laughter. Real laughter, rich and unguarded and intimate. The kind that comes from truly shared understanding, from jokes that don't need explanation.

The sound rolled down the table like a wave, stopping—with almost audible precision—just short of where we sat.

Francesco looked up instinctively, drawn by the sound of joy he wasn't part of. He caught the tail end of whatever had caused such mirth and smiled. A real smile, hopeful and open, as if understanding might be contagious.

One of the Strozzi cousins noticed him smiling. A young woman, perhaps twenty, beautiful in the careless way of people who've never had to think about it. She caught his eyes across the length of the table.

And she smiled back.

It was a kind smile. I want to be clear about that. There was no malice in it, no deliberate cruelty.

But it was also utterly, devastatingly clear.

_You weren't meant to understand. This wasn't for you. We see you trying, and it's sweet, but you're not one of us and smiling doesn't change that._

All of that, communicated in a single expression.

Francesco's smile froze on his face. Held there for a moment suspended in time, neither fading nor staying, just caught between what he'd hoped to convey and what had actually been received.

Then it faded. Not all at once, but with precision—starting at his eyes, then his cheeks, finally his mouth, until his face was simply neutral again. Carefully, deliberately neutral.

He lowered his gaze to his plate and returned his attention to his food, cutting his meat with focused precision. Slowly. Deliberately. As though control over something—anything, even just the way he cut his meat—still mattered.

I watched his hands. They didn't shake. He didn't let them.

I watched something settle inside him in that moment. Something that would shape the rest of our lives together, though I didn't know it yet.

Not anger. Anger would have been cleaner. This was quieter. More dangerous. Humiliation braided with resolve. Shame transformed into determination.

_I will not feel this again,_ his shoulders said. _I will find a way to matter._

The walk home was long and silent. Florence at night wrapped around us—familiar streets that were ours in a way the Palazzo Strozzi would never be. Our city. Our home. And yet somehow, after that dinner, it felt smaller.

We were nearly to our door when Francesco spoke.

"We need something they can't ignore."

Not to me, exactly. More like he was speaking to the night itself.

I didn't ask what he meant. I knew.

The next two days, Francesco was unbearable.

Not angry—that would have been simpler. Just restless. Distracted. He'd start conversations and abandon them mid-sentence. He'd pick up his ledgers, stare at them without seeing, set them down. Our children learned quickly to stay out of his path when he was pacing, which he did constantly, wearing invisible tracks through every room.

I'd catch him muttering to himself. Standing at windows, hands clasped behind his back, working through something in his mind.

"...patronage, that's how they did it..."

"...not just wealthy, but..."

"...something permanent, something that shows..."

He wasn't brooding, exactly. He was calculating. Turning the problem over like a merchant examining cloth for flaws, looking for the angle, the solution, the way through.

The humiliation had transformed into something else. Fuel. Purpose. An equation he was determined to solve.

I recognized that look. Francesco solving a problem was Francesco at his most dangerous—to our finances, to our peace, to any reasonable sense of proportion.

One afternoon in late spring—the kind of day that makes Florence feel like God's favorite city, all golden light and flower-scented air—we were having a perfectly peaceful moment in our courtyard. The children were with their tutor, the shop was closed, and we'd settled with a plate of figs between us, purple-ripe and sweet.

Francesco had been quiet through our meal. Too quiet.

He cleared his throat. "Lisa."

I set my fig down, preparing myself. "Should I brace myself, or will this be relatively painless?"

"I've been thinking." He leaned forward with the intensity he usually reserved for negotiations. "We need something visible. Something that shows who we are. That demonstrates our understanding of beauty, of culture. Not just commerce."

"You've been thinking about this since the dinner," I said. Not a question.

"Yes." He had the grace to look slightly embarrassed. "We need to be associated with something greater. Art, perhaps. Patronage."

"What did you have in mind?"

He took a breath, and I watched the idea settle over his face like sunlight. "A portrait. Not just any portrait—a proper commission from a master. Something people will see and know immediately: these are people of refinement."

I waited, because I already knew this had another act.

"From Leonardo da Vinci."

I stopped reaching for my fig. "Leonardo."

"Yes!" His face was bright with possibility.

"Leonardo da Vinci. The man who paints when he feels like it, finishes when the stars align favorably, and charges as if he's painting with crushed unicorn horns?"

Francesco waved this away. "Genius is eccentric. Everyone knows that."

"Genius is expensive and frequently unreliable."

"But imagine it, Lisa." He stood—unable to contain his enthusiasm while seated—and began pacing. His true vocation, really. The man could have made a career out of wearing paths in floors. "A portrait of you, painted by Leonardo. People would see it and know we're connected to greatness. The Del Giocondo name would be spoken in the same breath as the greatest artist of our age."

"Or associated with his inability to deliver things on time," I pointed out. "Everyone knows he left _The Adoration of the Magi_ unfinished for the monks of San Donato. If he won't finish work for the Church, what makes you think he'll finish it for a silk merchant?"

He stopped and faced me with that determined tilt of his chin—the one that meant arguing was pointless. "This is how families rise, Lisa. The Medici didn't become the Medici just through banking—they became who they are by supporting artists. Botticelli, Michelangelo. This is how we move upward."

"We're not moving upward. We're putting a painting on a wall."

"Exactly!" he said, as if I'd proven his point. "A painting that will hang in our home for generations. That our children will see and know: our ancestors had vision."

I looked at him standing there, glowing with ambition, and knew there was no stopping him. When Francesco decided he wanted something—truly wanted it—he became a force of nature. I could guide him, perhaps. Soften the eventual landing. But I couldn't turn him away from this idea any more than I could convince the Arno to flow backwards.

Besides, there was something almost sweet about it. My practical husband, who could calculate margins faster than most men could count their fingers, suddenly transformed into a dreamer by the thought of art.

And if I'm being honest—which I suppose I must be—there was a small, vain part of me that warmed to the idea. To be painted by Leonardo da Vinci. To have my face studied by those eyes that saw the world differently than anyone else.

Not that I'd ever admit that to Francesco. A wife must maintain some mystery.

"Fine," I sighed, making it sound like a grand concession. "Go ask him. But when he tells you he's too busy designing war machines or studying bird flight, don't say I didn't warn you."

Francesco beamed as if I'd handed him a crown. He crossed the courtyard in three strides, kissed my forehead with the enthusiasm of a much younger man, and practically ran toward the door.

"I'll go tomorrow," he called back. "First thing."

I watched him disappear into the house, already halfway to Leonardo's workshop in his mind.

I thought it was about a portrait then. About pride and belonging and leaving some mark on the world.

I didn't yet understand what it would actually become.

God help us both.
