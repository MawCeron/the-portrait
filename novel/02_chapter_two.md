**CHAPTER 2 – The Maestro**
_1503 – Florence_

If there is one thing my husband cannot stand, it is being ignored. It chafes him worse than cheap wool. And Leonardo da Vinci, without ever intending to, managed to ignore him spectacularly.

Francesco spent two weeks searching for him — not wandering, mind you, but “strategically placing himself in proximity,” which is what he called standing near places Leonardo might pass: the workshops, the plaza, the home of a friend-of-a-friend who once saw Leonardo at Mass in 1498. Every evening, my husband returned with some new piece of useless intelligence.

“He was spotted near Santa Maria Novella yesterday.”

“The man who sharpens his chisels says Leonardo bought pigments last month.”

“I heard he went to look at a canal—imagine!”

I nodded politely through all of it. A wife learns when to nod.

Finally, one morning, fortune (and an early start) put Leonardo directly in Francesco’s path. My husband spotted him outside a workshop — hair wild, robe stained with ideas, hands already moving as though he were explaining something to invisible apprentices.

Francesco rushed home afterward, breathless with victory.

“I spoke to him!” he announced, bursting into our courtyard like a boy who’d won a footrace.

I raised an eyebrow. “Did he run away?”

“No,” he said, however grudgingly. “He… listened. In his own way.”

Which meant Leonardo stared somewhere near Francesco’s general direction while thinking about something entirely different. But for my husband, that was close enough to divine recognition.

He replayed the negotiation for me — every detail, every nuance — all while I stirred the pot on the stove.

“He said he’d consider a portrait,” Francesco told me, chest puffed. “He asked questions. About you, about the house, about the children. He said he likes to know the soul of his sitters.”

“That sounds promising,” I said.

“And then,” he added, “he named his price.”

I stopped stirring.

“Oh?”

He told me the amount.

I had to grip the spoon like I was taming a snake. “Francesco. That is a considerable sum.”

“A masterpiece requires consideration,” he replied, quoting himself as if he were already immortal.

In the merchant world, prices are negotiated, contracts are sealed, and payments are tied to milestones. But this was Leonardo — who did not believe in milestones except geological ones. And yet, when the Maestro said he needed payment upfront, my husband didn’t hesitate.

“It shows honor, Lisa,” he insisted. “It proves we’re serious.”

So he brought the full payment to Leonardo the next morning. All of it. No quibbling, no installments, no conditions. That’s how merchants show trust — and expect trust in return.

Leonardo accepted the money with a smile so absentminded it almost looked like gratitude. He tucked the coins somewhere into the folds of his robes — possibly lost forever — and announced, “Good. Now I must check on a bridge I designed last night.”

And then he wandered off.

Francesco returned home practically glowing. Not because the portrait was underway, but because he had just shaken hands with genius. He might as well have been knighted.

A week later, Leonardo invited me to sit.

His studio was a chaos that had made peace with itself: half-finished sketches, coils of rope, gears, bits of contraptions that might have been models for flying machines or corkscrews — it was impossible to tell. The place smelled of oil, metal, and a faint sweetness, like crushed flowers left too close to a hearth.

“Sit here,” Leonardo said, pointing vaguely at a stool while adjusting a canvas. His eyes flicked over me, not quite settling anywhere. “Your face is excellent. Calm. Symmetrical. Useful.”

“Useful,” I repeated. “How flattering.”

He smiled, finally focusing. “A useful face is a blessing. Some people have faces that argue with you.”

He meant it kindly, I think.

Francesco hovered nearby, thrumming with pride. “Maestro, we are honored—”

Leonardo waved a dismissive hand. “Hold that thought.”

He studied me again, narrowing his eyes, then abruptly turned away to rummage through a stack of sketches. “Have you ever considered,” he asked, “how a bird lifts off when the wind is against it? The mechanics alone—”

“Maestro,” Francesco said gently, “the portrait?”

“Ah! Yes. The portrait.” Leonardo returned to me, brushes in hand. “Tilt your chin. No—other way. Excellent.”

He dabbed the first stroke of paint, then paused. “You know,” he said thoughtfully, “if we could build a waterway connecting the Arno to the sea—”

And just like that, we were off the rails again.

It took all morning for him to settle into something resembling focus. Even then, he would suddenly ask me about my childhood, or the color of a neighbor’s duck, or whether silk merchants ever dream in patterns.

He was charming — undeniably — but distant, like his mind occupied three rooms at once and only one of them had a door.

By the time I returned home, Francesco wanted to know every detail. “Was he impressed? Did he say anything about the composition? How long will it take?”

I shrugged. “He didn’t say. He was… thinking about bridges.”

My husband laughed in that delighted, indulgent way one laughs at a child who has dropped a cake but done it adorably. “That’s genius for you, Lisa.”

I wasn’t so sure. But I could see how intoxicating it was for him — to stand close enough to brilliance that he could feel its heat.

He thought this was the beginning of everything he ever wanted.

I knew enough to suspect it was the beginning of something else entirely.
