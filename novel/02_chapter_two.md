CHAPTER 2 – The Maestro
1503 – Florence

If there is one thing my husband cannot stand, it is being ignored. It chafes him worse than cheap wool against bare skin, worse than a merchant being kept waiting in an antechamber while lesser men are received first. And Leonardo da Vinci, without ever intending cruelty—perhaps without even noticing—managed to ignore Francesco spectacularly.

Not all at once, you understand. That would have been almost merciful. No, Leonardo ignored him the way water ignores a particular stone in a river: completely, continuously, and without malice.

Francesco spent two weeks searching for him. Not wandering aimlessly, mind you, but "strategically placing himself in proximity," which is what he called standing near places Leonardo might pass. The workshop on Via Ghibellina where Leonardo sometimes borrowed tools. The Piazza della Signoria where artists gathered to argue about perspective and anatomy. The home of a friend-of-a-friend who swore he'd once seen Leonardo at Mass back in 1498, though this seemed doubtful given Leonardo's well-known opinions about organized religion.

Every evening, my husband would return with some new piece of intelligence, delivered with the gravity of a spy reporting to his prince.

"He was spotted near Santa Maria Novella yesterday afternoon. Working on something for the friars, they say."

"The man who sharpens chisels for the sculptors' guild says Leonardo bought pigments last month. Lapis lazuli, Lisa. The expensive kind."

"Someone told me he went to look at a canal proposal. Can you imagine? A canal. The mind of the man."

I nodded politely through all of it, making the appropriate sounds of interest while I mended or sorted accounts or supervised the children's lessons. A wife learns when to nod, when to make eye contact, and when she can safely let her mind wander to more practical matters like whether we had enough lamp oil to last the week.

Finally, on a Tuesday morning—Francesco had started rising at dawn for this hunt, which should have warned me how serious he was—fortune and persistence intersected. He spotted Leonardo outside a metalworker's shop on Via dei Calzaiuoli, examining some mechanism with the focused intensity of a man who'd forgotten the rest of the world existed.

Francesco described him later: hair wild and going silver, robe stained with what might have been paint or wine or both, hands already moving as though he were explaining something to invisible apprentices. He was, Francesco said, exactly how genius ought to look.

My husband rushed home afterward, breathless with victory and triumph.

"I spoke to him!" he announced, bursting into our courtyard like a boy who'd won a footrace against older, faster competitors. "Lisa! I spoke to Leonardo da Vinci!"

I looked up from the inventory ledger I'd been reviewing. "Did he run away?"

"No," Francesco said, though with enough hesitation that I suspected it had been a near thing. "He… listened. In his own way."

Which meant, I would learn later, that Leonardo had stared somewhere in Francesco's general direction while clearly thinking about something entirely different—probably whatever mechanism he'd been examining when my husband interrupted him. But for Francesco, that qualified as divine recognition.

He replayed the entire negotiation for me, every detail, every nuance, pacing the courtyard while I pretended to focus on the accounts. In truth, I was watching my husband transform before my eyes into something I'd never quite seen before: not the careful merchant, not the ambitious social climber, but something simpler and more vulnerable. A man meeting someone he admired.

"He said he'd consider a portrait," Francesco told me, chest puffed with pride. "He asked questions. About you, about the house, about our children. He said—and these were his exact words, Lisa—he said he likes to know the soul of his sitters before he paints them. The soul. As if painting were a form of knowing."

"That does sound promising," I said, and meant it. There was something touching about Leonardo caring who I was beyond just another face to render.

"And then," Francesco added, his voice shifting to that particular tone that meant the next part of the story required careful handling, "he named his price."

I set down my pen. "Oh?"

He told me the amount.

I had to grip the edge of the table to keep my composure. The number was not obscene—Leonardo was expensive, but not ruinous. But it was substantial. Very substantial. The kind of sum that would mean delaying other plans, tightening our belts in ways the children would notice, being more conservative with the household budget for the next year at least.

"Francesco," I said carefully. "That is a considerable sum."

"A masterpiece requires consideration," he replied, quoting what I suspected was his own earlier justification to himself, as if he were already practicing for posterity.

I opened my mouth to suggest negotiation—in the merchant world, everything is negotiated, prices are discussed, contracts are sealed with carefully balanced terms, and payments are tied to milestones, to deliverables, to proof of progress. But before I could speak, Francesco held up a hand.

"He needs payment upfront," he said. "All of it."

I stared at him. "All of it? Before he's even begun?"

"It shows honor, Lisa." My husband's chin lifted in that stubborn way that meant his mind was made. "It proves we're serious. That we trust him. That we're not the sort of patrons who quibble over every florin like we're buying grain at market."

"We are the sort of patrons who are buying a portrait," I pointed out. "A portrait that doesn't exist yet. Francesco, in our business, we would never—"

"This isn't our business," he interrupted gently. "This is art. It works differently."

And there it was. My practical husband, the man who could spot a dishonest contract from across a room, who never signed anything without reading it twice, who had built our comfortable life on the foundation of careful dealings and calculated risks—that man was willing to hand over a fortune to someone he'd met once, for a painting that existed only as a promise.

Because it was Leonardo. Because it was genius. Because some things, in Francesco's newly expanded worldview, were worth the leap.

I could have argued. Perhaps I should have. But I looked at his face—lit with something I hadn't seen in years, maybe ever—and I found I couldn't crush that light.

"Very well," I said. "If you think it's right."

"I do," he said firmly. Then, softer: "Thank you, Lisa."

He brought the full payment to Leonardo the very next morning. All of it. No quibbling, no installments, no conditions or clauses about delivery dates. Just good Florentine gold, handed over with a handshake and a merchant's word that this was an agreement between honorable men.

That's how Francesco understood the world: you showed trust, and you expected trust in return. You kept your promises, and you expected others to keep theirs. It was the foundation of every successful business relationship he'd ever had.

Leonardo accepted the money with a smile so absentminded it almost looked like gratitude. He tucked the coins somewhere into the folds of his robes—possibly lost forever among the sketches and notes and mysterious devices he carried everywhere—and said, "Good. Excellent. Now I must check on a bridge I designed last night. The mathematics were keeping me awake."

And then he wandered off, already lost in whatever problem was currently consuming him.

Francesco returned home practically glowing. Not because the portrait was underway—it wasn't—but because he had just shaken hands with genius. He might as well have been knighted.

"He called me signore," Francesco told me that evening, still marveling. "Not as formality—really meant it. As if I were someone worth remembering."

I smiled and said nothing. Already, I could see the shape of what was coming.

A week later, Leonardo sent word that he was ready to begin. Would I come to his studio? Would tomorrow morning suit?

Francesco insisted on accompanying me, though Leonardo's note had been addressed to me alone. "I should be there," he said. "To observe. To understand the process."

"You want to watch me sit still for hours?"

"I want to see genius at work."

There was no arguing with that kind of devotion.

Leonardo's studio occupied the ground floor of a building near Santa Maria Novella—a vast space that might once have been a warehouse or workshop. It was chaos that had made peace with itself. Half-finished sketches covered every surface, some anatomical studies that made me look away quickly, others of machines and contraptions I couldn't begin to identify. Coils of rope hung from beams. Gears and pulleys and bits of wood that might have been models for flying machines or elaborate corkscrews—it was impossible to tell. In one corner, a complete horse's skeleton stood like a patient student waiting for class to begin.

The place smelled of linseed oil and metal and something faintly sweet, like crushed flowers left too close to a hearth. Light streamed through tall windows, catching dust motes that drifted through the air like lazy snow.

Leonardo emerged from behind a canvas, wiping his hands on a cloth that might once have been white. He looked older in proper light—the silver more pronounced in his hair, lines around his eyes that spoke of squinting at details for decades. But his gaze was sharp, assessing, missing nothing.

"Ah! Signora del Giocondo," he said warmly, as if we'd been friends for years rather than strangers who'd met once briefly. "Come, come. Sit here."

He pointed vaguely at a stool positioned near the windows while he adjusted a canvas on an easel, tilting it this way and that to catch the light properly. His movements were precise despite their apparent casualness, the gestures of a man who'd done this ten thousand times.

His eyes flicked over me, quick as a bird, not quite settling anywhere for long. "Your face is excellent," he announced matter-of-factly. "Calm. Symmetrical. Very useful."

"Useful," I repeated, not sure whether to be flattered or offended. "How... charming."

He looked up, focused on me properly for the first time, and smiled—a real smile, warm and slightly mischievous. "A useful face is a genuine blessing, signora. Some people have faces that argue with you. All sharp angles and conflicting lines. Exhausting to paint. Yours is peaceful. Harmonious. It will be a pleasure to study."

He meant it kindly, I realized. In Leonardo's world, being useful was perhaps the highest compliment. A thing that served its purpose well. A tool that fit the hand. A face that didn't fight the brush.

Still, there was something unsettling about it.

I had the peculiar sensation of being unrolled, like a bolt of fabric on a merchant's table—examined for flaws, measured for worth, assessed for what it might become. Not undressed, exactly, but _revealed_. Exposed in ways that had nothing to do with modesty and everything to do with being seen as a collection of parts rather than a whole person.

His gaze was not improper. Leonardo's eyes held no hunger, no desire, nothing that would have offended Francesco or made me uncomfortable in the way men's looks sometimes did in the market. But it was relentless. Clinical. He looked at me the way Francesco looked at silk: assessing weave, identifying strengths and weaknesses, calculating promise.

And just as with silk, once you notice the inspection, it becomes exhausting to endure.

Sitting there, holding still while his eyes moved across my face with the precision of someone solving a mathematical proof, I became acutely aware of my own features in a way I never had been before.

I noticed, for the first time in my twenty-four years, that my left eye sat slightly lower than my right. Not dramatically—no one had ever mentioned it, and I'd certainly never caught it in my reflection in water or polished metal. But under Leonardo's gaze, studying the proportions and symmetries of my face as if they were architectural elements, I suddenly _felt_ the asymmetry.

I had lived with this face my entire life without remark. Now I wondered how I had never seen it. How I had missed this small imbalance that Leonardo had probably noticed within moments of me sitting down.

I wondered if he saw it as a problem to be corrected. Or as interest to be emphasized. Whether the painted version of me would have level eyes, or if he'd preserve—even exaggerate—the flaw.

I wondered, more uncomfortably, whether the painting would capture who I actually was, or who he _thought_ I was.

The distinction suddenly seemed important in a way I couldn't quite articulate. Outside that studio, my husband was chasing an idea—reputation, cultural refinement, a future version of himself standing proudly beneath a finished masterpiece, gesturing to admiring guests: "My wife. Painted by Leonardo da Vinci himself."

Inside, I was the physical reality of that ambition. A woman on an uncomfortable stool. Spine already beginning to ache from holding still. Hands folded just so because he'd positioned them three times before being satisfied. Trying not to sneeze from the dust motes floating through the light. Trying not to fidget. Trying not to think about how long this would take or how many more sessions there would be.

Leonardo spoke in his distracted way of mystery and essence and balance. Of capturing not just appearance but some deeper truth. Of light and shadow and the geometry of beauty.

I thought about whether we had enough flour left for the week's bread. Whether our son's cough sounded worse at night than it had in the morning. Whether the light would shift soon and force me to hold this position even longer.

The gap between what he was trying to capture and what I actually was yawned before me like a chasm I could fall into.

Somewhere between us, I realized, a third woman was being created. One who existed only on that wooden panel, in layers of pigment and oil. She was not quite me—she would never have the concerns or irritations or small pains of my actual body. And she was not quite whatever Leonardo imagined when he studied my face—she would be limited by what his hands could actually execute, by what the paint would allow.

She would never worry about bread or children. She would never fidget or need to use the privy or have her stomach growl embarrassingly during a sitting. She would never be tired or bored or wonder how much longer this would take.

She would sit forever, suspended between thought and expression, caught in whatever moment Leonardo decided was worth preserving.

And people would look at her—at the painted version of me—and think they were seeing Lisa Gherardini. Think they were seeing _me_.

But they would be seeing someone else entirely. Some ideal. Some mystery. Some woman Leonardo had invented using my face as raw material.

There were moments when I felt utterly visible and completely invisible at once. Leonardo was looking at me so intensely, studying me with such focus, that I had never been more _seen_ by anyone. And yet—he did not see Lisa. He did not see the woman who'd woken that morning and argued with the baker's daughter about the price of eggs. Who'd soothed a crying child. Who'd helped Francesco practice what he'd say when he negotiated with the Medici agents.

He saw a problem to solve. A harmony to achieve. A collection of curves and shadows and proportions that either pleased him or didn't. A face that, thankfully, did not fight him.

Not Lisa. Just a useful face.

And yet—

Sometimes—

His brush would pause mid-stroke. His eyes would narrow, not critically as when he was assessing proportion, but _curiously_, as if he had stumbled upon something unexpected. Something he hadn't planned for. An expression or quality that surprised him.

"You know," he said once, tilting his head to study me from a different angle, "you have a rather knowing expression."

"I do?" I asked, startled both by the comment and by the break in the long silence.

"Yes." He leaned back slightly, still studying me. "As if you are aware of something the rest of us have missed. Some joke we're not in on. Or perhaps some truth we're too busy to notice."

I had no idea what he meant. I wasn't thinking anything particularly knowing. I was thinking about nothing at all, really. Or rather, about everything too ordinary and mundane to name. The small cascade of thoughts that fill the spaces when you're sitting still with nothing to occupy your hands: Did I remember to tell the servant about the torn hem? Was that spot on Piero's cheek just dirt or the beginning of a rash? Would Francesco remember to bring home the good olive oil or would I have to remind him again?

Nothing knowing. Nothing mysterious. Just the ordinary clutter of a woman's mind during a moment of forced stillness.

"Hold that," Leonardo murmured, picking up his brush again with renewed energy. "Yes—that. That is exactly it."

"What is?" I asked, confused.

But he didn't answer. He was already painting again, lost in whatever he'd seen that I hadn't known I was showing.

He never did answer such questions. It was as if the things he saw in faces weren't things that could be explained with words. Only captured with paint, if one was skilled enough. If one looked hard enough. If one was patient enough to wait for the right expression to surface.

Over subsequent sittings—and there would be many, though I didn't know that yet—he returned again and again to my mouth. To the way it rested when I wasn't thinking about it.

"Don't smile," he'd say. "But don't frown either."

"What should I do, then?"

"Just... let it rest as it pleases. Think of something neutral."

So I tried to think of nothing. Or perhaps of everything cancelling itself out—happy thoughts balanced against worrying ones, pleasant memories checked by tedious tasks ahead. I thought of the quiet between breaths. Of the moment just before sleep, when your mind hasn't quite gone dark but has stopped actively thinking. Of staring into space while waiting for water to boil, present but absent, there but elsewhere.

"Not sad," he said, studying my mouth with almost painful intensity. "Not happy. Something else. Something in between."

_In between what?_ I wanted to ask. Between emotions? Between thoughts? Between being and not being?

But I didn't ask. I just sat there, trying to let my face do whatever it apparently did when I wasn't controlling it. Trying to achieve this impossible state of being neither one thing nor another.

If the world later decided there was mystery in my expression—and I'm told they have, that people study that painting and debate what the woman in it is thinking, what secret lies behind that subtle curve of her lips—then it must have been born there, in that moment.

In the simple effort of a woman trying very hard to think about nothing at all.

Leonardo waved a dismissive hand without looking at him. "Yes, yes. Hold that thought."

He studied me again, eyes narrowed, head tilted like he was solving a geometric proof. Then he abruptly turned away to rummage through a stack of sketches on a nearby table, muttering to himself.

"Have you ever considered," he asked, apparently to no one in particular, "how a bird manages to lift off when the wind is against it? The mechanics alone are fascinating. The way the wing adjusts mid-stroke, the angle of the feathers..."

"Maestro," Francesco said gently, trying to redirect him. "The portrait?"

"Hmm? Ah! Yes. The portrait." Leonardo returned to me, selecting brushes from a jar with the care of a surgeon choosing instruments. "Tilt your chin. No—the other way. Down slightly. Yes. Excellent. Hold that."

He made his first stroke, a thin line of umber establishing some invisible boundary on the canvas. Then he paused, brush raised, staring at nothing.

"You know," he said thoughtfully, "if we could build a proper waterway connecting the Arno directly to the sea, bypassing all those troublesome bends... the commercial implications alone would transform Florence. Have you ever thought about the mathematics of water flow? Remarkable substance, water. Refuses to compress, always seeks its level..."

And just like that, we were off the rails.

Francesco shot me a helpless look. I tried not to laugh.

It took all morning for Leonardo to settle into something resembling focus, and even then he would suddenly break off to ask me questions that had nothing to do with painting. Had I been happy as a child? What did I think about when I couldn't sleep? Did silk merchants ever dream in patterns? What was the first thing I remembered seeing?

He was charming—undeniably, effortlessly charming in the way very intelligent people sometimes are when they've stopped trying to be anything other than themselves. But he was also distant, distracted, like his mind occupied three rooms at once and only one of them had a door.

By the time he declared the session finished—which seemed to be when he noticed the light had changed rather than any actual milestone in the work—I was exhausted from holding still and Francesco was hoarse from trying to steer conversations back to art.

"Come back next week," Leonardo said cheerfully, already cleaning brushes with the same distracted precision he brought to everything. "Same time. We're making excellent progress."

I glanced at the canvas as we left. There was almost nothing on it. A few lines. Some shadows. The ghost of a composition.

By the time we returned home, Francesco wanted to know every detail. He'd been there, of course, had seen it all himself, but he needed me to confirm his experience, to tell him what I'd noticed, what I'd felt.

"Was he impressed? Did he say anything about the composition? How long do you think it will take?"

I pulled off my good shawl and draped it carefully over a chair. "He didn't say. He was mostly thinking about bridges and birds and water systems."

My husband laughed—that delighted, indulgent sound you make when a child has done something disruptive but somehow endearing. "That's genius for you, Lisa. The mind works differently. Sees connections we can't imagine."

I wasn't so sure. But I could see how intoxicating it was for Francesco—to stand close enough to brilliance that he could feel its heat, even if he couldn't quite touch the flame.

He thought this was the beginning of everything he'd ever wanted. The first step toward the recognition he craved, the cultural capital that would elevate us beyond mere merchants into something more lasting.

I knew enough to suspect it was the beginning of something else entirely.

Something that would take much longer, cost much more, and change us in ways we couldn't yet imagine.

But that afternoon, watching my husband glow with reflected glory, I kept my suspicions to myself.
