# CHAPTER 3 – The First Delays

## 1503 – Florence

The first delay didn't even feel like a delay. That's how these things sneak up on you—quietly, politely, the way a cat slips into a room you thought you'd closed and locked, and suddenly you turn around to find it curled on your best cushion looking perfectly innocent.

A week after my first sitting, Leonardo sent word that he'd need another session "soon." The vagueness of "soon" should have warned me, but Francesco took it as encouragement. Two days later, he announced he would "casually" stop by the Maestro's workshop to inquire about scheduling.

Casual, in my husband's vocabulary, meant rehearsing a greeting at least three times while standing in front of our courtyard fountain, adjusting his doublet repeatedly, and pretending he just happened to be in that neighborhood carrying a small gift of perfectly arranged figs wrapped in linen.

"You're going to intimidate him with fruit?" I asked.

"I'm being gracious," Francesco replied with dignity. "Artists appreciate gestures."

"Artists appreciate payment," I said. "Which you've already provided. Generously."

But he was already halfway out the door, figs in hand, purpose in his stride.

He returned an hour later—cheeks flushed from walking quickly, hair wind-tossed in a way that made him look younger, a man vibrating somewhere between pride and confusion. I recognized that particular combination. It meant Leonardo had charmed him again.

"Well?" I asked, not looking up from the bread I was slicing for our midday meal.

"He's working," Francesco said, setting the empty linen on the table. So Leonardo had accepted the figs. That was something.

"Was he working on _my_ portrait?"

"Yes," he answered—far too quickly, the way one answers when the truth is more complicated than a simple affirmative. "That is, he said he's 'continuing to develop the essence of the composition.' Which I assume represents progress."

I raised an eyebrow and set down the knife. "Did he show it to you?"

A pause. Just long enough to be telling.

"He said it was at a delicate stage," Francesco replied carefully. "That looking at it now would be like... like judging bread before it's finished rising. His exact words."

Which meant no. He hadn't shown Francesco anything. But my husband wasn't ready to accept that answer as the polite dismissal it clearly was, so he pressed on with determined optimism.

"He did say we'll need a few more sessions. Just a few. To capture the light properly. He was very specific about the light, Lisa. He talked about it for quite some time."

"I'm sure he did," I said gently.

A few, in Leonardo's world, could mean anything from three days to three cycles of the moon to an entire age of mankind—but I let Francesco enjoy his hope. What harm was there in it? The man had paid good money for a portrait. He deserved to believe it would materialize eventually.

And so the weeks passed in this new rhythm. Francesco would find excuses to drop by the workshop—sometimes with small gifts, sometimes with "questions about the sitting schedule," sometimes simply because he was "in the area" despite the fact that Leonardo's workshop was nowhere near any place my husband had legitimate business being.

Each visit came back the same: Leonardo smiling warmly, gesturing grandly with those elegant hands, and insisting the portrait was progressing beautifully but needed "just a little more time to resolve certain technical challenges." The challenges were never specified. The timeline was never clarified. But Francesco would return home satisfied that progress was being made, somewhere, somehow, in the mysterious realm where genius operated.

I should have been annoyed. Perhaps I should have put my foot down, insisted Francesco stop pestering the poor man and let him work. But in truth, I found it rather sweet. My practical husband, transformed into a pilgrim making offerings at the shrine of art.

Then came the afternoon that changed things.

It was late September, the air finally cooling after a brutal summer, and Francesco returned from one of his "casual visits" looking different. Not glowing with secondhand glory or frustrated by vague promises—something broader, deeper. Excitement mixed with wonder, with a touch of awe that made him look almost boyish.

He found me in the courtyard supervising while our youngest tried to catch a chicken that had escaped from the neighbor's coop. Francesco pulled me aside, away from the chaos, like he was about to reveal a scandal or a secret too precious to share with just anyone.

"Lisa," he whispered, his eyes bright, "you will not believe the things that man is working on."

"I can imagine," I said, thinking of the cluttered studio, the half-finished projects, the horse skeleton standing sentinel in the corner.

"No," he insisted, gripping my arm with unusual intensity. "You truly can't. He showed me his notebooks, Lisa. Pages and pages of sketches, designs, ideas. Flying machines! Actual machines meant to carry a man through the air like a bird. Wings shaped like bat wings, enormous contraptions with gears and pulleys and frames that would need to be light as hollow bones but strong as steel."

I blinked. "He showed you designs for flying machines."

"He didn't just show me—he _explained_ them. The mechanics of it. How a bird's wing isn't just flapping but creating something he calls 'lift,' how the air itself becomes solid if you push against it correctly. He says the human body could be lighter than wind if we understand its nature properly, if we build the frame right, if we calculate the ratios..." Francesco's hands were moving now, trying to sketch invisible shapes in the air. "I didn't understand half of it, but Lisa, the _possibility_ of it—"

"And did you tell him your nature is not suited for flying?" I asked, smiling despite myself.

"Ha. Very funny." But he was too enchanted to even pretend to be offended. "That's not the point. The point is he's thinking about things no one else thinks about. Seeing solutions to problems most people don't even know exist."

He pulled me over to sit on the courtyard bench, needing me to understand, needing to share what he'd witnessed.

"He showed me a design for a machine that moves water uphill—uphill, Lisa, against its nature—using nothing but a screw rotating in a cylinder. And a siege weapon that looks like a giant crossbow, but hinged in a way that multiplies the force. And sketches of the human heart that were so detailed I felt like I was seeing inside a living person." He paused, shaking his head in wonder. "And then—and this is the part that..." He trailed off, searching for words.

"What?" I prompted.

"He asked my opinion."

I waited for the rest of the sentence, but apparently that was the complete thought. "Your opinion on... the human heart?"

"On the siege weapon," Francesco clarified, though this did not particularly reduce the absurdity. "On the wood, specifically. He was designing the frame and he asked which wood a merchant like myself would trust for something that needed to be both strong and flexible. I told him walnut, naturally—best grain structure, doesn't split easily under stress. And he nodded, Lisa. Very thoughtfully, very seriously, as if I'd said something genuinely wise. Then he made notes in his book."

I could picture it exactly: Leonardo, distracted but genuinely appreciative, listening to my husband with that focused attention he gave to anything that interested him—which was nearly everything—treating Francesco's merchant knowledge as valuable, as worthy of consideration.

Francesco wasn't finished. "We ended up discussing the price of walnut in Venice. Which led to the cost of transporting lumber from the mountains. Which led to silk, since I mentioned we deal in fabrics, and he wanted to know about trade routes through the Ottoman territories and whether certain dyes were truly more expensive or just marketed that way." He laughed, a sound of pure delight. "He asked real questions, Lisa. Listened to the answers. Didn't treat me like just another patron writing checks."

"That must have been..." I searched for the right word. "...gratifying."

"It was extraordinary," he said simply.

There was a pause. I watched a cloud cross the sun, shadow sliding across the courtyard stones.

"And the portrait?" I asked gently.

"Oh." Francesco blinked, as though returning from a great distance. "Yes. Still in progress. He mentioned something about the hands being particularly challenging, getting the position right. Said he'll need me to bring you for another sitting soon. Just a few more sessions."

But he said it differently now. Not with frustration or even impatience. He said it almost fondly, the way one speaks of a friend's endearing flaw. The delay had become acceptable, perhaps even welcome, because it meant more visits, more conversations, more glimpses into that extraordinary mind.

The frustration had softened into something else entirely—admiration. Not the distant admiration of a patron for an artist whose work he appreciates, but something more personal. The admiration of a man who'd been allowed, however briefly, to stand beside genius and be treated as an equal.

After that, Francesco found reasons to stop by the workshop more often. At first, he maintained the pretense that he was checking on the work, inquiring about scheduling, ensuring Leonardo had everything he needed. But the excuses grew thinner and more transparent with each visit.

He would bring small items Leonardo "might find useful": a scrap of particularly fine silk to test how light affected different textures, a length of strong cord he'd gotten from a ship-maker, a piece of smooth walnut to experiment with carving, a vial of expensive crimson dye from the East.

Each gift came with a plausible explanation, but none of them fooled me. I'd been married to the man for years. I knew the difference between Francesco conducting business and Francesco pursuing something else entirely.

This wasn't about the portrait anymore. Not entirely. Perhaps not even primarily.

Francesco had stepped into Leonardo's world—cluttered, brilliant, chaotic, half-finished, overflowing with ideas and possibilities—and he liked it there. More than liked it. He'd found something in that workshop that he hadn't found in any guild hall or merchant meeting or dinner party with important families.

He'd found curiosity welcomed rather than dismissed. Intelligence engaged rather than measured by lineage. Questions asked not because they led to contracts but because the answers themselves were worth knowing.

It stirred something in him I hadn't seen before, or perhaps something that had always been there but never had space to grow. A genuine curiosity about how things worked, why they worked, what might be possible if you thought beyond the obvious. An excitement that had nothing to do with social advancement or recognition or leaving a legacy.

Just the pure, simple pleasure of learning something new from someone brilliant.

These were the first seeds of an admiration that would later grow into something far more complicated—an obsession, perhaps, or a devotion, or maybe just a deep and uncomplicated affection for a man who saw the world differently than anyone else Francesco had ever known.

He would come home from these visits glowing, full of stories about whatever Leonardo had shown him or explained to him or asked him about. Flying machines and water systems and the mathematics of perspective and why certain pigments lasted longer than others and whether it was true that Chinese silk was superior to Italian or just differently processed.

I would listen, because I loved him. Because I found his enthusiasm endearing even when I couldn't follow all the technical details. Because it made him happy in a way that had nothing to do with money or status or any of the things he usually chased.

But I also worried, just a little.

Because I could see what was happening. My husband wasn't just visiting an artist anymore. He was visiting a friend. Or at least, he believed he was. And while Leonardo was kind and welcoming and genuinely seemed to enjoy Francesco's company—as much as Leonardo enjoyed anyone's company when his mind was only partly present—I wasn't sure they were having the same relationship.

Francesco thought he'd been invited into genius's inner circle. Leonardo, I suspected, simply enjoyed having someone to talk to who knew about trade routes and lumber quality and didn't expect him to finish anything.

Though I didn't know it then—couldn't have known it, really, any more than one can predict where a river will bend years downstream—I was watching the beginning of something that would define the rest of Francesco's life.

A man chasing genius rarely notices how far he's wandered from the path he meant to follow. He's too busy looking up at the stars to see he's walking in circles.

And Leonardo, brilliant and distracted and kind, probably never realized he'd become the center of someone else's orbit.
