**CHAPTER 3 – The First Delays**
_1503 – Florence_

The first delay didn’t even feel like a delay. That’s how these things sneak up on you — quietly, politely, the way a cat slips into a room you thought was closed.

A week after my first sitting, Francesco announced he would “casually” stop by the Maestro’s workshop. Casual, in my husband’s vocabulary, meant rehearsing a greeting three times and pretending he just happened to be in the neighborhood carrying a small gift of figs.

He returned an hour later, cheeks flushed, hair wind-tossed, a man vibrating somewhere between pride and confusion.

“Well?” I asked, slicing bread.

“He’s working,” Francesco said. “On many things.”

“Was he working on _my_ portrait?”

“Yes,” he answered — far too quickly. “That is, he said he’s ‘continuing to develop the essence.’ Which I assume is progress.”

I raised an eyebrow. “Did he show it to you?”

“He said it was at a delicate stage.” Which meant no. But my husband wasn’t ready to accept that answer, so he pressed on. “He did say we’ll need a few more sessions. Just a few.”

A few, in Leonardo’s world, could mean anything from three days to three revolutions of the moon to an age of mankind — but I let Francesco enjoy his hope.

And so the weeks passed, with my husband dropping by the workshop like a farmer checking on an orchard. Each visit came back the same: Leonardo smiling warmly, gesturing grandly, and insisting the portrait needed “just a little more time.”

Then came the afternoon that changed things.

Francesco returned, not glowing with frustration or pride this time, but something broader — excitement, wonder, maybe a bit of awe. He pulled me aside like he was about to reveal a scandal.

“Lisa,” he whispered, “you will not believe the things that man is working on.”

“I can imagine,” I said. I had seen the chaos in that studio.

“No, you can’t,” he insisted. “He showed me sketches — flying machines, Lisa! Wings shaped like bat wings, enormous contraptions meant to lift a man into the air. He says the body could be lighter than the wind if we understand its nature properly.”

I smiled. “And did you tell him your nature is not suited for flying?”

“Ha. Very funny.” He was too enchanted to even pretend to be offended. “He showed me a design for a machine that moves water uphill. And a siege weapon that looks like a giant crossbow. And—and—he asked my opinion!”

“Your opinion. On siege weapons.”

“On the wood,” Francesco clarified, though this did not particularly reduce the absurdity. “He asked which wood a merchant like myself would trust for the frame. I told him walnut, naturally. And he nodded, very thoughtfully, as if I’d said something wise.”

I could picture it exactly: Leonardo, distracted but appreciative, listening to my husband like one listens to an enthusiastic visitor at a feast — pleasantly, but with one ear still tuned to the music.

Francesco continued, “We ended up discussing the price of walnut in Venice. And then silk. And then trade routes. He asked questions, Lisa. Real questions.”

“And the portrait?”

“Oh, yes,” he said, as though remembering an old friend. “Still in progress. Just a few more sessions.”

He said it lightly now, almost fondly. The frustration had softened into something else — admiration. Not the admiration of a patron for an artist, but of a man dazzled against his will.

After that, Francesco found reasons to stop by more often. At first, he claimed he was checking on the work. But soon he was bringing small items Leonardo “might find useful”: a scrap of fine cloth, a length of cord, a piece of smooth wood to test carvings on.

Each gift came with an excuse, but none fooled me.

This wasn’t about the portrait anymore. Not entirely.

Francesco had stepped into Leonardo’s world — cluttered, brilliant, half-finished — and he liked it. More than liked it. It stirred something in him I hadn’t seen before. A curiosity. An excitement. A sense of belonging, however imagined, to a realm bigger than merchants and guild dinners and the endless ladder of Florence society.

These were the first seeds of admiration that would later grow into something far more complicated.

And though I didn’t know it then, I’d soon learn that a man chasing genius rarely notices how far he’s wandered from the path he meant to follow.
