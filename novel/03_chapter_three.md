# CHAPTER 3 — The First Delays

## 1503 — Florence

The first delay didn't even feel like a delay. That's how these things sneak up on you—quietly, politely, the way a cat slips into a room you thought you'd closed and locked, and suddenly you turn around to find it curled on your best cushion looking perfectly innocent.

A week after my first sitting, a messenger arrived at our door with a note from Leonardo. Not a letter, really—more of a suggestion written down so it wouldn't be forgotten. It said only that he would need another session soon.

_Soon_ is one of those words that sounds reassuring until you've lived with artists long enough to know it means absolutely nothing.

I read it once, then again, then folded it carefully and set it on the table. "He wants another sitting," I told Francesco.

His head snapped up from his ledger. "Already?"

"Yes. Soon."

He smiled—the way men smile when they hear what they want to hear and stop listening immediately after. "That's excellent. It means he's making progress."

"It means he's not finished," I said.

"Nonsense. Great work takes time." He reached for the note as if it might contain more meaning if he held it himself, squinting at Leonardo's script. "Soon is good. Soon means it's happening."

I let that go. There was no point in correcting optimism in its early stages; it only hardened into stubbornness.

Two days passed. No further word arrived.

On the third morning, Francesco began adjusting his doublet at breakfast.

Casually.

He smoothed the fabric across his shoulders, tugged at the sleeves, frowned at the collar. Then he stood and walked past me toward the courtyard—only to return moments later and adjust it all over again. He drank his wine more slowly than usual, as if he had nowhere particular to be. He checked the gate. Twice.

"You're restless," I observed.

"I'm not restless," he said, too quickly. "I was simply thinking I might pass by the workshop today."

I looked at him over my cup. "To do what?"

"Nothing in particular." He waved a hand vaguely. "Just inquire. Casually. About the schedule for the next sitting."

"Casually," I repeated.

"Yes. There's no harm in showing interest. It reminds him we're engaged patrons."

"He has already been paid," I reminded him. "In full. Generously."

"And that," Francesco said with sudden firmness, "is precisely why it's appropriate to inquire. It shows we value the work."

He moved to the kitchen and selected a small basket of figs—our best ones, the ones I'd been saving for dinner with the Rucellai—and began wrapping them carefully in clean linen.

"You're bringing a gift," I said.

"It's polite."

"You're checking on a schedule."

"It's gracious."

"You're nervous."

He paused at the door, figs balanced in one hand, and turned to give me a wounded look. "I am being respectful of his process."

"Of course you are," I said. "Try not to look like you're begging."

"I am not—" He stopped himself, drew up his shoulders with dignity. "I am _inquiring_."

"Naturally."

I watched him walk away, posture carefully composed, his gift balanced like an offering to a temperamental saint. I could see his lips moving as he rehearsed words under his breath—words he would absolutely forget the moment Leonardo smiled at him.

He returned an hour later—cheeks flushed from walking quickly, hair wind-tossed in a way that made him look younger, a man vibrating somewhere between pride and confusion. I recognized that particular combination. It meant Leonardo had given him vague reassurances again.

"Well?" I asked, not looking up from the bread I was slicing for our midday meal.

"He's working," Francesco said, setting the empty basket on the table. So Leonardo had accepted the figs.

"Was he working on _my_ portrait?"

"Yes," he answered—far too quickly, the way one answers when the truth is more complicated than a simple affirmative. "That is, he said he's 'continuing to develop the essence of the composition.' Which I assume represents progress."

I set down the knife. "Did he show it to you?"

A pause. Just long enough to be telling.

"He said it was at a delicate stage," Francesco replied carefully. "That looking at it now would be like... like judging bread before it's finished rising. His exact words."

Which meant no. He hadn't shown Francesco anything. The irritation flared hot in my chest—we'd paid him a fortune, and he was comparing my portrait to unrisen bread?

"He did say we'll need a few more sessions. Just a few. To capture the light properly. He was very specific about the light, Lisa. He talked about it for quite some time."

"I'm sure he did," I said, hearing the edge in my own voice.

Francesco either didn't notice or chose to ignore it. "A few more sessions. That's all."

A few, in Leonardo's world, could mean anything from three days to three cycles of the moon to an entire age of mankind—and I was beginning to suspect which end of that spectrum we were dealing with.

---

The weeks that followed established a pattern I came to know far too well.

Francesco would find excuses to drop by the workshop—sometimes with "questions about the sitting schedule," sometimes simply because he was "in the area" despite the fact that Leonardo's workshop was nowhere near any place my husband had legitimate business being.

Each visit came back the same: Leonardo smiling warmly, gesturing grandly with those elegant hands, and insisting the portrait was progressing beautifully but needed "just a little more time to resolve certain technical challenges." The challenges were never specified. The timeline was never clarified. But Francesco would return home satisfied that progress was being made, somewhere, somehow, in the mysterious realm where genius operated.

I should have been annoyed.

Actually, I was annoyed. Increasingly so.

We had paid the man. In full. Upfront. Against my better judgment and every instinct I possessed as a merchant's wife. And what did we have to show for it? Vague promises and poetic excuses about light and essence and technical challenges.

Meanwhile, I had sat on that uncomfortable stool for hours, holding perfectly still while my back ached and my neck cramped and Leonardo muttered about birds and waterways. I had done my part. I had been the useful face.

And apparently, that was worth nothing more than "soon" and "just a few more sessions" and bread metaphors.

But Francesco—Francesco seemed content with this arrangement. More than content. He was enjoying it.

---

Then came the afternoon in late October that changed things entirely.

Francesco returned from one of his visits looking different. Not glowing with secondhand glory or frustrated by vague promises—something broader, deeper. Excitement mixed with wonder, with a touch of awe that made him look almost boyish.

He found me in the courtyard supervising while our youngest tried to catch a chicken that had escaped from the neighbor's coop. Francesco pulled me aside, away from the chaos, like he was about to reveal a scandal or a secret too precious to share with just anyone.

"Lisa," he whispered, his eyes bright, "you will not believe the things that man is working on."

"I can imagine," I said flatly, thinking of all the things that apparently mattered more than finishing what he'd been paid to do.

"No," he insisted, gripping my arm with unusual intensity. "You truly can't."

He pulled me over to sit on the courtyard bench, needing me to understand, needing to share what he'd witnessed. And despite my irritation—despite the unpainted portrait and the wasted hours and the empty promises—I found myself listening.

Because Francesco's face was lit with something I hadn't seen in years. Maybe ever. And watching him—so animated, so boyish, gesturing with his hands like a child describing something wonderful he didn't fully understand—I felt something twist in my chest. Part of me wanted to smile at his enthusiasm. Part of me wanted to remind him sharply that we'd paid for a portrait, not whatever distractions Leonardo was offering.

I did neither. I just listened.

"I walked in with a plan, Lisa. A rehearsed speech. Very polite, very firm. 'Maestro, perhaps we might discuss when my wife's portrait will be ready.' I practiced it the entire walk there."

"And?" I prompted, though I could already guess how this had gone.

"I got as far as 'Maestro' before he interrupted." Francesco laughed—not his usual careful merchant's laugh, but something younger, freer. "He was at his worktable, charcoal already smudged across three fingers, barely glanced up. 'Ah! Signor Francesco,' he said, waving me in like I was expected. 'Good timing. I was just thinking about wings.'"

I settled back against the bench. This was going to take a while.

"I tried again," Francesco continued, standing now because apparently sitting was impossible when one had witnessed genius at work. "'About the portrait—' But he was already moving. You know how he moves, Lisa? Like water flowing around stones, like he's not walking so much as being pulled by invisible threads toward whatever thought just caught his attention."

Francesco began to pace, his hands starting to move the way they always did when he was excited, sketching shapes in the air that only he could see.

"He swept me over to this table—absolutely buried in papers, Lisa, weighted down with the strangest collection of objects. A stone. A cog. A length of twine. A chicken bone." He paused. "Actually, I'm not certain about the chicken bone. Might have been from a crow. Regardless—he shoved half the papers aside and there it was."

"There what was?"

"A machine." Francesco's eyes went distant, seeing it again. "Or a creature pretending to be one. I couldn't tell which. Two enormous wings, curved out like this—" He spread his arms wide, fingers reaching. "Ribbed like a bat's wing, stretched over some kind of frame. And underneath, this narrow body, almost skeletal, with footrests and levers and something like a tail."

"A tail."

"Yes! And I thought—well, naturally I thought it was a boat. Some kind of ornamental boat, perhaps for a pageant. So I said, very reasonably, 'Is that a boat?'"

Francesco grinned at the memory, and I realized I was smiling too, caught up in his telling despite myself. Despite my frustration. Despite everything.

"And he laughed, Lisa. Not at me, exactly, but with such delight—like a child who's just realized his father doesn't understand the marvelous secret he's discovered. 'No, no,' he said. 'Look closer.'"

Francesco moved to our own table now, pushing aside my sewing basket to make space, miming Leonardo's movements.

"He grabbed another sheet and started sketching right over it. Lines flying so fast I couldn't track them. And he's talking the whole time—'A bird does not flap. Not really. That's what everyone thinks. But flapping is crude. Inefficient. What matters is lift.'"

"Lift," I repeated.

"Exactly! That's what I said. Just: 'Lift.' Completely blank. And he turned to me—so pleased that I was confused, mind you—and said, 'Yes! You push against the air, and the air pushes back. Hard enough, and it becomes almost solid. The wing is not beating the air—it is arguing with it.'"

"Arguing with it."

"Forcefully," Francesco added.

I laughed—I couldn't help it. Not because flying machines were funny, though they were absurd. But because Francesco looked so alive, so caught up in the telling, waving his arms and mimicking Leonardo's gestures. Like a boy who'd just discovered the world was bigger than he'd imagined.

And beneath the laugh, something tighter. Because while my husband was discovering the wonders of arguing with air, my portrait sat unfinished in a corner somewhere, gathering dust.

"So I tried one more time," he continued. "'About the portrait—' And he waved his hand without even looking. Just—" Francesco demonstrated, a dismissive flutter of fingers. "'Later. This is more important.'"

He paused there, and something shifted in his expression. Confusion, almost. Wonder at himself.

"And here's the thing, Lisa. I should have been annoyed. I should have insisted. I should have reminded him that I'd paid good money for a portrait, not an education in things that will never work." He shook his head slowly. "But I didn't. I can't even explain why. That man has something. When he talks, when he shows you what he's thinking... you just want to understand it. To be part of it."

He looked at me, almost helpless. "So instead I just... watched."

Francesco sat back down, slower now, his voice quieter.

"He leaned over that table like he was showing me the secrets of the universe. Started explaining about ratios and weight distribution and how birds are strong because they are light. And then—I don't know what possessed me—I said something about balance. That a merchant knows weight isn't just about mass, it's about distribution. How you arrange it matters as much as how much there is."

Francesco looked at me then, and I saw something vulnerable in his face.

"He stopped, Lisa. Just froze mid-gesture and turned to look at me. Really look at me. Not as a patron. Not as an interruption. And he said, very softly, 'Yes. Exactly.' Then he asked..." Francesco's voice caught slightly. "He asked how I would do it."

I waited, understanding now why he'd come home looking the way he did.

"I started thinking aloud—about the wings, how they'd need to be flexible but resilient. And I told him about silk. Not common silk, but treated silk, layered, the kind we use for sails when weight matters. Over a frame of walnut, maybe. Strong grain. Flexible."

Francesco's hands were moving again, but differently now. More carefully, like he was handling something precious.

"And he started sketching again, Lisa. Furiously. Muttering to himself—'Yes. Yes. Silk skin. A merchant's wing. Do you see?' And the extraordinary thing was..." He looked at me, almost apologetic. "I did see it. Not entirely. Not precisely. But I saw the possibility."

"And did you tell him your nature is not suited for flying?" I asked, keeping my voice light.

He blinked at me, caught off guard. Then smiled, missing—or choosing to miss—the edge beneath my words. "Ha. Very funny."

Francesco picked up his wine cup but didn't drink, just turned it slowly in his hands.

"I forgot about the portrait completely," he said finally. "Stood there for nearly an hour, pointing at things, making suggestions, listening while he explained and corrected and contradicted himself and laughed when I admitted I didn't understand half of it. And when I finally said I was confused, he just smiled and said, 'That's all right. Understanding comes later.'"

He set down the cup without drinking.

"He showed me other things too. A machine that moves water uphill using a screw rotating in a cylinder. Sketches of siege weapons and the human heart. Asked my opinion on logistics, on trade routes, on the cost of materials." He laughed softly. "He listened, Lisa. Really listened. Didn't treat me like just another patron with a commission."

"That must have been..." I searched for the right word, trying to push down the resentment that wanted to surface. "...gratifying."

"It was extraordinary," he said simply.

There was a pause. I watched a cloud cross the sun, shadow sliding across the courtyard stones.

"And the portrait?" I asked, keeping my voice carefully neutral.

"Oh." Francesco blinked, as though returning from a great distance. "Yes. Still in progress. He mentioned something about the hands being particularly challenging. Said he'll need you for another sitting soon."

But he said it differently now. Not with frustration or even impatience. He said it almost fondly, the way one speaks of a friend's endearing flaw.

The delay had become acceptable, perhaps even welcome, because it meant more visits. More conversations. More glimpses into that extraordinary mind.

And I realized, sitting there watching my husband glow with reflected glory, that I had lost him to this.

Not to another woman. Not to drink or gambling or any of the usual ways men wandered from their marriages.

To Leonardo da Vinci. To genius. To the intoxicating feeling of being valued by someone brilliant.

The portrait—my portrait, the thing we'd paid for, the reason this had all started—had become an afterthought. A convenient excuse for Francesco to keep visiting, to keep bringing gifts, to keep standing in that cluttered workshop hoping to be asked his opinion on things that had nothing to do with silk or commerce or any of the practical knowledge that had built our life.

I should have said something. Should have reminded him that we'd paid for a service that wasn't being rendered. That Leonardo's brilliance didn't excuse his failure to deliver what he'd promised. That Francesco was being used—kindly, perhaps, but used nonetheless—as an audience for a man who loved having admirers.

But I looked at his face, lit with wonder and belonging, and I couldn't do it.

So I said nothing.

And the weeks continued. October bled into November. Francesco's visits became more frequent. The gifts started then—small items Leonardo "might find useful": scraps of silk, lengths of cord, pieces of wood, vials of expensive dye. Each one a reason to return, to see what Leonardo was working on, to be part of that world.

The portrait remained unfinished, barely begun, little more than a ghost of composition on canvas.

And I sat at home, managing the household and the children and the accounts, growing more frustrated with each passing week that my husband seemed to have forgotten why any of this had started in the first place.
