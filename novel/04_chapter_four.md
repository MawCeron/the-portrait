**CHAPTER 4 – Florence: The Republic’s Painter**
_1503–1504 – Florence_

In those early months, before the Maestro vanished to France or Milan or whichever corner of the world required his genius that week, Francesco made a habit of storming toward Leonardo’s workshop and floating back.

He always _left_ the house annoyed — determined, jaw tight, muttering things like, “This can’t go on,” and “A contract is a contract,” and “Just a few more sessions, he says!”

And every single time, he _returned_ transformed — breathless, dazzled, the annoyance washed clean off him like mud after a rainstorm.

One afternoon sticks with me. He’d left in a fury so focused he forgot his hat. When he came back, he was carrying not the hat, not an update on the portrait, not even a scrap of a painting cloth — but stories. Stories spilling out of him like someone had kicked over a bucket.

“Lisa,” he said, grabbing my hands with both of his, “he was consulting with Niccolò Machiavelli.”

I blinked. “The bureaucrat? The one who scowls at everything?”

“He wasn’t scowling today,” Francesco insisted. “He was listening.”

It turned out Leonardo had been summoned to discuss an engineering challenge for the Republic. Something involving water, fortifications, or possibly both — Francesco spoke so quickly the details tangled together like loose threads.

“The Maestro had sketches everywhere,” he continued. “Not just paintings — machines, gears, pulleys, cross-sections of fortresses. They were debating—debating!—how to divert a river.”

“And how did that help the portrait?” I asked, because someone in this marriage had to.

Francesco brushed this aside with the impatience of a man who’d glimpsed heaven and was being asked about laundry. “This isn’t about the portrait, Lisa. This is about Florence.”

So the portrait was already fading, becoming an excuse instead of the purpose. I saw it happening before he did.

The next visit was the same, though somehow bigger. Francesco left grumbling about delays and returned glowing as if Leonardo had handed him a personal invitation to Olympus.

“He’s painting the Battle of Anghiari, Lisa.”

“I know,” I said. “All of Florence knows.”

“No, no — you don’t _understand_. I saw it. Horses exploding off the wall! Soldiers tangled like serpents! He’s trying to paint motion itself.”

“Is it finished?”

He paused. “Well… no. But the idea is magnificent.”

Ideas were always finished with Leonardo. The rest—well, reality took longer.

Week after week, my husband returned from those visits full of things that had nothing to do with my portrait. Leonardo planning war machines. Leonardo advising the Signoria. Leonardo sketching inventions that made as much sense as dreams. Leonardo muttering to himself about anatomy. Leonardo unraveling geometry the way one unspools a ribbon.

It was endless. Mesmerizing, apparently.

And slowly, I watched the shift in my husband.

At first, he spoke of Leonardo as a painter — _the_ painter he had hired, the painter who was behind schedule, the painter who needed to focus.

But as the months went on…

“He explained the angle of water pressure to me…”

“He asked my opinion about metals…”

“He said merchants like me understand materials better than soldiers…”

“He trusts my judgment.”

Trust. Judgment. _My husband had become convinced Leonardo da Vinci valued his thoughts._

Let me be fair: Leonardo probably _did_ value them, in the same vague, scattered, generous way he valued most things handed to him with enthusiasm. But that wasn’t the point.

The point was what it did to Francesco.

He started talking about Leonardo in a different tone — reverent, wide-eyed, almost youthful. He’d come home empty-handed but full of stories.

And I realized something quietly startling:

I had married a practical man who dealt in wool and silk.
I was now living with a boy who believed he had discovered that gods walked among us.

It was both sweet and slightly alarming.

This was the transformation — the moment when admiration slipped its leash and wandered into devotion. And once that happens, no portrait, finished or otherwise, can save a man from himself.
