# CHAPTER 4 – Florence: The Republic's Painter

## 1503–1504 – Florence

In those early months, before the Maestro vanished to Milan or Rome or whichever distant corner of the world required his genius that particular week, Francesco made a habit of storming toward Leonardo's workshop and floating back.

The pattern was so consistent I could have set a clock by it.

He always _left_ the house annoyed—determined, jaw tight, shoulders set with purpose, muttering things like, "This can't go on," and "A contract is a contract," and "Just a few more sessions, he says! Ha! We'll see about that." He'd stride out our door like a general marching to demand terms of surrender, ready to insist on progress, on timelines, on the basic professional courtesy of actually working on the commission he'd paid for.

And every single time—_every single time_—he returned transformed. Breathless, dazzed, eyes bright with reflected wonder, the annoyance washed clean off him like mud after a rainstorm. The general had somehow become a pilgrim.

One afternoon in particular sticks with me, clear as yesterday despite all the years that have passed.

He'd left in such focused fury that he forgot his hat—forgot it entirely, despite the October sun being fierce enough to punish that kind of carelessness. I'd called after him but he was already around the corner, a man on a mission, deaf to all practical concerns.

When he came back two hours later, face flushed from walking hatless in the heat, he was carrying not the forgotten hat (still missing), not an update on the portrait (still unfinished), not even a scrap of painting cloth to prove Leonardo remembered I existed—but stories. Stories spilling out of him like someone had kicked over a bucket and couldn't stop the flood.

"Lisa," he said, grabbing both my hands with both of his before I'd even fully turned from the account books I'd been reviewing. "Lisa, you won't believe—he was consulting with Niccolò Machiavelli."

I blinked, setting down my pen carefully before the ink could smudge. "The bureaucrat? The one from the Second Chancery who scowls at everything like the world owes him an apology?"

"He wasn't scowling today," Francesco insisted, his grip on my hands almost tight enough to hurt. "He was _listening_. Really listening. Taking notes. They were—Lisa, they were _collaborating_."

He pulled me over to sit, needing me anchored for the story he was about to tell.

It turned out Leonardo had been summoned—officially summoned, by the Signoria itself—to consult on an engineering challenge for the Republic. Something involving water systems, fortifications, or possibly both. Francesco spoke so quickly the details tangled together like loose threads from different spools, but the essence was clear: the government of Florence had called upon Leonardo not as an artist but as an engineer, a problem-solver, a man whose understanding of mechanics and mathematics made him valuable beyond his ability to paint beautiful things.

"The Maestro had sketches everywhere," Francesco continued, his hands finally releasing mine so they could help him paint the scene in the air between us. "Not paintings—I mean, yes, there were paintings, but they weren't what mattered. He had designs covering every surface. Machines, gears, pulleys, cross-sections of fortress walls showing where they were weak and how to strengthen them. Mathematical calculations I couldn't begin to follow. And he and Machiavelli were debating—actually _debating_, as equals—about how to divert a river to cut off an enemy's water supply."

"Divert a river," I repeated, trying to picture it. "That sounds... ambitious."

"It's brilliant! The Arno, Lisa. They want to redirect the Arno away from Pisa. Can you imagine? No water for the city, and we take it without a single soldier dying in siege. Leonardo had calculations for the amount of earth that would need to be moved, the slope of the new channel, how many men it would take, how long it would take..." He shook his head in wonder. "And Machiavelli was taking him seriously. Not humoring him—actually debating the logistics, the political implications, whether the money would be better spent there or on mercenaries."

"That does sound remarkable," I admitted. Because it was, truly. To be consulted by the Republic on matters of war and defense—that was recognition of the highest order.

"And then," Francesco added, his voice dropping slightly as if confiding a secret, "Leonardo turned to me—to _me_, Lisa—and asked what I thought about labor costs for that kind of project. What would workers from the countryside expect to be paid? How would you provision them? What would the merchants charge to supply food for that many men over months of work?"

"And you told him?"

"I estimated as best I could," he said, and I could hear the pride in his voice. "And he wrote it down. In his notebook. My thoughts on logistics and labor, right there alongside his mathematical proofs and engineering diagrams."

I looked at my husband's face—glowing with something I'd rarely seen there. Not the satisfaction of a successful business deal. Not the nervous pride of being acknowledged by someone important at a social function. Something purer than either of those things. The simple, uncomplicated joy of being asked for his opinion and having it valued.

"And how did all this help with the portrait?" I asked, because someone in this marriage had to maintain focus on the actual reason my husband kept visiting Leonardo's workshop in the first place.

Francesco brushed this aside with the casual impatience of a man who'd glimpsed something extraordinary and was being asked about mundane practicalities. "This isn't about the portrait anymore, Lisa. This is about _Florence_. About solving problems no one else can solve. About genius being applied to real challenges."

So there it was. The portrait was already fading from primary purpose to convenient excuse. I saw it happening before he did—watched it transform in real time from the thing he wanted to merely the reason he could give himself for continuing to visit.

The next visit was the same, though somehow even bigger in Francesco's telling. He left grumbling about delays and broken promises and painters who couldn't focus on commissioned work, and returned glowing as if Leonardo had handed him a personal invitation to witness the divine at work.

"He's painting the Battle of Anghiari, Lisa," he announced, still breathless from his rapid walk home.

"I know," I said, not looking up from the mending in my lap. "All of Florence knows. It's supposed to be the greatest fresco ever attempted. The Signoria commissioned it months ago."

"No, no—you don't _understand_." He crouched beside my chair, needing my full attention. "I _saw_ it. Not finished, obviously, but the drawings, the preparatory sketches. Lisa, horses exploding off the wall like they're about to trample you where you stand! Soldiers tangled together like serpents fighting, all muscle and fury and desperation! He's not just painting a battle—he's trying to paint _motion itself_. The moment before impact, the split second where everything is moving and nothing is resolved."

"That sounds extraordinary," I said, and meant it.

"It is! It absolutely is!" He stood, pacing now, unable to contain his excitement while stationary. "He showed me how he's studying horses, how they move, the mechanics of their bodies. Did you know a horse's leg has more in common with a human arm than it seems? The bones correspond, he said. The same structures, just adapted for different purposes. He has sketches of horse anatomy that would make you weep, Lisa. The beauty of understanding something so completely..."

"Is it finished?" I asked, already knowing the answer but hoping I might be surprised.

He paused, mid-stride. "Well... no. Not yet. But the _idea_ is magnificent. Once he resolves some technical problems with the plaster—something about it not adhering properly, I didn't entirely follow that part—it'll be the greatest thing Florence has ever seen."

Ideas were always finished with Leonardo. The rest—well, reality took longer. Sometimes much, much longer.

Week after week, my husband returned from those visits full of everything except news about my portrait.

Leonardo planning war machines that looked like giant crossbows on wheels.

Leonardo advising the Signoria on military engineering, on fortifications, on siege tactics.

Leonardo sketching inventions that made as much sense as fever dreams—flying machines, diving bells, tanks that moved on their own power.

Leonardo muttering to himself about anatomy while dissecting cadavers at the hospital, trying to understand how muscles worked, how blood flowed, what the heart actually did.

Leonardo unraveling problems of perspective and geometry the way one unspools a ribbon, making the complex simple and the simple profound.

It was endless. Mesmerizing, apparently. At least to Francesco.

And slowly—so slowly I almost didn't notice it happening until it had already happened—I watched the fundamental shift in my husband.

At first, in those early weeks, he spoke of Leonardo as a painter. _The_ painter he had hired. The painter who was behind schedule. The painter who needed to focus on the task at hand instead of getting distracted by every shiny idea that floated past his extraordinary mind.

But as the months went on, as autumn turned to winter and winter began its slow surrender to spring, something changed in Francesco's voice when he spoke that name.

"He explained the mathematics of water pressure to me today. How the deeper you go, the more force pushes back. It's why wells can only be so deep before—"

"He asked my opinion about different types of metal for gears. Whether bronze or iron would hold up better under constant stress. I told him about how different alloys behave in our machinery at the shop, and he said that was exactly the kind of practical knowledge he needed—"

"He says merchants like me understand materials better than soldiers do. Soldiers just want things that work right now. Merchants think about whether something will last, whether it's worth the investment, whether there's a better way to do it cheaper without sacrificing quality—"

"He trusts my judgment, Lisa. He actually trusts my judgment on these things."

Trust. Judgment. Value.

_My husband had become convinced that Leonardo da Vinci—the greatest artist and possibly the greatest mind in all of Italy—genuinely valued his thoughts._

Let me be fair, because I'm trying to tell this honestly: Leonardo probably _did_ value Francesco's input, in the same vague, scattered, generous way he valued most things offered to him with genuine enthusiasm. The man was curious about everything, interested in everyone who showed real knowledge about anything. He probably enjoyed talking to Francesco about trade and materials and practical matters the same way he enjoyed talking to mathematicians about geometry or musicians about harmonics or physicians about anatomy.

But that wasn't really the point.

The point was what it did to Francesco.

He started talking about Leonardo in a different tone—reverent, wide-eyed, with a kind of youthful enthusiasm I hadn't seen in him since... well, possibly ever. Not when we were courting. Not when our first child was born. Not when a particularly advantageous contract came through. This was something else entirely.

He'd come home empty-handed—no progress on the portrait, no new sitting scheduled, no concrete evidence that Leonardo even remembered he'd been commissioned to paint my face—but full of stories.

Stories about genius at work. Stories about problems being solved in ways no one had thought of before. Stories about being included, however peripherally, in something greater than buying and selling silk.

And I realized something that was both touching and slightly alarming:

I had married a practical man who dealt in wool and silk and account books and contracts.

I was now living with a boy who believed, with absolute sincerity, that he had discovered gods walked among us—and that one of them occasionally asked his opinion about lumber quality.

It was sweet, in its way. To see my careful, calculating husband transformed by admiration into something more open, more curious, more alive to possibility.

But it was also concerning.

Because I recognized the look in his eyes when he talked about Leonardo now. It wasn't the look of a patron discussing an artist he'd hired. It wasn't even the look of a student discussing a teacher he respected.

It was devotion.

This was the transformation—the moment when simple admiration slipped its leash and wandered into something deeper, more consuming. Once that happens, no portrait, finished or otherwise, can save a man from himself. The thing he was chasing had stopped being the painting and become something much harder to pin down, much harder to satisfy.

Recognition, yes. Belonging, perhaps. But more than that—proximity to genius. The chance to stand close enough to brilliance that some of its light might reflect onto him, might transform him from a merchant into something more, something that mattered in ways beyond the ledger book.

And Leonardo, brilliant and distracted and genuinely kind, probably had no idea what was happening. He was just being himself—curious, welcoming, happy to discuss anything with anyone who showed genuine interest. He couldn't have known he was becoming the center of someone else's orbit.

Or perhaps he did know, and simply didn't think it mattered. After all, what was one more admirer to a man whose genius had made him famous across Italy?

But it mattered to Francesco. It mattered enormously.

And it would matter even more in the years to come, though none of us knew that yet.
