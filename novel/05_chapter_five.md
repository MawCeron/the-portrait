**CHAPTER 5 – The Departure**
_1504 – Florence_

If you’ve ever watched a man discover betrayal in slow motion, you’ll understand what my household felt like in the spring of 1504.

It began with rumors — always how these things begin with Leonardo. Whispers drifting through the market: _He’s leaving. He’s packing. He’s been summoned. He’s off to Milan again._

I didn’t believe it at first. My husband certainly didn’t. He called rumors “the disease of idle tongues.” And besides, Leonardo had a portrait to finish. _My_ portrait. Surely that bound him to Florence for at least another… decade.

But rumors have a way of ripening into facts, and one morning they arrived fully grown.

Francesco burst through the door, face flushed, hair disheveled, breathing as though he’d run the entire city.

“He’s gone,” he gasped.

“Gone where?”

“Milan.”

“Well,” I said carefully, “that’s unfortunate.”

“He took it, Lisa.” His voice cracked in a way I had never heard from him — thin, confused, wounded. “The portrait. He took it with him.”

I sat back, stunned. Not _shocked_—this was Leonardo, after all—but stunned at how deeply it hit my husband.

“But why would he take it?” I asked.

He shook his head, then paced in a small, tight circle. “He said—before he vanished—that he needed better pigments. Better varnish. Better light. Better something! Every time I visited, it was always something.”

“Maybe he intends to finish it in Milan,” I suggested gently. “And send it to us when it’s done.”

Francesco stopped pacing. His eyes flickered with hope, then suspicion, then that weary, heartbreaking disbelief that settles in when a promise has stretched too thin.

“Perhaps,” he said.

But that “perhaps” didn’t land on the table between us — no, it floated somewhere above his head, twisting into an idea far more dangerous than hope.

I knew that look. I’d seen it when he bought his first bolt of expensive silk. When he argued his way into a merchant council. When he decided we needed a bigger house despite having no more children to fill it.

It was the look of a man already making plans.

“Francesco,” I said slowly, “we have a business here. A life. You can’t just… chase a painter to another city.”

He blinked at me, as if I’d spoken in Greek.

“Chase? Lisa, please. I’m not chasing him. I simply need to… understand what happened. Clarify the arrangement. That’s all.”

“By going to Milan.”

“It’s not so far.”

“It is if you intend to come back with your dignity.”

He ignored that. My husband had a remarkable ability to edit reality the way other men edited letters — trimming the inconvenient parts and pretending they were never there.

For the next week, he walked around the house like a recently abandoned dog — not pitiful, but confused as to why the door had shut. Everything reminded him of Leonardo.

He’d see a spool of thread and mutter, “He once asked me about tensile strength…”

He’d look at our courtyard fountain and sigh, “He was redesigning them for the Republic…”

He’d stare at the Arno and whisper, “He wanted to move rivers…”

And at night, he lay awake, breathing out long, defeated exhalations that made me want to smother him with a pillow just to put him out of his misery.

Finally, one morning, he said it outright:

“I need to go to Milan.”

And I—his loyal, practical, long-suffering wife—did what all good Renaissance wives did.

I didn’t forbid him.
I didn’t encourage him.
I simply asked the one question that mattered:

“When?”

He didn’t answer with words. The look on his face told me everything.

He had already chosen the day.

And so the chase began — long before my husband understood he wasn’t hunting a portrait, but the man who had painted only half of it.
