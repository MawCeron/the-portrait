# CHAPTER 5 – The Departure

## 1504 – Florence

If you've ever watched a man discover betrayal in slow motion—each piece of evidence arriving like drops of water that eventually become a flood—you'll understand what my household felt like in the spring of 1504.

It began, as these things always do with Leonardo, with rumors.

Whispers drifting through the market while I bought vegetables. Murmurs in the shop when merchants came to examine our silks. Gossip traded like currency at the well where our servants filled buckets: _He's leaving. He's packing. He's been summoned. He's off to Milan again. The French need him there. Or was it the Duke? Someone important, anyway. Someone who could pay better than Florence._

I didn't believe it at first. Leonardo had been in Florence for years now, on and off. He had commissions here—the Battle of Anghiari for the Signoria, various private portraits, engineering projects for the Republic. Surely he wouldn't just abandon everything.

My husband certainly didn't believe it. He called rumors "the disease of idle tongues that have nothing better to wag about." And besides, Leonardo had a portrait to finish. _My_ portrait. My husband's commission, paid for in full, in good Florentine gold. Surely that bound him to Florence for at least another... well, however long it took. Which, granted, might be years, but still. The commitment existed.

But rumors have a way of ripening into facts when you're not paying attention, and one morning in late March they arrived on our doorstep fully grown and impossible to ignore.

Francesco burst through the door—not walked, not entered, but _burst_—face flushed red from exertion, hair disheveled in a way that would normally horrify him, breathing as though he'd run across the entire city without stopping.

"He's gone," he gasped, one hand braced against the doorframe.

I looked up from the household accounts I'd been reviewing. "Who's gone? Francesco, sit down before you collapse—"

"Leonardo." He said the name like it hurt. "He's gone."

"Gone where?"

"Milan." He pushed himself away from the doorframe and staggered further into the room. "He left for Milan. Yesterday. Maybe the day before. No one seems certain."

I set down my pen carefully, buying time to think. "Well," I said, choosing my words with the care one uses when approaching a wounded animal, "that's... unfortunate."

"Unfortunate." He laughed—high, sharp, nothing like his normal laugh. "Yes. Unfortunate is certainly one word for it."

"Francesco—"

"He took it, Lisa." His voice cracked in a way I had never heard from him before—thin, confused, wounded in a place deeper than mere disappointment. Like a child who'd been promised something precious and woken to find it vanished in the night. "The portrait. He took it with him."

Now I did stand, the chair scraping against the floor. "He took the portrait? To Milan?"

"Everything." Francesco was pacing now—not the purposeful stride I was used to, but a small, tight circle like a dog on a chain. "His workshop is empty. Cleared out. The apprentices are gone. That damned horse skeleton, gone. All the sketches, the machines, the notebooks—everything packed up and hauled north while we slept like fools."

I sat back down, carefully, my mind working through what this meant. Not shocked—this was Leonardo, after all, Leonardo who left things unfinished, Leonardo who got bored or distracted or summoned elsewhere—but stunned at how deeply it had struck my husband.

"But why would he take an unfinished portrait?" I asked, trying to understand the logic. "If he meant to abandon it, wouldn't he just leave it behind?"

Francesco stopped his pacing to stare at me like I'd missed something obvious. "That's exactly what I said! To Tommaso—you know Tommaso, the carpenter who worked in the building?—I asked him that exact question. And he said Leonardo mentioned something about needing better pigments in Milan. Better varnish. Better _light_, for God's sake. Better _something_."

"So perhaps he intends to finish it there," I suggested gently, trying to find the reasonable explanation that would calm my husband down. "And send it to us when it's done. That would make sense, wouldn't it? If Milan has better materials—"

"That's what I thought!" Francesco seized on this like a drowning man grabbing rope. "That was my first thought exactly. Of course. He's gone to finish it properly, and then he'll send it back. Or have it delivered. Or..."

He trailed off, and I watched hope flicker across his face, then suspicion, then something worse—that weary, heartbreaking disbelief that settles into a man's bones when a promise he'd believed in absolutely has stretched too thin and finally snapped.

"Perhaps," he said finally, quietly.

But that "perhaps" didn't land solidly on the table between us—no, it floated somewhere above his head, light and insubstantial, already twisting itself into something far more dangerous than simple hope. Already transforming into determination, into purpose, into a plan half-formed but growing stronger by the moment.

I knew that look. I'd been married to this man for years. I'd seen it when he bought his first bolt of truly expensive silk, gambling that he could sell it for enough profit to expand the business. When he argued his way onto a merchant council despite being younger than anyone else there. When he decided we needed a bigger house despite having no more children to fill it, simply because the address would impress the right people.

It was the look of a man already making plans.

"Francesco," I said slowly, carefully, the way one might approach a horse that's thinking about bolting, "we have a business here. A life. Children who need their father. You can't just... chase a painter to another city."

He blinked at me as if I'd spoken in Greek, or perhaps as if I'd suggested something so fundamentally contrary to the natural order that he couldn't quite process the words.

"Chase?" He shook his head, almost laughing. "Lisa, please. I'm not _chasing_ him. That's absurd. I simply need to... understand what happened. Clarify the arrangement. Make sure he knows we're still expecting the work to be completed. That's all. Basic communication between patron and artist. Perfectly reasonable."

"By going to Milan."

"It's not so far." Said as if distance were measured only in miles, not in time away from home, not in money spent on travel, not in the growing obsession I could see taking root behind his eyes.

"It is," I said quietly, "if you intend to come back with your dignity intact."

He ignored that. My husband had a remarkable ability to edit reality the way other men edited letters—trimming away the inconvenient parts, adjusting the phrasing, pretending the original version had never existed at all.

For the next week, he walked around our house like a recently abandoned dog—not pitiful exactly, but confused as to why the door had shut in his face when he'd been so certain it would stay open forever. Everything reminded him of Leonardo. Every small detail of daily life became another thread connecting back to the man who'd left.

He'd see a spool of thread in the shop and mutter, "He once asked me about tensile strength... whether silk was stronger than linen of the same thickness..."

He'd look at our courtyard fountain—the one we'd had installed at considerable expense two years ago—and sigh, "He was redesigning the fountain system for the Republic... said the current ones wasted water through poor engineering..."

He'd stand at the window staring at the Arno flowing past and whisper, "He wanted to move rivers... actually believed it was possible to redirect an entire river with enough planning..."

And at night, he lay beside me in the dark, breathing out long, defeated exhalations that made me want to either comfort him or smother him with a pillow just to put us both out of this misery. I did neither. I just listened to him not sleeping, felt him staring at the ceiling in the darkness, knew he was thinking about Milan.

Our children noticed. Young Piero asked me why Father seemed sad. Camilla wanted to know if someone had died. I told them their father was simply tired from work, which was both true and entirely insufficient as an explanation.

Finally, one morning at breakfast—a perfectly ordinary morning, sun streaming through the windows, bread and cheese and olives on the table—he said it outright:

"I need to go to Milan."

Not a question. Not a proposal for discussion. A statement of fact, as if he were announcing he needed to visit our warehouse or meet with a supplier.

And I—his loyal, practical, long-suffering wife who had watched this obsession grow from a seed into something that now dominated our household like an unwelcome vine—did what all good Renaissance wives learned to do when their husbands made terrible decisions disguised as reasonable ones.

I didn't forbid him. That would only make him more determined, and besides, I had no real authority to forbid. I didn't encourage him. That would make me complicit in what I knew was foolishness.

I simply asked the one question that mattered:

"When?"

He didn't answer with words. The look on his face—relief mixed with something like guilt, gratitude that I wasn't fighting him but also shame that he was doing this at all—told me everything I needed to know.

He had already chosen the day. Probably had it circled in his mind for the past week, just waiting for the right moment to tell me.

"Next week," he admitted. "I've already arranged for Marco to handle the shop while I'm gone. Two weeks, maybe three at most. Just to speak with Leonardo, clarify expectations, perhaps see the portrait's progress. Then I'll be home."

Two weeks. Three at most.

We both knew it was a lie, though I don't think Francesco understood that yet. I could see the future more clearly than he could, perhaps because I wasn't blinded by devotion. This wouldn't be two weeks. This wouldn't be the end of it.

This was just the beginning.

"Very well," I said, reaching for the bread. "Will you need anything prepared for the journey?"

He looked at me with such gratitude I almost felt guilty for not stopping him. Almost.

"Thank you, Lisa," he said quietly. "For understanding."

But I didn't understand. Not really. I couldn't comprehend what drove a man to chase after someone who'd taken his money and his trust and disappeared north without so much as a farewell. Couldn't understand the pull that genius exerted on my practical husband, turning him into someone I barely recognized.

What I _did_ understand was this: my husband wasn't hunting a portrait anymore. That had stopped being the point months ago, maybe longer. He was hunting something much more elusive, much more dangerous.

He was hunting the man who'd shown him there was more to life than silk and contracts and climbing social ladders. The man who'd treated him like an equal, like someone whose thoughts mattered, like a friend rather than a patron.

And so the chase began—long before my husband understood what he was really chasing, long before he could admit even to himself that the portrait had become nothing more than a convenient excuse.

He thought he was going to Milan to check on his commissioned work.

I knew he was going to Milan because Leonardo had left a hole in his life, and Francesco couldn't bear the empty space.
