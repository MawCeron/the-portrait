## **Chapter 6: Milan — The First Journey**

Francesco left Florence with the determination of a man chasing a runaway cart. He wasn’t angry anymore; he was _injured_. There’s a difference. Anger cools. Injured pride festers. And when a man like my husband thinks his honor is at stake, he’ll cross the Apennines just to get a sentence of reassurance.

The trip cost him more than he’d admit—lost business, new expenses, and the humiliation of explaining to every fellow merchant on the road why he was traveling so far.

“My painter left with my portrait,” he said awkwardly the first few times.

By the fifth retelling he upgraded it to, “I have urgent matters with an engineer in the service of the Republic.”

He arrived in Milan and discovered, to his horror and secret delight, that Leonardo wasn’t hiding at all. He was simply _busy_. Very busy. Adored. Indispensable.

When Francesco finally found him—after chasing rumors through workshops, palaces, and kitchens—Leonardo greeted him as though they’d parted the day before.

“Oh, Signor del Giocondo! You’re here. Wonderful. You must see what we’re building for the governor.”

That “we” did something to Francesco. It pulled him forward like a hooked fish.

Leonardo swept him through rooms full of musicians rehearsing for court entertainments, engineers folding paper into wings, young assistants grinding pigments, French officials arguing with Italians, and a French governor—Charles d’Amboise—who actually paused to greet Francesco because Leonardo introduced him as “a most honorable Florentine patron.”

Francesco told me that part three times. He even changed his voice to imitate the governor’s accent.

And, of course, the portrait—_my_ portrait—was somewhere in that chaos, but never quite in reach.

“Almost finished,” Leonardo promised warmly. “You’ll see her soon enough.”

Francesco didn’t see even a corner of the panel.

But he did accompany Leonardo to a demonstration of some flying contraption. And to a discussion about fortress defenses. And to an evening gathering where musicians played pieces Leonardo had improved while sketching a new water pump.

When Francesco finally returned home, he carried no painting, no guarantees, and no apology.

He brought back something worse: **admiration**.

He came in exhausted, covered in road dust, and grinning like he’d tasted a new life.

“He introduced me to the governor,” he said before I even welcomed him back. “He asked for my opinion on the strength of a trade canal. Me! As though—well—Lisa, he really listens to people.”

I teased him, of course. “So. The portrait is finished?”

That wiped the grin off his face for a moment. Then he shrugged.

“Not yet. But, Lisa… you should see the things he’s doing.”

He talked for hours. Longer than he had on the day our first son was born.

And I’ll admit it: he looked younger. Happier. As if being ignored by a genius was the most thrilling experience he’d ever had.

The portrait remained in Milan.

My husband’s heart, however, stayed everywhere Leonardo had stood.
