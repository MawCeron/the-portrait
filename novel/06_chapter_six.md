# CHAPTER 6 – Milan: The First Journey

## 1504 – Milan

Francesco left Florence with the determination of a man chasing a runaway cart—urgent, focused, convinced that if he could just catch up, everything would be set right again.

He wasn't angry anymore; that had burned off somewhere during the week of planning. He was _injured_. There's a difference, and it's an important one. Anger cools, given time and distance. Injured pride festers. It grows. It convinces a man that his very honor is at stake, that he must act, that standing idle would be a form of defeat worse than any financial loss.

And when a man like my husband thinks his honor is at stake—his reputation as someone who keeps his word and expects the same in return—he'll cross the Apennines in spring when the roads are still muddy from snow melt, just to hear a sentence or two of reassurance.

The trip cost him more than he'd admit, even to me. Lost business while the shop ran without him. Expenses for lodging, meals, the hiring of a guide through the mountain passes. And perhaps most painfully for a man who valued his dignity: the small humiliation of explaining to every fellow merchant he encountered on the road why he was traveling so far from home when it wasn't even a major trading season.

"My painter left with my portrait," he said awkwardly the first few times, to men at roadside inns who looked at him with polite confusion.

By the fifth or sixth retelling, somewhere in the mountains, he'd refined it: "I have urgent matters to discuss with an engineer currently in the service of the French governor of Milan."

That sounded more impressive. More like business. Less like a man chasing after someone who'd taken his money and vanished.

He arrived in Milan after nearly two weeks of travel—longer than he'd hoped, because spring rains had made some roads impassable and he'd had to take a longer route—and discovered, to his simultaneous horror and secret delight, that Leonardo wasn't hiding at all.

He was simply _busy_. Very, very busy. Adored by the French court. Indispensable to the governor. In demand by artists, engineers, musicians, nobles, and apparently everyone in Milan who had a problem they thought genius could solve.

When Francesco finally tracked him down—after two days of chasing rumors through workshops, palaces, construction sites, and even, bizarrely, a kitchen where Leonardo had apparently been consulting on the mechanics of a new type of oven—Leonardo greeted him as though they'd parted only the day before, as though Francesco's presence in Milan was the most natural thing in the world.

"Oh! Signor del Giocondo!" Leonardo's face lit with what appeared to be genuine pleasure. "You're here. How wonderful. You absolutely must see what we're building for the governor. The engineering is fascinating—wait until you see the hydraulics."

That casual "we"—as if Francesco were already part of Leonardo's circle, as if his appearance in Milan had been expected, welcomed, perfectly natural—did something to my husband. It hooked him. Pulled him forward like a fish that doesn't realize it's been caught until the line goes tight.

Before Francesco could even ask about the portrait—the entire reason he'd come, supposedly—Leonardo had swept him into his current life like a leaf caught in a stream.

Through rooms full of musicians rehearsing for court entertainments, their instruments half-assembled while Leonardo sketched improvements to their design. Past engineers folding paper into wing shapes, testing theories about flight that seemed equal parts brilliant and insane. Through workshops where young assistants ground pigments while arguing about color theory in three different languages. Into halls where French officials in elaborate dress argued with Italian craftsmen about measurements and materials and money, everyone gesturing dramatically, no one quite understanding anyone else but somehow making progress anyway.

And then—and this was the part Francesco told me three times, his voice still breathless with the memory—Leonardo introduced him to Charles d'Amboise himself. The French governor. The man who effectively ruled Milan on behalf of King Louis XII.

"Your Excellency," Leonardo had said, one hand on Francesco's shoulder in a gesture of easy familiarity that made my husband stand a little straighter, "may I present Francesco del Giocondo, a most honorable Florentine merchant and patron of the arts. A man of excellent judgment and fine taste."

And the governor—the _governor_—had paused his conversation with two men who looked far more important than my husband, and actually _spoken_ to Francesco. Asked about Florence. About trade between the cities. About silk, because someone had mentioned Francesco's business. Three full minutes of conversation, maybe four, before duty pulled him away.

Francesco told me that part three times. He even changed his voice to imitate the governor's French accent, which would have been funny if he hadn't been so deadly serious about it.

And throughout it all, the portrait—_my_ portrait, the reason for this entire expensive journey—was supposedly somewhere in that chaos. Leonardo mentioned it vaguely, gestured toward a far corner of his workshop where panels leaned against walls, covered in cloth to protect them from dust.

"It's coming along beautifully," he assured Francesco. "Just a matter of resolving some technical challenges with the sfumato technique I'm developing. You'll see her soon enough. The smile is particularly challenging—I want to capture something... elusive."

Francesco didn't see even a corner of the panel. Didn't get close enough to verify it even existed in Milan, let alone that any progress had been made.

But he did accompany Leonardo to a demonstration of some flying contraption—a frame of wood and canvas that was supposed to catch the wind but mostly just collapsed dramatically, though Leonardo seemed delighted by what he learned from its failure.

And to a discussion with military engineers about fortress defenses, where Leonardo sketched rapid diagrams showing how traditional fortifications could be defeated by new artillery tactics, and how walls needed to be redesigned entirely for this new age of gunpowder.

And to an evening gathering at a minor noble's palazzo, where musicians played pieces Leonardo had apparently "improved" with suggestions about tempo and harmony, all while he sketched designs for a new type of water pump on whatever paper was available, including, at one point, the back of someone's dinner invitation.

Three days in Milan. Three days of being pulled through Leonardo's world like a tourist being shown the wonders of a foreign city.

And then Leonardo had to leave for somewhere else—Pavia, Francesco thought, or maybe Cremona, some place that needed his expertise on something urgent—and there was no time to discuss the portrait properly, no moment to pin him down about timelines or delivery.

"Write to me," Leonardo said warmly, clasping Francesco's hand in both of his. "Let me know how things are in Florence. And don't worry about the portrait—these things take time, but they're worth the wait. You'll see."

When Francesco finally returned home—another two weeks of travel, more expenses, more time away from business—he carried no painting, no binding agreements, no firm promises, and not even an apology for the inconvenience.

He brought back something far worse: deeper, more complicated admiration.

He came through our door exhausted, covered in road dust that had worked its way into every fold of his clothing, his boots worn from walking when the roads were too muddy for riding, his face sun-weathered and wind-chapped.

And he was grinning like he'd tasted a new life and found it intoxicating.

"Lisa!" He barely waited for me to greet him before the words started tumbling out. "He introduced me to the French governor. The _governor_. Charles d'Amboise himself. We spoke about trade, about Florence, about the differences between Italian and French silk. And Leonardo—" He paused to catch his breath, setting down his travel bag without even looking where it landed. "Leonardo asked for my opinion on the strength and durability of materials for a trade canal they're considering. _Me_. As though my judgment—well, Lisa, he really listens to people. Truly listens, as if every person has something valuable to offer if you just pay attention."

I helped him off with his filthy cloak, trying not to show my exasperation. "So," I said, keeping my voice light, almost teasing. "The portrait is finished, then?"

That wiped the grin off his face for exactly three seconds. Then he shrugged—actually _shrugged_—as if the portrait were a minor detail we'd gotten too fixated on.

"Not yet. There were some technical challenges he's still working through. But, Lisa..." The grin returned, brighter than before. "You should see the things he's doing. The _scale_ of it. He's not just painting pretty pictures for nobles—he's redesigning how buildings are constructed, how water flows through cities, how music is composed. He's trying to understand _everything_. How often do you meet someone like that?"

He talked for hours. Longer than he'd talked on the day our first son was born, longer than he'd talked about any business triumph, longer than I'd heard him talk about anything except perhaps the first time he'd told me he loved me, back when we were young and everything seemed possible.

He described Milan's streets, the French presence, the mixture of cultures and languages. He explained Leonardo's workshop in elaborate detail—every tool, every sketch, every half-finished project. He repeated conversations he'd overhead between engineers, musicians, artists, all orbiting around Leonardo like planets around the sun.

And I'll admit it, though it pained me to see: he looked younger. Happier. More alive than he'd been in months, maybe years. His eyes were bright with something I could only call wonder. As if being casually included in Leonardo's chaotic, brilliant world—even being politely ignored by him, really—was the most thrilling experience he'd ever had.

The portrait remained in Milan, wherever it actually was.

My husband's heart, however, had taken up permanent residence in every room Leonardo had stood in, every conversation Leonardo had led, every casual moment of recognition Leonardo had offered.

And I knew, watching him glow with this secondhand light, that this was far from over.

This was just the beginning.

---

But there was one evening in Milan that Francesco told me about much later, almost sheepishly, as if he'd been hoarding the memory like a treasure he wasn't quite ready to share.

It was his second night there. Leonardo had finished whatever urgent consultation he'd been conducting—something about theatrical machinery for an upcoming court entertainment—and as the workshop emptied of assistants and hangers-on, he'd turned to Francesco with a casual invitation.

"Are you hungry? I know a place. Nothing fancy, but the wine is decent and they don't mind if you sit for hours."

They'd walked through Milan's evening streets to a small osteria tucked away from the main thoroughfares. The kind of place where artists and craftsmen gathered, where the walls were stained with smoke and wine, where the conversation was loud and the food was simple but good.

Leonardo had ordered without looking at Francesco—bread, cheese, olives, roasted chicken, two carafes of wine—with the easy authority of someone who ate there often.

They'd sat in a corner, and for the first time since Francesco had arrived in Milan, Leonardo had stopped moving. Stopped sketching. Stopped explaining things to other people or being pulled in twelve directions at once. He'd simply sat there, eating and drinking, and for a while they didn't talk about much at all.

Francesco had asked him about Milan, about the French, about whether he missed Florence.

"Miss it?" Leonardo had considered this, tearing bread and dipping it in olive oil. "I miss certain things. The light is different there—better for painting, though Milan has its own beauty. But Florence is... complicated. Too many memories. Too many people who want things from me." He'd smiled then, a little rueful. "Though I suppose that's true everywhere."

"Do I want too much from you?" Francesco had asked, surprising himself with his own boldness. Perhaps it was the wine.

Leonardo had looked at him then—really _looked_, in a way he rarely did when his mind was full of other things. "You want a portrait of your wife. That's reasonable. You paid for it, and you should have it. I _will_ finish it, Francesco. I promise you that. Some day."

"Some day," Francesco had repeated, and they'd both laughed, because what else could you do with a promise like that?

"The problem," Leonardo had said, leaning back in his chair, "is that I keep seeing new things. New ways to approach the shadows, the light on skin, the quality of her expression. Every time I think I'm close, I realize there's something more subtle, more true, that I haven't captured yet. It's maddening."

"Then perhaps you should just finish it," Francesco had suggested gently. "Accept that it's good enough."

"Good enough." Leonardo had smiled. "That's not really in my nature, I'm afraid. Never has been. I start things with such enthusiasm, such certainty that _this time_ I'll see it through to completion. And then I see the flaws, the possibilities, all the ways it could be better, and I can't help myself. I have to keep working, keep trying, even when everyone else thinks I should move on."

They'd sat in comfortable silence for a moment, drinking wine, watching the other patrons argue and laugh and live their ordinary lives.

"I'm glad you came to Milan," Leonardo had said finally. "It's good to see a friendly face. Most people here want something specific from me—finish this project, solve this problem, make this thing work. You just... came to see me. That's rare."

Francesco had felt a flush of pleasure at those words, even as he'd recognized their gentle untruth. He _had_ come for something specific—the portrait. But in that moment, in that small osteria with good wine and easy companionship, it had felt almost true that he'd come just to see Leonardo.

"I consider you a friend," Francesco had said, the wine making him brave. "If that's not presumptuous."

Leonardo had reached across the table and clasped his hand briefly. "Not at all. Friends are precious. I don't have as many as I should, perhaps. Too busy chasing ideas to maintain proper friendships. But yes, Francesco. I'd be honored to call you a friend."

They'd stayed until late, until the osteria was nearly empty and the owner was giving them pointed looks. They'd talked about everything and nothing—about their childhoods, about what they'd imagined their lives would be, about the strangeness of growing older and finding yourself in places you never expected.

And when they'd finally left, walking through Milan's dark streets back toward where Francesco was staying, Leonardo had said, almost as an afterthought: "The portrait really is coming along well, you know. Your wife has an interesting face. Calm, but with something mysterious underneath. It's taking time because I want to get it exactly right. You understand, don't you?"

"I understand," Francesco had said, and meant it.

That was the evening he carried home more carefully than any painting could have been carried. The memory of being seen not as a patron, not as a merchant, not as someone from a certain class or city, but simply as Francesco. A friend.

No wonder he came home glowing.
