## **Chapter 7: The Pattern Establishes**

The years that followed developed a rhythm—an absurd, expensive rhythm that only made sense to one man: my husband.

It always began the same way. Francesco would burst into our home announcing that he was _furious_. Outraged. Offended to the core. His honor—always his honor—had been trampled again.

“I am going to settle this once and for all,” he’d declare, slamming a hand on the table for emphasis. “The portrait is my right. He was paid. _Paid._”

And I would nod as though this speech were new.

Then, after two days of storming around the house, he’d pack his best clothes, his merchant’s purse, and the little traveling icon he insisted protected him from accidents. Off he went—again—to retrieve the painting from the maestro who treated completion like a philosophical question.

Leonardo, meanwhile, moved like a migratory bird with a sketchbook. One year he drifted back to Florence, and Francesco rushed after him. The next, he slipped away to Rome, and Francesco followed again, insisting the business could run itself for “just a little while.”

Each time he returned empty-handed, and each time he insisted he’d been _this_ close.

In Florence, Leonardo had shown him a new design for a bridge so ambitious that Francesco stopped being angry and started calculating how many ships could pass beneath it.

In Rome, Leonardo had demonstrated an experiment with optics that nearly set fire to a tapestry, and Francesco had cried laughing while explaining it to me.

I teased him once—just once—that perhaps he didn’t want the portrait finished. Perhaps the chase itself had become his true commission.

He denied it so quickly and so dramatically that I knew I’d struck truth. “My honor is at stake,” he insisted, thumping his chest like a general. “This portrait is my priority.”

But priorities don’t usually make a man look _that happy_.

Our expenses, however, were entirely real. The journeys weren’t short, nor cheap. And each time he returned, a little thinner, a little dustier, a little more enchanted, I wondered how long the chase could continue before he realized he didn’t actually want it to end.

Meanwhile, our children grew from toddlers into small philosophers. They learned their father’s pattern as well as I had.

“Where is Papa?” they’d ask the servants.

And every time—without hesitation, without confusion—they got the same answer:

“He’s gone to visit Maestro Leonardo again.”

Eventually, the kids stopped treating it like travel.

To them, it was simply where fathers went.
