# CHAPTER 7 – The Pattern Establishes

## 1504–1510 – Florence, Rome, and the Roads Between

The years that followed developed a rhythm—an absurd, expensive, utterly predictable rhythm that only made sense to one man in our household: my husband.

It always began the same way, like a theatrical performance he'd rehearsed so often he could deliver his lines while barely awake.

Francesco would burst into whatever room I occupied—the courtyard, the kitchen, our bedchamber, once memorably while I was supervising the washing—announcing that he was furious. Outraged. Offended to his very core. His honor—always his honor, never simply his money or his patience—had been trampled upon yet again.

"I am going to settle this once and for all," he'd declare, often slamming a hand on the nearest table or wall for dramatic emphasis, making the servants jump. "The portrait is my right. He was paid. _Paid_. Good Florentine gold, and what do I have to show for it? Nothing. This cannot stand. I will not allow myself to be taken for a fool any longer."

And I would nod as though this speech were fresh and new, as though I hadn't heard variations of it a dozen times before.

"Of course, dear," I'd say. "When will you leave?"

"As soon as—" He'd pause, caught off guard that I'd skipped ahead in the script. "Well. Soon. Very soon. I must make arrangements."

Then, after two days of storming around the house practicing increasingly severe things he would say to Leonardo, he'd pack his best traveling clothes, his merchant's purse (fuller than it should be for a simple confrontation), and the little traveling icon of Saint Christopher he insisted protected him from accidents on the road. Off he would go—_again_—to retrieve the painting from the maestro who treated completion like an interesting philosophical question rather than a contractual obligation.

Leonardo, meanwhile, moved like a migratory bird with a sketchbook—no fixed home, just a series of perches chosen for various obscure reasons that made perfect sense to him and no one else.

One year he drifted back to Florence, summoned by the Signoria for some half-forgotten commission, and Francesco rushed after him with the urgency of someone chasing the last ship before winter. The next year, Leonardo slipped away to Rome, lured by papal patronage and promises of unlimited resources for whatever projects caught his fancy, and Francesco followed again within weeks, insisting the business could run itself for "just a little while."

"Marco is perfectly capable," he'd say, referring to our senior shop assistant. "And it's only two weeks. Three at most. I'll be back before you notice I'm gone."

I always noticed. The children certainly noticed. But Francesco had convinced himself that these absences were brief interruptions rather than the growing center of his life.

Each time he returned empty-handed, and each time he insisted he'd been _so close_ to resolution.

---

The visits when Leonardo returned to Florence were different, though. More complicated. Because then Francesco couldn't excuse his obsession as necessary travel—Leonardo was right here, working out of various borrowed workshops, and Francesco had no reason to spend quite so much time visiting except that he wanted to.

And Leonardo, with either genuine thoughtfulness or masterful manipulation—I was never quite sure which—would occasionally visit our home.

"I should see your lovely wife again," he'd say to Francesco, as if the idea had just occurred to him. "Refresh my memory of her features. The light in your home, the way she sits naturally—these things matter for the portrait, you understand."

Francesco would light up like a child promised a festival. "Of course! Yes! Come for dinner. Come any time. Lisa would be delighted."

I was not delighted, exactly, but I was curious.

Leonardo would arrive—always slightly disheveled, always carrying something (a sketchbook, a strange mechanical device, once a jar containing something he was studying for anatomical purposes that I refused to look at too closely)—and Francesco would transform.

He became the perfect host, the cultured patron, showing off our home as if Leonardo were a prince doing us honor with his presence. Which, in Francesco's mind, he probably was.

"Please, Maestro, sit here. The light is good. Lisa, come sit where he can see you properly."

And I would sit, and Leonardo would study me with that peculiar intensity that made me feel both invisible and too visible at once. His eyes would move over my face the way Francesco's moved over fine fabric—analytically, appreciatively, seeing things I couldn't see in myself.

"The smile," he'd murmur, sometimes to me, sometimes to himself. "There's something in it I keep trying to capture. Not happiness exactly. Something more knowing. More mysterious."

"I'm not mysterious," I'd say. "I'm a merchant's wife who manages a household and tries to keep the accounts balanced while my husband chases painters across Italy."

Leonardo would laugh at that—a real laugh, warm and surprised. "That is exactly the mystery. You see clearly, yet you allow this chase to continue. Why?"

Because I loved my fool of a husband, but I didn't say that. I just smiled, and Leonardo would nod as if I'd confirmed something important.

"There. That smile. That's what I'm trying to paint."

These visits would inevitably expand beyond their stated purpose. Leonardo would stay for dinner, and the meal would stretch into evening as he and Francesco talked. Sometimes about art—techniques, pigments, the challenges of working on large commissions. More often about everything else.

Engineering projects Leonardo was consulting on. The politics of Rome versus Florence. Whether the Turkish threat was as serious as people claimed. The best way to transport marble. Why certain bridges collapsed while others stood for centuries. The nature of human flight. The movement of water. The structure of the human heart.

Francesco would lean forward, enraptured, asking questions that showed he'd been thinking about things Leonardo had mentioned months ago. And Leonardo would answer, drawing diagrams on whatever paper was available, including once on the back of one of our inventory sheets, which Francesco carefully preserved as if it were a holy relic.

I'd watch them from across the table, these two men from such different worlds, finding common ground in curiosity and conversation. And I'd understand, watching them, why my husband kept chasing. It wasn't really about the portrait anymore, if it ever truly had been.

It was about these moments. About being the person Leonardo chose to talk to, to share ideas with, to trust with his half-formed thoughts.

After Leonardo left—always with promises about the portrait, always with "soon, very soon, just a few more details to resolve"—Francesco would be high on the encounter for days.

"Did you hear what he said about the aqueduct design? Brilliant. Absolutely brilliant."

"He's thinking of writing a treatise on painting. He asked if I thought merchants would buy such a book. I said of course they would—anyone with sense wants to understand beauty."

"He remembered that conversation we had last year about wood grain. Mentioned it specifically. The man forgets nothing when it interests him."

I never had the heart to point out that Leonardo seemed to forget quite reliably about finishing the portrait.

---

But most of the time, Leonardo wasn't in Florence, and Francesco had to travel to wherever genius had temporarily landed.

In Florence, during one of Leonardo's brief returns, he'd shown Francesco a design for a bridge so ambitious—a single span that could cross the entire Golden Horn in Constantinople—that Francesco had stopped being angry about the portrait entirely and spent the next hour calculating how many ships could pass beneath it, what the trade implications would be, whether such a thing was even possible.

In Rome, where Leonardo had gone to work for the Pope's brother, he'd demonstrated an experiment with curved mirrors and sunlight that was supposed to prove something about optics but instead nearly set fire to an expensive tapestry. Francesco had laughed so hard telling me about it that he couldn't breathe—partly at the near-disaster, partly at the Pope's brother's expression, partly at Leonardo's complete lack of concern about almost burning down a room full of priceless art.

"He just moved the mirrors and tried again," Francesco had wheezed, wiping his eyes. "As if it were perfectly normal to nearly incinerate a palazzo."

I teased him once—just once, because his reaction told me never to do it again—that perhaps he didn't actually want the portrait finished. Perhaps the chase itself had become his true commission, the thing he'd actually paid for without realizing it.

He denied it so quickly and so dramatically—standing up, hand over his heart like he was taking an oath—that I knew I'd struck some uncomfortable truth he wasn't ready to face.

"My honor is at stake," he insisted, thumping his chest like a general rallying troops. "A man's word is his bond, and when that bond is broken, he has a duty to seek redress. This portrait is my priority. My absolute priority. Everything I do is in service of securing what is rightfully mine."

But priorities don't usually make a man's eyes shine like that when he talks about them. Priorities are grim things, duties to be fulfilled. This was something else—something closer to joy.

Our expenses, however, were entirely real and entirely ungrim. The journeys weren't short, and they certainly weren't cheap. Travel costs, lodging, meals, the gifts Francesco always brought to smooth his way. The time away from business during crucial seasons. The opportunities missed while he was on the road.

I kept the accounts. I saw the numbers. We weren't in danger—our business was solid, and Francesco's earlier years of building it carefully meant we had reserves. But those reserves were slowly, steadily being spent on roads and inns and the pursuit of a painting that never seemed to get any closer to our walls.

Each time he returned—a little thinner from the journey, a little dustier from the roads, a little more enchanted with whatever he'd witnessed—I wondered how long the chase could continue before he realized he didn't actually want it to end.

Because an ended chase meant no more visits. No more conversations. No more moments of being treated as Leonardo's friend rather than his patron. No more standing close enough to genius to feel its warmth.

As long as the portrait remained unfinished, Francesco had a reason to keep going back. A legitimate reason, one he could defend to me, to himself, to anyone who questioned why a sensible merchant kept traveling across Italy to visit an artist who clearly had no intention of delivering his commission any time soon.

Meanwhile, our children grew from toddlers into small, observant people with opinions about everything. They learned their father's pattern as thoroughly as I had, absorbed it as simply part of the natural rhythm of our household.

"Where is Papa?" they'd ask the servants when Francesco's place at the table was empty for dinner.

And every time—without hesitation, without confusion, as if it were the most normal thing in the world—they got the same answer:

"He's gone to visit Maestro Leonardo again."

Sometimes they'd add details: "In Rome this time," or "Milan, I think," or "Somewhere north—your father wasn't entirely clear."

Eventually, after the first dozen absences, the children stopped treating it like travel, like something unusual worth remarking on. It became simply where fathers went, what fathers did. Like how some fathers went to the docks to oversee shipments, or to the countryside to check on property. Their father went to chase a painter who wouldn't finish a portrait.

"Will Maestro Leonardo ever finish Mama's painting?" young Piero asked me once, maybe seven years old, with the devastating directness children sometimes have.

"Someday," I told him, echoing Leonardo's favorite word.

"When is someday?"

"When it arrives," I said, because I had no better answer.

He'd accepted this with a solemn nod, as if I'd explained something profound about the nature of time itself, and gone back to his lessons.

And Francesco kept going. Kept chasing. Kept returning with empty hands and full stories.

Kept being happy in a way he'd never been happy before, even if he couldn't quite admit what was making him happy.

The portrait remained wherever Leonardo was, probably still unfinished, possibly still being worked on, certainly still the perfect excuse for everything my husband had come to need.
