# CHAPTER 8 – Lisa's Reckoning

## 1507 – Florence

At some point—don't ask me to pinpoint the exact moment, because I couldn't tell you if my life depended on it—I realized the portrait had ceased to matter.

Not to me, particularly. I'd stopped caring about having my face immortalized in paint somewhere around the second or third year of this saga. But more significantly: it had ceased to matter to Francesco, though he'd have denied this with his dying breath.

It might have been after the third journey to Milan. Or the fifth trip to Rome. Or maybe the seventh expedition to God knows where, when Francesco returned empty-handed as always but carrying a detailed drawing of some sort of mechanical man—a device Leonardo had sketched that could supposedly walk on two legs like a human being—and insisted our eldest son "ought to build a model of it, just to understand the principles involved."

Piero was nine. He could barely build a tower of blocks without it collapsing. But Francesco was utterly serious about this project.

That should have been my first clear warning.

But the moment that truly stands out sharp and clear in my memory, the moment I _knew_ with absolute certainty, came on an unremarkable Tuesday afternoon. I was sitting at our dining table with the late autumn light slanting through the windows, mending a torn sleeve on one of Francesco's better doublets—the one he wore when he wanted to make a good impression, which was most of the time lately.

I suggested, as gently as I possibly could, keeping my voice casual and my eyes on my needlework, that perhaps we should consider commissioning a new portrait.

"A new portrait?" Francesco looked up from the account books he'd been pretending to review.

"Raphael is in Florence now," I continued, threading my needle carefully. "Working on something for the Popes nephew, but apparently taking other commissions as well. People say his work is extraordinary. Very elegant. Very... refined."

Francesco was already shaking his head, but I pressed on.

"And people say—well, they say he delivers on time. Takes direction well. Works quickly. Most importantly, he actually finishes things." I glanced up, meeting my husband's eyes. "We could have a portrait hanging in our hall within six months. A beautiful portrait. Of both of us, perhaps. A proper family commission."

Francesco reacted as though I'd proposed replacing his heart with a kitchen implement.

"Raphael?" he sputtered, standing up so quickly his chair scraped against the floor. "_Raphael_?"

"I only meant—"

"No, no, Lisa. Absolutely not." He was pacing now, hands gesturing emphatically. "I won't have a portrait by some—some—_boy_ hanging in my home when the great Leonardo da Vinci has already begun ours. Started it. Invested his genius in it. You can't just... replace Leonardo."

"He's hardly a boy," I said mildly. "Raphael is quite accomplished. And he's actually in Florence. Available. Working. Unlike—"

"It would be an insult," Francesco interrupted, his voice rising with genuine distress. "To Leonardo, yes, but also to ourselves. To settle for second-best just because the best is taking his time? What would that say about our judgment? Our taste? Our standards?"

I opened my mouth to point out that waiting forever for something we'd never receive seemed like poor judgment too, but then I saw his face. Really saw it. The panic in his eyes, the almost desperate set of his jaw.

And I quietly set my needle aside.

That was the moment I understood, with perfect clarity: this had nothing to do with a painting.

Francesco didn't want a portrait. Any portrait. He wanted Leonardo's portrait specifically. More precisely, he wanted _Leonardo_. His attention, his conversation, his recognition. The chance to be part of that world of genius and ideas, even from the margins. My husband, practical merchant Francesco del Giocondo, had developed the Renaissance equivalent of being a devotee. A follower. A—what would they call it in later times?—a groupie.

He had found his calling, and it wasn't silk.

I looked at him standing there, still agitated, still ready to defend his position against any reasonable argument I might make, and I felt something shift inside me. Not anger. Not even disappointment, really. Just a kind of weary acceptance.

And honestly? When I thought about it clearly, when I compared Leonardo to some of the things other wealthy men in Florence obsessed over—gambling away fortunes at dice, keeping expensive mistresses in houses nicer than their wives enjoyed, playing dangerous political games that could get their entire families exiled or worse, buying titles and offices they had no business holding just to feed their vanity—when I considered all those alternatives, Leonardo da Vinci was practically wholesome.

At least my husband was chasing knowledge, beauty, innovation. At least he came home enthusiastic rather than drunk or guilty or paranoid. At least his obsession was with a man who, for all his flaws regarding completion and timelines, was genuinely extraordinary.

There were worse vices.

So I stopped fighting it. Not because I agreed with it, not because I thought it was wise or sensible or good for our finances. But because resistance at this point was like trying to shout down a cathedral bell. Futile, exhausting, and likely to give me a headache.

"You're right," I said, picking up my mending again. "Raphael wouldn't be appropriate. I wasn't thinking."

Francesco looked at me suspiciously, clearly not trusting this sudden capitulation. "You're... agreeing with me?"

"I am."

"Just like that?"

"Just like that." I pulled thread through fabric, keeping my eyes on the work. "Leonardo has begun our portrait. It would be foolish to abandon it now. We simply need to be patient."

I could feel him studying me, trying to determine if this was some kind of trap. But eventually he seemed to accept it at face value, and the tension drained from his shoulders.

"Yes. Exactly. Patient. These things take time when you're creating something truly great."

"Mmm," I agreed, noncommittally.

He sat back down, returning to his account books with renewed energy, as if this conversation had somehow validated his entire approach to the situation.

And I continued mending, thinking about what I'd realized.

Besides... he was happier this way. That was undeniable. Eventually.

The pattern had become familiar enough that I could track it like phases of the moon. He'd work himself into a state about the portrait, declare he was going to confront Leonardo once and for all, travel for weeks, come back without the painting but transformed—glowing, energized, full of stories and ideas.

He would talk about mechanisms Leonardo was designing for stage productions, the mathematical principles behind perfect perspective, new theories about human anatomy that would change how artists understood the body, proposed tunnels under mountains, improved systems for everything from city water supplies to military fortifications.

Most of it sounded to me like someone dreaming out loud, sketching possibilities that might or might not ever become real. But I could see it fed something in Francesco that had been starving before he met Leonardo. Some need for wonder, for complexity, for questions that didn't have simple answers or immediate commercial applications.

Our dinner conversations had changed entirely. Where once Francesco had talked about wool prices and guild politics and which merchant families were rising or falling, now he talked about whether human flight was physically possible, why certain arches held weight better than others, what made one painting luminous and another flat.

Our children heard these conversations. Absorbed them. Grew up thinking that dinner table discussions about the mechanics of bird wings and the properties of light were perfectly normal.

I'm not sure that was entirely healthy, but I also wasn't sure it was harmful.

Francesco didn't see it, of course. He never did, could never admit it to himself.

That the chase had already given him what the portrait was supposed to provide. Recognition—Leonardo remembered him, welcomed his visits, asked his opinions. Purpose—he had a quest, a goal, something beyond daily commercial transactions. Excitement—every journey brought new discoveries, new ideas, new stories.

Everything he'd tried to buy with money and commissions, everything he'd hoped would come from patronizing a great artist and hanging the results in our hall, he'd found instead in the endless pursuit of a genius who had no interest in completion, schedules, or contractual obligations.

If I told him that directly, he might stop chasing. Might realize that achieving his stated goal would mean losing what he'd actually found. And I couldn't predict how he'd react to that realization. Depression? Denial? Something worse?

So I kept quiet.

I was beginning to understand that my husband needed this the way some men need wine to get through the day, or dice to feel alive, or constant risk to feel like they matter. It was his addiction, his passion, the thing that made his life feel larger than the accumulation of florins and the running of a business that would probably be sold or divided after his death and forgotten within a generation.

This made him feel part of something that would last. That mattered. That transcended the ordinary.

And really, when I thought about it that way, how could I fight it?

At least Leonardo da Vinci was a relatively harmless vice. He didn't gamble away our fortune or bring home diseases or get Francesco involved in conspiracies that could end with exile or execution.

He just... never finished things.

And my husband had learned to live on hope, on proximity to genius, on the promise of "someday" that never quite arrived.

I could live with that. Had to live with that. Was learning to find my own amusement in it, even, the way one learns to find humor in any absurd situation that goes on long enough.

Besides, I had the house to run, the children to raise, the business to help manage when Francesco was away. I had my own life, my own concerns, my own small pleasures that had nothing to do with portraits or genius or quests across Italy.

Let Francesco have his Leonardo. Let him chase his impossible dream. Let him come home glowing with secondhand brilliance and stories about machines that walked like men.

At least he came home.

At least he was happy.

At least, in his own strange way, he'd found something worth chasing, even if he'd never catch it.

And who was I to say that wasn't enough?
