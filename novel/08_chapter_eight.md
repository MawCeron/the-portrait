## **Chapter 8: Lisa’s Reckoning**

At some point—don’t ask me exactly when—I realized the portrait had ceased to matter.

It might have been after the third journey. Or the fifth. Or maybe the seventh, when Francesco returned with a drawing of a machine that could walk like a man and insisted our eldest son “ought to build one, just to see.”

But the moment stands out sharply now: I was sitting at our dining table, mending a sleeve, when I suggested, as gently as I could, that perhaps we should commission a new portrait. Raphael was in Florence then, graceful and talented and, most importantly, _available_. People said he delivered on time. People said he took direction. People said he finished things.

Francesco reacted as though I’d proposed replacing his heart with a mop.

“Raphael?” he sputtered. “Raphael?!”

“I only meant—”

“No, no, Lisa. Absolutely not. I won’t have a portrait by some—some—_boy_ hanging in my home when the great Leonardo da Vinci has already begun ours.”

I opened my mouth. Closed it. And quietly set my needle aside.

That was the moment I understood: this had nothing to do with a painting. Francesco didn’t want _a_ portrait. He wanted _Leonardo’s_ portrait. More precisely, he wanted _Leonardo_. His attention, his genius, his orbit. My husband had developed the Renaissance equivalent of being a groupie.

And honestly? Compared to some of the things wealthy men in Florence obsessed over—gambling, mistresses, politics, buying titles they had no business buying—Leonardo da Vinci was practically wholesome.

I stopped fighting it. Not because I agreed with it, but because resistance was like trying to shout down a cathedral bell.

Besides… he was happier this way. Eventually.

He would come home after weeks away, glowing, transformed into a man who had glimpsed eternity and taken notes. He’d talk about mechanisms and pigments and tunnels under cities, and even though most of it sounded like someone dreaming out loud, I could see it fed him.

He didn’t see it, of course. He never did.

That the chase had already given him what the portrait was supposed to. Recognition, purpose, excitement—everything he’d tried to buy with money and commissions, he found instead in pursuing a genius who had no interest in completion or schedules.

If I told him that, he might stop chasing.

And I was beginning to understand that my husband needed this the way some men need wine or dice.

At least Leonardo da Vinci was a relatively harmless vice.
