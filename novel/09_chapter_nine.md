# PART THREE: The Grand Tour (1513-1516)

### "Following Genius Across Italy"

# CHAPTER 9 – Rome: The Pope Dodge

## 1513 – Rome

There comes a point in every long, ridiculous endeavor when a person tells himself, "This is the final attempt. Truly. I mean it this time."

Francesco had said this before—after Milan the second time, after Florence yet again, after that unfortunate detour through Bologna where he'd somehow managed to miss Leonardo entirely despite reliable reports that he'd been there just the week prior. Each time, he'd declared it his last expedition, his final effort, the moment when he'd either succeed or accept defeat with dignity.

But in 1513, ten years after that first commission, when news reached Florence that Leonardo had gone to Rome to work for the Pope himself, Francesco said it with a look that made me actually believe he might mean it this time.

"Leonardo is in Rome," he told me one evening, standing at the window overlooking our courtyard as if preparing for battle. He was tightening his belt—literally, not metaphorically, though both applied—with the grim determination of a man going to war. "Working for Pope Leo himself. Pope Leo X, Lisa. A Medici. A _Florentine_ Medici."

"I'm aware of who the Pope is," I said mildly, not looking up from my sewing.

"If I cannot reclaim the portrait there, under those circumstances, with that connection..." He trailed off, shaking his head. "Then it was never meant to be. This is it. The final attempt. After this, I'll..." He paused, as if unsure what he'd actually do. "I'll accept reality."

I set down my work and looked at him properly. He was older now—past fifty, though he carried himself like a younger man. But I could see the grey in his hair, the lines around his eyes. Ten years of this chase had marked him, though not always in bad ways. He looked more alive than he had at forty, if more tired.

"Very well," I said. "When do you leave?"

"Next week. I've already arranged everything with Marco."

I wished him luck, kissed his cheek—I had to stand on my toes now; when had he started stooping?—and advised him to at least try not to offend anyone who could excommunicate us.

"The Pope is Florentine," he said, as if this were a shield against all possible diplomatic disasters. "He'll understand."

He promised nothing else.

---

When he returned three weeks later and told me what had happened, he spoke slowly, carefully, the way a man recalls an accident he witnessed but still cannot fully process or believe was real.

He had found Leonardo on a street corner in Rome.

Not in a workshop. Not in the Vatican. Not even in some artist's studio or patron's palazzo. Just standing on a street corner near the Campo de' Fiori, apparently studying the way sunlight hit the corner of a building, or perhaps watching a bird, or possibly just lost in thought. After ten years of chasing across Italy, of careful planning and strategic arrivals, all it took was turning down the right alley at the right moment.

Fate, Francesco said, has a twisted sense of humor.

Leonardo had looked up, seen him, and his face had lit with what appeared to be genuine pleasure.

"Francesco! Francesco del Giocondo! What extraordinary fortune!" He'd crossed the street, dodging a cart, and clasped Francesco's hands warmly. "You've caught me between appointments. Perfect timing. How are you? How is Florence? How is your lovely wife?"

My husband had stood there, momentarily speechless. He'd rehearsed this confrontation a hundred times during the journey to Rome. Had prepared speeches about contracts and obligations and reasonable expectations. Had practiced being firm but not rude, assertive but not aggressive.

All of it evaporated in the face of Leonardo's genuine warmth.

He managed to explain—carefully, politely, with that trembling dignity I know too well, the kind that comes from trying to maintain composure when you want to either shout or weep—that he had come about the portrait. That it had been nearly a decade now. Nearly _ten years_, Maestro. That he understood these things took time, that genius couldn't be rushed, but that perhaps, possibly, if it wouldn't be too much trouble...

"Oh, the portrait!" Leonardo had exclaimed, as if suddenly remembering an old friend he'd been meaning to visit. "Yes, yes, of course! You're absolutely right. It's been too long. Unconscionable, really. Come—let's fetch it right now. I'll show you the progress. You'll be amazed at how it's developed."

This, apparently, was the moment Francesco allowed himself to imagine the triumph. The actual, tangible triumph he'd been chasing for a decade.

The portrait carefully wrapped and crated. The journey home with his prize finally secured. The unveiling in our hall, with neighbors and business associates invited to admire it. Proof that he was a man of culture, a patron of genius, someone whose judgment had been vindicated by time and patience.

The years of absurdity wrapped up with a tidy bow by the greatest artist alive.

They began walking together through Rome's crowded streets.

Francesco followed, chest swelling with anticipation, already mentally composing the letter he'd send ahead announcing his triumphant return... until he began to notice the direction they were walking.

North. They were walking north and east, away from the Trastevere district where Leonardo's workshop was supposedly located.

"Maestro," he said cautiously, trying to keep his voice light, "I was told your workshop is near Santa Maria in Trastevere. Are we not going—"

"No, no, that's just where I do the dissections," Leonardo said casually, as if this were a perfectly normal thing to say. "The paintings are elsewhere. Just ahead. Not far at all."

But "just ahead" led them toward Vatican Hill.

And "not far at all" turned out to be the Apostolic Palace itself.

At this point—my husband claims, and I believe him because his voice still carried the trauma of it when he told me—his soul attempted to flee his body. He actually stopped walking for a moment, staring at the imposing facade of the palace, and found himself looking up at the sky as though asking invisible saints for help, for guidance, for some way out of whatever was about to happen.

But Leonardo was already striding ahead, nodding to guards who recognized him, and Francesco had no choice but to follow or be left standing in the street like a fool.

Leonardo led him through hallways that seemed designed to make ordinary men feel small. Corridors painted by artists who actually finished things—Raphael's work practically glowed from the walls, fresh and complete and glorious, a silent rebuke to every incomplete project Leonardo had ever abandoned.

Francesco would tell me later, his voice quiet, that it felt like walking through a gallery of his own bad decisions. If he had commissioned Raphael to paint my portrait ten years ago, he'd have had it hanging in our hall for nine years now. Beautiful, finished, no longer a source of quiet humiliation when business associates asked to see the famous Leonardo portrait he'd supposedly commissioned.

But it was too late for such thoughts. They were deep in the palace now, and Francesco's sense of impending disaster was growing with each step.

Armed guards stood at attention at every turn—Swiss mercenaries mostly, serious men in practical military dress. Cardinals in red robes swept past, deep in conversation about theology or politics or whatever cardinals discussed. The air smelled of incense and ambition and the peculiar mixture of sanctity and worldly power that only Rome could produce.

Leonardo seemed utterly at home, greeting people by name, making small jokes, completely relaxed.

Francesco felt like he might vomit.

They climbed a staircase. Walked down another corridor. Turned a corner.

And then a guard was opening an ornate door, and Leonardo—his voice bright with what Francesco swears was genuine innocence, though I have my doubts—was leading him into a chamber where Pope Leo X himself sat at a desk covered in documents, his jeweled fingers holding a pen, his round face bent in concentration.

"Your Holiness," Leonardo said with cheerful formality, as though this were the most natural thing in the world, "may I present Francesco del Giocondo, a most distinguished merchant of Florence and a generous patron of the arts."

The Pope looked up.

Giovanni de' Medici—Pope Leo X, the former Giovanni, whom Francesco had probably seen a dozen times in Florence before he became Pope—his face lit up the way only a Medici recognizing another Florentine in a foreign city can light up.

"A Florentine!" The Pope actually stood, smiling warmly. "We are practically neighbors! Come, come closer. Tell me, how fares our beautiful city? I miss it terribly, you know. They tell me Rome is the center of Christendom, but Florence is the center of my heart."

And that was that.

My husband, who had traveled to Rome for the specific and singular purpose of confronting a chronic procrastinator about a decade-old commission, found himself bowing deeply to the Vicar of Christ, successor to Saint Peter, the most powerful religious figure in the known world, while trying desperately not to sweat through his second-best doublet.

He could not be rude. Not to the Pope. Not even slightly impolite.

He could not demand his painting. Not here. Not now. Not ever, possibly.

He could barely speak without his voice shaking.

For what felt like hours but was probably twenty or twenty-five excruciating minutes, the Pope chatted pleasantly about Florence. About governance and the challenges of running a republic versus a monarchy. About wool taxes and trade with the Levant. About mutual acquaintances—did Francesco know this family, that merchant, those bankers? About the weather in Florence versus Rome. About food, about architecture, about a dozen things that Francesco would later not remember clearly because his entire being was focused on not fainting or saying something catastrophically stupid.

And at some point during this conversation—Francesco isn't sure exactly when, because time had stopped making sense—he glanced sideways toward where Leonardo had been standing near a window.

Leonardo was gone.

Simply gone. Vanished. As though he had stepped behind a tapestry and evaporated into mist.

Francesco's eyes darted around the room with what he hoped was subtle desperation. But no, Leonardo da Vinci was simply no longer present in the chamber. He had somehow slipped away while the Pope was discussing Florentine banking practices, and no one had noticed or cared.

The conversation with the Pope continued—His Holiness seemed in no hurry to dismiss this fellow Florentine—but Francesco's mind was reeling. Had Leonardo planned this? Surely not. Surely even Leonardo wouldn't weaponize the Pope himself as a distraction technique.

And yet.

Eventually, finally, mercifully, a cardinal appeared in the doorway with an apologetic cough, and the Pope sighed regretfully. "Duty calls, as always. It was a pleasure, Signor del Giocondo. Give my regards to Florence. And if you see our dear Leonardo, tell him I still await those designs he promised for the villa."

Francesco bowed his way out of the papal presence, his legs somehow still functioning despite feeling like they'd been replaced with rope.

He stumbled through the corridors of the palace, past the glowing Raphaels, past the armed guards, past the cardinals and priests and politicians, until he finally emerged into the Roman sunlight like a man escaping a shipwreck.

He stood there on the street, dazed, humiliated, and—despite everything—impressed against his will.

"That man," he muttered to himself, aware he probably looked like a madman talking to the air, "walked me into the papal chambers to avoid telling me he hasn't finished my portrait."

It was brilliant. Infuriating and brilliant.

He never found Leonardo again during that visit to Rome. Searched for two more days, but the Maestro had apparently become very busy with urgent projects that kept him away from his workshop whenever Francesco came calling.

---

He told me all of this when he returned to Florence, his voice mixing awe and devastation in equal measure.

"I met the Pope," he said, still somewhat stunned by this fact. "Had a conversation with Pope Leo X about Florentine wool taxes."

"That's wonderful," I said, genuinely meaning it. "That's quite an honor."

"He used the Pope as a shield, Lisa." Francesco shook his head slowly. "He saw me on that street corner and thought, 'I know exactly how to avoid this conversation,' and walked me straight into the Apostolic Palace."

"Perhaps he genuinely wanted you to meet the Pope," I suggested. "You said they seemed to know each other well."

"Oh, they do. Leonardo is apparently indispensable to His Holiness. Which makes it all the more remarkable that he still hasn't finished a portrait he was paid for a decade ago."

I studied my husband's face. He looked tired, yes. Frustrated, certainly. But also—and this was what struck me—he looked almost proud. Proud to have been used so brilliantly. Proud to have a story this absurd to tell. Proud that Leonardo had considered him important enough to deploy the Pope as a distraction.

"Did you see the portrait at all?" I asked, though I already knew the answer.

"No."

"Do you know if it still exists?"

"No."

"Do you have any more information than you did before you left?"

"No." He paused. "Well, I know the Pope likes me. That's something."

I looked at him—this man I'd married, this merchant who'd become a pilgrim, this practical person who'd spent ten years chasing something he'd never catch—and I thought, not for the first time: If Leonardo ever actually finished that painting and delivered it to our door, my husband would have no idea what to do with himself.

The chase had become the point. The friendship—if that's what it was—had become more valuable than the art. Being part of Leonardo's world, even from the absurd periphery where one gets walked into papal chambers as a dodge, had become more important than owning any painting.

"I'm glad you met the Pope," I said finally. "That will be a good story for the children."

"Yes," he agreed, brightening slightly. "Yes, it will be."

And just like that, the disaster had become an anecdote. The humiliation had become an adventure. The complete failure to achieve his stated goal had somehow transformed into a victory of a different sort.

Ten years in, and Francesco had learned to call losing "winning."

I suppose that's a kind of wisdom.

Or madness.

Sometimes it's hard to tell the difference.
