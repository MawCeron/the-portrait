## **Chapter 9: Rome — The Pope Dodge (1513)**

There comes a point in every long, ridiculous endeavor when a person tells himself, “This is the final attempt. Truly. I mean it this time.”
Francesco had said this before—after Milan, after Florence again, after that unfortunate detour through Bologna—but in 1513 he said it with a look that made me believe he might actually mean it.

“Leonardo is in Rome,” he told me, tightening his belt as though going to war. “Working for Pope Leo himself. If I cannot reclaim the portrait there, then it was never meant to be.”

I wished him luck, kissed his cheek, and advised him to at least try not to offend anyone who could excommunicate us.

He promised nothing.

---

When he told me later what happened, he spoke slowly, the way a man recalls an accident he still cannot fully process.

He had found Leonardo on a Roman street corner—just standing there, as though waiting for an apple cart to pass. Ten years of chasing, and all it took was turning down the right alley. Fate has a twisted sense of humor.

Leonardo had greeted him warmly, delighted, as though Francesco were an old friend he’d surprisingly remembered.

“Francesco! What fortune. You’ve caught me between appointments.”

My husband explained—carefully, politely, with that trembling dignity I know too well—that he had come for the portrait. That it had been nearly a decade. That he would be most grateful if—

“Oh, the portrait!” Leonardo exclaimed. “Yes, yes, of course. You’re right. Come, let’s fetch it.”

This, apparently, was the moment Francesco allowed himself to imagine the triumph. The crate carried home. The unveiling. The years of absurdity wrapped up with a tidy bow by the greatest artist alive.

They began walking.

Francesco followed, chest swelling with anticipation… until he noticed the direction.

“Maestro,” he said cautiously, “your workshop is not—”

“No, no, it’s just ahead,” Leonardo said, waving his hand in a way that could mean anything or nothing.

But “ahead” turned out to be Vatican Hill.

And “just ahead” turned out to be the Apostolic Palace.

At this point in Francesco’s retelling, he stared into the middle distance as though asking invisible saints for help.

Because the next thing he knew, a Swiss Guard had opened a door, and Leonardo—utterly cheerful—led him straight into a chamber where Pope Leo X himself was reviewing documents.

“Your Holiness,” Leonardo said with innocent delight, “may I present Francesco del Giocondo, a distinguished merchant of Florence.”

The Pope’s face lit up as only a Medici recognizing another Florentine can.
“A Florentine! We are practically neighbors.”

And that was that. My husband, who had come to confront a chronic procrastinator about a decade-old commission, found himself bowing to the Vicar of Christ while trying not to sweat through his doublet.

He could not be rude.
He could not demand his painting.
He could barely speak.

For twenty excruciating minutes, the Pope chatted about Florence, about governance, about wool taxes, about the weather—while Francesco nodded like a man trying desperately to stay alive.

And at some point, he glanced sideways…

Leonardo was gone.

Simply gone. As though he had stepped behind a tapestry and evaporated.

Francesco eventually stumbled back onto the Roman street, dazed, humiliated, impressed against his will.

“That man,” he muttered, “walked me into the papal chambers to avoid telling me he hasn’t finished my portrait.”

He told me this with a mixture of awe and devastation.

And I thought, not for the first time:
If Leonardo ever finished that painting, my husband would have no idea what to do with himself.
