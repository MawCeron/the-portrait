# CHAPTER 10 – The Conversation

## 1513 – Florence

Francesco told me the story three times before I understood the true shape of it.

The first time, he recounted it breathlessly—still wearing his travel-stained clothes, barely through the door—half thrilled, half horrified, as though he'd just outrun a stampede of papal guards and wasn't entirely sure he'd escaped. Words tumbled over each other. The street corner. Leonardo's smile. The walk. The _palace_. The Pope. The _Pope_, Lisa. The vanishing.

The second time, after he'd bathed and eaten and we were sitting in the quiet of our bedchamber, he added details he'd forgotten in the first telling because he'd been too overwhelmed to process them properly. The Pope's rings—massive things, jewels that caught the light and threw it back like little suns. The way Leonardo had touched his shoulder with such easy familiarity before disappearing. The taste of fear in his mouth when he'd realized where they were heading. The specific way the Pope had pronounced "Firenze" with such fondness, like a man speaking of a lost love.

By the third time, late that night when he couldn't sleep and I'd found him sitting by the window staring at nothing, he spoke in a dull, almost embarrassed tone, rubbing at his forehead as though the memory itself gave him a headache.

"I was made a fool," he said quietly. "Used like a puppet. Walked into the papal chambers like a pet being shown off, and then abandoned there. And the worst part—the absolute worst part, Lisa—is that it was _brilliant_. So perfectly executed I almost admired him for it even while I was standing there sweating through my clothes and trying to remember how to speak Latin."

Only when he finally fell silent, when the words had run dry and he just sat there looking defeated in a way I hadn't seen since our first son died, did I reach for his hand.

It felt older than I remembered. When had his knuckles become so prominent? When had these calluses formed—not merchant's calluses from handling fabric, but traveler's calluses from gripping reins and walking staffs?

"Francesco," I said, as gently as I knew how, keeping my voice soft in the darkness, "it's been ten years."

He didn't respond, but his hand tightened slightly around mine.

"Ten years of traveling," I continued. "Of expenses that add up to more than we like to calculate. Of time away from home, away from the business, away from watching the children grow. Away from—"

"From you," he finished quietly, his voice rough with something that might have been guilt or grief or just exhaustion. "I know. Away from you."

He said it not dramatically, not as an apology seeking absolution, just... truthfully. Stating a fact that had slowly accumulated weight over a decade until it was too heavy to pretend it didn't exist.

And something in him sagged a little when he said it, like he'd been holding himself rigid against that truth and finally allowed himself to drop the weight he'd been carrying too tightly for too long.

We sat in silence for a moment. Outside, Florence slept. Inside, a marriage reckoned with itself.

I brushed my thumb over his knuckles, feeling the familiar landscape of his hand—so different now from the smooth-skinned merchant's hand I'd held when we married twenty-some years ago. "Raphael is still in Rome," I said carefully, not pushing, just offering. "You could commission him. While you were there. Or send word now. He finishes things, Francesco. People say he's remarkably fast. Reliable. Professional."

Francesco shook his head before I could even finish the thought. A small, definitive movement. "It wouldn't be the same."

"No," I agreed, because there was no point pretending otherwise. "It would be finished."

He winced—only slightly, just a tightening around his eyes, but I caught it. I'd been married to this man long enough to read his face in darkness.

There was a whole world trapped in that silence that followed. His pride that wouldn't let him admit he'd been chasing the wrong thing. His longing for something he couldn't quite name. His confusion about what he even wanted anymore. The strange, complicated joy he found in these impossible pursuits that always ended in failure but somehow felt like success anyway.

He couldn't explain it. I don't think he even understood it himself, not really. How do you explain to someone—to yourself—that you've spent ten years and a small fortune chasing something you don't actually want to catch? That the portrait stopped being the point years ago, if it ever truly was the point?

And pushing him to articulate it wouldn't change a thing. It would only make him defensive, force him to construct elaborate justifications for something that operated on a level beyond reason.

So I let the moment soften, let the tension drain away like water finding its level.

"Well," I said, injecting a note of lightness into my voice, trying to pull him back from wherever dark place his thoughts had wandered, "at least you can say you've met the Pope. That's something. How many merchants in Florence can claim that?"

That finally earned a small smile. Not a triumphant one—not the grin he'd worn when he came back from his first visit to Leonardo's workshop all those years ago. More the exhausted, bewildered kind one has after being led into the Apostolic Palace under completely false pretenses and somehow surviving the experience.

"He asked if we were neighbors," Francesco muttered, and now there was the faintest hint of humor creeping into his voice. "The Pope. Asked if I lived near the Medici palazzo in Florence, as if I were some sort of... of peer. As if I routinely dropped by for dinner with Cardinals."

"You do have very nice dinnerware," I offered. "Perhaps he sensed that."

A soft laugh escaped him—brief, but real. "The man rules all of Christendom and he wanted to discuss wool taxes. _Wool taxes_, Lisa. I had prepared for so many possible conversations, and somehow wool taxes wasn't on my list."

I squeezed his hand, then shifted to a more practical topic, one I knew would genuinely interest him. "Speaking of the Medici," I said, keeping my tone casual, as if this were just an afterthought rather than something I'd been saving for the right moment, "they've sent more orders. More than before. Substantially more, actually. It seems your name was mentioned favorably in certain circles."

His head turned toward me, eyes catching what little moonlight came through the window. "From Leonardo?"

I shrugged, though he probably couldn't see it in the darkness. "From someone. Someone with influence. And whoever it was, they spoke well of you. Well enough that the Medici factor specifically asked if Signor Francesco del Giocondo was the same merchant who had dealings with 'the renowned Maestro Leonardo.' I said yes, and he seemed impressed."

"Impressed," Francesco repeated, testing the word.

"Impressed enough to place a large order for ceremonial fabrics. Apparently they're preparing for some important Vatican function and want our finest work." I paused, then added carefully, "The kind of order that will be noticed by other families. The kind that establishes reputation beyond mere commerce."

I felt rather than saw the shift in him. The slight straightening of his spine. The way his breathing changed, became less defeated and more thoughtful.

This was what I'd learned over the years: Francesco needed to find his own way to understanding. If I told him directly—"The chase has already given you what you wanted, you fool"—he'd reject it, defend himself, double down on his stated goals. But if I let him discover it himself, let the evidence accumulate until he could no longer ignore it...

"So the journeys..." he said slowly, thinking out loud. "The visits to Leonardo, being seen in those circles, meeting the people he knows..."

"Has consequences," I finished gently. "Not always the ones we expect or plan for, but consequences nonetheless."

I didn't say what I was thinking—that the chase, with all its absurdity and frustration and expense, had already given him what the portrait was supposed to deliver. Recognition. Reputation. Connection. A place in the circles he'd longed to enter, not as someone buying his way in but as someone who belonged there, who was spoken of favorably by artists and governors and perhaps even the Pope himself.

He'd won without realizing it. Won by losing. Succeeded by failing to achieve his stated goal.

The portrait had been the excuse, but the chase itself was the prize.

And I wasn't about to rob him of the one thing he hadn't quite admitted to himself yet—that maybe, just maybe, Leonardo had given him something more valuable than a painting.

Even if he'd never meant to. Even if it had all been accidental. Even if Francesco had paid for one thing and received something entirely different.

We sat together in the darkness, my hand in his, and let the realization settle between us unspoken.

Sometimes the things you chase give you exactly what you need, just not in the form you expected.

And sometimes a wife's job is to help her husband see that without making him feel foolish for having chased in the first place.

"Come to bed," I said finally, standing and pulling gently at his hand. "Tomorrow the children want to hear about your meeting with the Pope. Piero has been practicing his Latin specifically so he can ask you what His Holiness sounded like."

Francesco stood, allowing himself to be led. "I should probably make it sound more impressive than it was."

"You met the Pope," I said firmly. "It was impressive. The circumstances were absurd, but the fact itself is impressive."

"Leonardo used me as a human shield."

"Leonardo showed you to the Pope," I corrected. "Presented you. Introduced you as a distinguished merchant and patron. There are worse things to be used for."

He was quiet for a moment, then: "You're trying to make me feel better about being made a fool."

"I'm trying to help you see that you weren't," I said. "That's different."

We climbed into bed, and I felt him settle beside me, still thinking, still processing, but calmer now. Less raw.

"Lisa?" he said after a moment.

"Mmm?"

"Thank you."

"For what?"

"For not saying 'I told you so.'"

"About what specifically? The portrait? The journeys? Leonardo's inability to finish anything? Raphael being a better choice? There are so many things I could say it about."

His laugh was soft but genuine. "All of them. Any of them."

I reached over and patted his hand in the darkness. "I'm saving them up. For when you're really insufferable about something and I need ammunition."

"That's my practical wife."

"Someone in this marriage has to be," I said, but I was smiling.

And sometime later, when his breathing had evened out into sleep, I lay awake thinking about the strange paths lives take. About how the things we chase shape us more than the things we catch. About how my merchant husband had become something else—something neither of us had quite expected—while chasing a portrait that would probably never hang on our walls.

About how that might not be entirely bad.

About how Leonardo da Vinci, chronic procrastinator and genius, had accidentally given Francesco something more lasting than paint on wood: a story. An identity. A sense of being connected to something larger than buying and selling silk.

Even if he'd never meant to.

Even if Francesco still couldn't quite see it.

Even if the portrait remained unfinished, wherever it was, waiting for "someday" to finally arrive.
