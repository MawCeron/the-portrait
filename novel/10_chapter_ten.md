## **Chapter 10: The Conversation**

Francesco told me the story three times before I understood the shape of it.

The first time, he recounted it breathlessly—half thrilled, half horrified—as though he’d just outrun a stampede. The second time, he added details he’d forgotten because he’d been too busy being stunned by the Pope’s rings. By the third time, he spoke in a dull, almost embarrassed tone, rubbing at his forehead as though the memory itself gave him a headache.

Only when he finally fell silent did I reach for his hand.

“It’s been ten years,” I said, as gently as I could. “Ten years of traveling, of expenses, of time away from home, away from the business, away from—”

He let out a soft exhale. “From you.”

He said it not dramatically, not in apology, just… truthfully. And something in him sagged a little, like he’d dropped a weight he’d been holding too tightly.

I brushed my thumb over his knuckles. “Raphael is still in Rome. You could commission him. He finishes things. People say he’s fast.”

Francesco shook his head before I could finish the thought. “It wouldn’t be the same.”

“No,” I agreed. “It would be finished.”

He winced—only slightly, but I caught it. There was a whole world trapped in that silence that followed: his pride, his longing, his confusion, the strange joy he found in these impossible pursuits. He couldn’t explain it. I don’t think he even understood it.

And pushing him wouldn’t change a thing.

So I let the moment soften. “Well,” I said, trying to lighten the air, “at least you can say you’ve met the Pope.”

That finally earned a small smile. Not a triumphant one—more the exhausted, bewildered kind one has after being led into the Apostolic Palace under false pretenses. “He asked if we were neighbors,” Francesco muttered. “As if I lived down the street from the Medici.”

I squeezed his hand. “Speaking of the Medici,” I said, keeping my tone casual, “they’ve sent more orders. More than before. It seems your name was mentioned favorably.”

His eyes lifted, startled. “From Leonardo?”

“From someone,” I said. “And whoever it was, they spoke well of you.”

I didn’t say what I was thinking—that the chase, with all its absurdity and frustration, had already given him what the portrait was supposed to: recognition, connection, a place in the circles he longed to enter. He’d won without realizing it.

And I wasn’t about to rob him of the one thing he hadn’t admitted yet.

The portrait wasn’t the prize anymore.

But the chase?
Ah, that was something he couldn’t let go.
