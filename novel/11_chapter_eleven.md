# CHAPTER 11 – The Rumors

## 1514–1517 – Florence

In the years that followed the papal incident—which Francesco recounted so often and with such elaborate detail that our children could recite whole sections of it in perfect unison, complete with his dramatic pauses and hand gestures—our pursuit of Leonardo slowed.

But it never truly stopped.

Age does that to a man. Not wisdom, mind you; simply age. The body begins its quiet rebellion. Knees stiffen and protest on cold mornings. Backs complain about positions that once caused no trouble. Sleep becomes more elusive, and when it comes, less restorative. Suddenly a journey that once felt like an exciting adventure, a chance to see new cities and chase possibility, feels instead like a personal negotiation with one's own joints and stamina.

Even Francesco—who had chased Leonardo across Italy with the single-minded determination of a hound after a hare, who had once traveled to Milan on impulse with barely a day's preparation—began spacing out his expeditions. A year would pass, then two. Sometimes three.

His hair had gone more silver than dark. He moved more carefully in the mornings, though he tried to hide it. He needed more time to recover from journeys, would spend days after returning home doing little but sitting in the courtyard, claiming he was "thinking about business" when really he was just letting his body remember what rest felt like.

But he never stopped _listening_.

Every time a merchant from Rome passed through Florence on his way north, Francesco would hover a little too close to the shop conversations, pretending to browse bolts of wool or examine new dye samples while actually eavesdropping shamelessly on gossip about who was where and doing what. Every time a letter arrived from one of our family contacts near Milan or Venice or Bologna, he'd tear it open on the spot—right there in the street if the messenger caught him outside—just in case it contained news. Even rumors from apprentices stopping by our shop to sell sketches or supplies were treated like papal decrees, listened to with serious attention, filed away in memory for later consideration.

He had become a collector of Leonardo sightings the way some men collect coins or religious relics.

One evening, over supper—this would have been late 1514, I think, or early 1515—a silk trader from Bologna mentioned, offhand, the way someone might mention bad weather or rising bread prices, that Leonardo da Vinci had moved back to Rome. Again. Something about working for Giuliano de' Medici this time, the Pope's brother, though everyone knew that relationship was complicated.

Francesco went completely still, his fork halfway to his mouth.

Our eldest daughter, Maria, glanced at me with barely suppressed amusement. She knew what was coming.

"Rome?" Francesco said carefully, as if testing the word. "Recently?"

"A few months past, I heard. Designing something. Or dissecting something. You know how he is." The trader shrugged, already moving on to discuss shipping routes through the Romagna.

But Francesco had stopped listening to anything else. I could see it in his eyes—that particular light that meant his mind had already left Florence and was walking Roman streets, calculating distances and expenses and possibilities.

The next day he was lighter on his feet, humming during breakfast—actual humming, Lisa—smiling at customers with genuine warmth rather than practiced courtesy. He did not travel. Not then, not immediately. He was old enough now to know better than to rush off without proper planning, and besides, winter was coming and the mountain passes would soon be treacherous.

But the spark was back. Like a candle reigniting on a draft, suddenly bright again after seeming nearly extinguished.

He began making casual inquiries about road conditions to Rome. Asked returning travelers about lodging, about which routes were safest, about whether the winter in Rome was mild enough for an older man to manage comfortably. All very casual. Very theoretical. Just in case he ever had reason to travel south for, say, business purposes.

I said nothing. Just watched him plan a journey he might or might not ever take to see a man who might or might not remember him to retrieve a portrait that might or might not still exist.

Then, perhaps two years later—1516 by my best reckoning, because it was shortly after Maria's wedding—word drifted into Florence from a well-traveled banker, the kind of man who knew everyone's business before they knew it themselves.

Leonardo da Vinci had left Italy altogether.

Gone to France. Accepted an invitation from King Francis I himself. Crossed the Alps—at his age, mind you, in his sixties—carrying his most precious possessions with him. The gossip flowed through Florence like wine at a wedding, getting more elaborate with each retelling.

"The French king adores him," someone claimed at the market. "Treats him like visiting royalty."

"He gave him a manor house," another merchant insisted over cards. "A proper château near the royal residence at Amboise. Leonardo is apparently calling it Clos Lucé."

"He lets him do whatever he pleases," a third person whispered conspiratorially at Mass, as if this were the most scandalous part. "No commissions. No deadlines. Just... freedom to create whatever comes to mind. Can you imagine? A king paying for that?"

And that last part, of course, was what most impressed my husband. Not the manor house, not even the royal patronage. But the freedom. The idea that someone, finally, had given Leonardo exactly what he needed: unlimited time and no pressure to finish anything.

Francesco tried to appear unmoved by this news. Truly, he did his best. When people mentioned it, he'd fold his hands like a contemplative monk and nod with the carefully neutral expression of someone entirely unaffected by the fact that the man he'd been intermittently chasing for nearly fifteen years had crossed an international border and settled in a country Francesco had never visited.

"France," he'd say mildly. "How interesting. Well, I hope the climate suits him."

Very convincing, my husband. Absolutely no one could tell it bothered you.

But that night he lay awake beside me, staring at the ceiling in the darkness. I knew because I lay awake too, listening to his breathing, to the small sounds he made when he thought I was sleeping.

Long, thoughtful sighs. The kind that mean a man is quietly arguing with himself about something, weighing options, conducting internal debates about what's reasonable and what's merely possible.

I didn't ask what he was thinking. I didn't need to.

France. Leonardo was in France now. Not a week's journey away, or two weeks. Not even three. France was far—weeks of travel each direction, through territory Francesco didn't know, speaking languages he barely managed, at an age when such journeys stopped being adventures and started being genuinely dangerous.

France was, for all practical purposes, unreachable.

Unless a man was very determined. Unless a man was willing to risk considerable discomfort and expense. Unless a man couldn't quite let go of something he'd been chasing for half his life.

By 1517, our business had grown beyond anything we'd imagined in those early years when Francesco first commissioned the portrait. The Medici family favored us for ceremonial fabrics and special orders. Foreign merchants from as far as Constantinople sought us out specifically, asking for "the del Giocondo quality." Even nobles—the kind who would have looked right through Francesco in our early years, who wouldn't have acknowledged a silk merchant at a dinner party—now invited him to banquets and asked his opinion on matters of cloth and color, on aesthetics and design.

If you had told Francesco fifteen years earlier, back when he first came home glowing with the news that Leonardo had accepted his commission, that he would eventually become such a respected figure in Florence's cultural circles, he would have assumed it was because of the portrait. The beautiful, prestigious portrait by the greatest artist alive, hanging in his hall for all to see, proof of his taste and judgment.

Instead, it had come from something else entirely. From the years he'd spent in Leonardo's orbit, however peripherally. From the stories he could tell, the people he'd met, the ideas he'd been exposed to. From being able to say, with complete honesty, "When I visited the Maestro in Milan..." or "His Holiness and I once discussed..."

The chase itself had given him what the portrait was supposed to provide.

But the portrait still hovered over him like a half-remembered promise. Not painful anymore, not urgent or frustrating the way it had been in those early years. Just... unfinished. A thread dangling from an otherwise complete garment that he couldn't quite bring himself to cut.

He began telling Leonardo stories at dinners. Not intentionally, not as planned performances, but casually, organically, as though dropping references to acquaintances everyone was expected to know.

Someone would mention a bridge design, and Francesco would say, "Oh yes, the Maestro once showed me sketches for a single-span bridge that could cross the Golden Horn. The mathematics were extraordinary."

A guest would admire our fountain, and he'd explain, "When I visited him in Milan—no, the second time, or possibly the third—he explained how the Roman aqueducts managed water pressure over such distances. Fascinating principles."

Or my personal favorite, deployed whenever someone mentioned Rome or the Church: "His Holiness Pope Leo is quite charming in person, you know. Very down-to-earth for a Medici. We spoke for twenty minutes about Florentine wool production."

He never mentioned that those twenty minutes had occurred because he'd been essentially held hostage by protocol while Leonardo escaped. That part didn't make it into the dinner party version.

People listened—sometimes politely, sometimes with genuine interest. His tales had become a kind of soft social currency, traded at gatherings. And Francesco had what seemed to be a limitless supply. Fifteen years of periodic visits, of conversations and observations and those moments when genius had briefly turned its attention toward him.

He'd become, in a strange way, a Leonardo scholar. An amateur biographer. The merchant who knew the artist, who could speak with authority about his process, his thinking, his impossible standards.

It suited him. Made him interesting in a way that simply being wealthy never could.

One night, after a dinner party where he'd been particularly animated—telling the story about the optical experiment that nearly burned down a palazzo, complete with sound effects for the cardinal's shrieking—he turned to me in our carriage with a faint, almost shy smile.

The streetlamps of Florence rolled past, casting moving shadows across his face. He looked older in that light, but also somehow more himself than he'd been at forty.

"Do you think," he asked softly, almost hesitantly, as if afraid of my answer, "that I am too old to attempt the journey to France?"

I looked at him—really looked. At the silver in his hair and the lines around his eyes. At the way he held himself more carefully now, conscious of his body's limitations. At the hands resting on his knees, merchant's hands grown older but no less capable.

But also at the light in his eyes. Still there. Still bright. Still curious about what might be possible.

I rested my hand on his, feeling the familiar warmth, the landscape of veins and tendons I knew as well as my own. Feeling the history of our life together in that simple touch.

"You'll know when the time is right," I said gently. "If it ever is."

He nodded slowly. He didn't argue, didn't insist, didn't immediately begin planning the journey the way younger Francesco would have. He only looked out the carriage window, watching Florence roll past in the lamplight—our Florence, the city we'd built a life in, the streets we'd walked for decades.

And I saw it clearly for the first time, understood it completely in a way I'd been approaching slowly for years:

My husband had become a man defined not by the portrait he never received...

But by the pursuit of the man who never finished it.

And for better or worse—for richer or poorer, in completion or in endless waiting—that pursuit had shaped him more profoundly than any painting hanging on any wall ever could.

It had made him curious where he'd been merely ambitious. Had made him interesting where he'd been simply wealthy. Had given him stories that would outlast any business success, connections that transcended mere commerce, a sense of having brushed against something extraordinary even if he'd never quite grasped it.

The portrait was unfinished.

But Francesco, in his own strange way, was complete.

And I loved him for both—for the merchant he'd been and for the devotee he'd become, for the practical man who balanced accounts and the impractical dreamer who still, at sixty, wondered if he might be able to make one more journey.

Even if that journey led to France.

Even if Leonardo had forgotten him entirely.

Even if the portrait no longer existed.

Some men chase fortunes. Some chase power. Some chase women or wine or glory.

My husband chased genius.

And somehow, in the chasing, found himself.
