## **Chapter 11: The Rumors**

In the years that followed the papal incident—which Francesco recounted so often that our children could recite whole sections of it in unison—our pursuit of Leonardo slowed… but never truly stopped.

Age does that. Not wisdom, mind you; simply age. Knees stiffen, backs complain, and suddenly a journey that once felt like an exciting commercial opportunity feels like a personal negotiation with one’s own joints. Even Francesco, who had chased Leonardo across Italy with the determination of a hound after a hare, began spacing out his expeditions. A year, then two. Sometimes three.

But he never stopped listening.

Every time a merchant from Rome passed through Florence, Francesco would hover a little too close, pretending to browse bolts of wool while eavesdropping on gossip. Every time a letter arrived from a family contact near Milan, he’d tear it open on the spot—just in case. Even rumors from apprentices stopping by to sell sketches or supplies were treated like papal decrees.

One evening, over supper, a traveler from Bologna mentioned—offhand, the way someone might mention bad weather—that Leonardo da Vinci had moved to Rome.
Francesco went still.

The next day, he was light on his feet, humming during breakfast, smiling at customers. He did not travel—not then—but the spark was back. Like a candle reigniting on a draft.

Then, a few years later, word drifted in from a banker: Leonardo had left Italy altogether. He’d gone to France, accepted by King Francis I. The gossip flowed through Florence like wine at a wedding. “The French king adores him,” someone claimed. “He gave him a manor house,” another insisted. “He lets him do whatever he pleases,” a third whispered, and that, of course, was the part that most impressed my husband.

Francesco tried to appear unmoved. Truly, he did. He folded his hands like a monk and nodded with the expression of someone entirely unaffected by the fact that the man he’d been chasing for a decade and a half had crossed a national border.

But that night he lay awake, staring at the ceiling.

I knew because I lay awake too, listening to his sighs—long, thoughtful ones, the kind that mean a man is quietly arguing with himself.

By then our business had grown handsomely. The Medici favored us. Foreign merchants sought us out. Even nobles—ones who would have ignored Francesco in our early years—invited him to banquets and asked his opinion on cloth and color. If you had told him fifteen years earlier that he would become a respected figure in Florence, he would have believed the credit would go to the portrait he had proudly announced was underway.

Instead, it came from the years he had spent chasing a genius instead of courting clients.

But the portrait still hovered over him like a half-remembered promise. Not painful, not urgent—just... unfinished. A thread dangling from a garment he couldn’t bring himself to cut.

He began telling Leonardo stories at dinners. Not intentionally, but casually, as though dropping references to acquaintances everyone was expected to know.

“Oh yes, the Maestro once showed me a sketch of that device,” he’d say.
Or: “When I visited him in Milan—no, the second time—he explained how the arches distribute weight.”
Or my personal favorite: “His Holiness is quite charming in person, you know. We spoke for twenty minutes.”

People listened politely—or sometimes eagerly. His tales had become a kind of soft currency, traded at gatherings. And Francesco had a limitless supply.

One night, after a dinner where he’d been particularly animated, he turned to me in the carriage with a faint smile.

“Do you think,” he asked, softly, “that I am too old to attempt the journey to France?”

I rested my hand on his, feeling the lines age had carved across both our lives.

“You’ll know when the time is right,” I said. “If it ever is.”

He nodded. He didn’t argue. He didn’t insist. He only looked out the window, watching Florence roll past, and I saw it clearly for the first time:

My husband had become a man defined not by the portrait he never received…
but by the pursuit of the man who never finished it.

And for better or worse, that pursuit had shaped him more than any painting ever could.
