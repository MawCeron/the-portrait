## **Chapter 12: The Quiet Years**

By the time the rumors settled into certainty—_Leonardo da Vinci is in France now, living like a prince at the king’s side_—my husband no longer leapt to pack a bag.

For years, any whisper of the Maestro’s whereabouts sent Francesco into a frenzy of preparations: counting coins, arranging travel, leaving instructions for the shop, promising he’d be back “in just a few days” (which was never true). But this time… nothing.

He heard the news, nodded slowly, and then asked our daughter to pass him the olives.

At first, I thought he hadn’t understood. That maybe he’d missed the part about Leonardo living in a château, dining with a king who adored him, working—or not working—with the same leisurely inconsistency he’d shown the rest of the world.

But no. Francesco understood perfectly.

He just didn’t move.

Life still moved around us, of course. Our sons stepped more firmly into the business; our daughter was nearly married. The Medici contracts kept coming. Florence bustled along its usual, unpredictable rhythms. And my husband… he softened.

That’s what it felt like—softening, not fading. Francesco had always been driven by some inner motor that never fully turned off. When we were first married, that motor was tuned to ambition. For the last ten years, it had hummed to the rhythm of “Where is Leonardo now?”

But during those quiet years, the motor sputtered. Slowed. Sometimes it stopped entirely.

There were days when he seemed almost at peace with it all. He would sit in our courtyard, wine in hand, watching the afternoon settle over the tiled roofs with a half-smile. He talked more with our children, laughed more easily. He even slept later, which in our marriage was unheard of.

Yet sometimes I’d catch him in a moment he didn’t mean to show. A pause over dinner. A distant look when sorting contracts. Or that odd habit he developed of staring at the workshop wall—as if half-expecting a portrait that never came to materialize through sheer will.

And every time he drifted like that, I knew exactly where his thoughts had gone.

France.

To the Maestro who had never finished our commission, who had slipped through my husband’s fingers for more than a decade, who now lived an entire kingdom away. And still… Francesco didn’t go.

Was it age? Probably. He was no longer the eager man who’d sprint across half of Italy on a rumor. His knees cracked. His back stiffened. His beard came in whiter each season.

Was it cost? Maybe. Leonardo served a king now; there was no pretending the chase was a merchant’s errand anymore. It would be a pilgrimage.

But I also wondered—quietly, to myself, never aloud—whether something in him had shifted. Whether he had finally realized that reaching Leonardo would only break the magic spell the chase had cast over him.

If he went to France and Leonardo greeted him with that familiar distracted warmth, promising yet again to find the portrait “very soon,” would Francesco be able to bear it? And if Leonardo truly had no idea where the portrait was—lost in some trunk, smeared under a reworked canvas, traded for a curiosity he found more interesting—would my husband survive knowing the quest had never been leading anywhere at all?

So he stayed. And told himself practical stories about why.

“It’s too far,” he’d say.

Or, “The business needs me.”

Or, “Kings don’t like people arriving uninvited.”

But at night, when he thought I’d fallen asleep, I’d feel him shift beside me. Restless. Quiet. His breath catching slightly, the way it used to when he talked about Leonardo’s workshop, or the Battle of Anghiari, or the time he was dragged—accidentally, he swore—into the papal chambers.

He never said it, but I knew.

He wasn’t done with the chase. He was simply… resting from it.

And maybe—just maybe—preparing himself for the moment when rest would no longer feel like enough.
