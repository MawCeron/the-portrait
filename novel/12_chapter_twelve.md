# CHAPTER 12 – The Quiet Years

## 1517–1519 – Florence

By the time the rumors settled into certainty—Leonardo da Vinci is in France now, established there, living like an honored guest at the king's pleasure—my husband no longer leapt to pack a bag.

This surprised me more than perhaps it should have.

For fifteen years, any whisper of the Maestro's whereabouts had sent Francesco into an immediate frenzy of preparations: counting coins, sorting them into travel pouches, arranging coverage for the shop, leaving detailed instructions with Marco that were invariably ignored, promising me and the children he'd be back "in just a few days, a week at most" (which was never, ever true). The pattern had been so reliable I could have predicted his movements like the phases of the moon.

But this time... nothing.

He heard the news—from multiple sources, over the course of weeks, each adding new details about the château at Clos Lucé, about King Francis's devotion to his Italian genius, about Leonardo dining at the royal table and being given complete freedom to work or not work as he pleased—and he simply nodded slowly, absorbed the information with the thoughtful expression of someone filing away an interesting fact.

And then asked our daughter to pass him the olives.

At first, I thought perhaps he hadn't understood. That maybe he'd missed the part about Leonardo living in what amounted to a small palace, dining regularly with a king who reportedly adored him and treated him like visiting royalty, working—or more likely not working—with the same leisurely inconsistency he'd shown to every other patron in his long, distinguished, frustratingly incomplete career.

But no. Francesco understood perfectly. I could see it in his eyes, in the way his hand tightened briefly around his wine cup before relaxing again.

He just didn't move.

Didn't start planning. Didn't begin the familiar rituals of preparation. Didn't even mention the possibility of travel.

Life still moved around us, of course, the way it always does regardless of private revolutions or internal reckonings. Our sons—young men now, not boys—stepped more firmly into the business, learning not just the technical aspects of trade but the relationships, the judgments, the subtle negotiations that separated successful merchants from merely competent ones. Our daughter Camilla was betrothed to a good family, planning a wedding that seemed to consume half our household's attention and most of our spare coin.

The Medici contracts kept coming, steady and lucrative. Florence bustled along its usual unpredictable rhythms—political intrigues and artistic scandals, new buildings rising while old families fell, the endless dance of commerce and culture that made our city what it was.

And my husband... he softened.

That's what it felt like—softening, not fading. Not diminishing or retreating or giving up. Something gentler than that. More like clay that had been fired hard becoming pliant again, or metal that had been forged finding its natural state.

Francesco had always been driven by some inner motor that never fully turned off. When we were first married, twenty-five years ago now, that motor had been tuned to pure ambition—the need to rise, to be more, to prove himself worthy of notice beyond his merchant status. For the last fifteen years, it had hummed to the single-note rhythm of "Where is Leonardo now?" and "How can I reach him?"

But during those quiet years between 1517 and 1519, the motor sputtered. Slowed. Sometimes it stopped entirely, and Francesco would simply exist in the present moment rather than always leaning forward into some imagined future.

There were days—whole beautiful days—when he seemed almost at peace with it all.

He would sit in our courtyard in the late afternoon, wine in hand, watching the sunlight shift across the tiled roofs of Florence with a half-smile that asked nothing and expected nothing. Just present. Just there. He talked more with our children during those months, really talked rather than half-listening while his mind was elsewhere. He laughed more easily at dinner, at the small absurdities of daily life. He even slept later in the mornings, which in all our years of marriage had been virtually unheard of—Francesco had always been the first awake, already mentally sorting through the day's obligations before his feet touched the floor.

But now? Now he sometimes dozed until I woke him gently, and he'd smile at me with a contentment I'd rarely seen in him.

Those were good days. Days when I thought perhaps the chase was finally over, that he'd found whatever he'd been seeking not in the pursuit but in the letting go.

Yet there were other days. Harder days.

Days when I'd catch him in a moment he didn't mean to show, when his careful contentment slipped and revealed what lay beneath. A pause over dinner, his fork suspended while his mind traveled elsewhere. A distant look when sorting through contracts, as if the numbers and names on the pages had suddenly become meaningless symbols. Or that odd new habit he developed of staring at the wall in his study—the wall where, presumably, he'd once imagined the portrait would hang—as if half-expecting it to materialize through sheer force of will after all these years.

And every time he drifted like that, pulled away from the present into some interior landscape I couldn't quite access, I knew exactly where his thoughts had gone.

France.

To the Maestro who had never finished our commission. Who had slipped through my husband's fingers for more than fifteen years, always just out of reach, always "soon," always somewhere else. Who now lived not just a few weeks' journey away but across an entire kingdom, speaking a language Francesco barely knew, in a place he'd never visited, surrounded by people he'd never met.

And still... Francesco didn't go.

I tried to understand why. Tried to read the reasons in his silences, in the things he didn't say as much as the things he did.

Was it age? Almost certainly that was part of it. He was past sixty now—sixty-one, or perhaps sixty-two, he'd never been entirely sure of his exact birth date. He was no longer the eager man in his forties who could spring across half of Italy on the strength of a rumor and a hope. His knees cracked audibly when he stood. His back stiffened if he sat too long in one position. His beard came in whiter with each passing season, and some mornings he needed time to work the ache out of his joints before he could move normally.

Was it cost? That seemed plausible too. France wasn't like traveling to Rome or Milan—cities we had contacts in, routes we knew, currencies we understood. France was foreign in every sense, and Leonardo served a king now. There was no pretending this chase was still a reasonable merchant's errand, a simple matter of checking on a commissioned work. It would be a pilgrimage. An expensive, difficult, possibly dangerous pilgrimage to a foreign court in search of a man who might not even remember him.

But I also wondered—quietly, to myself, never aloud because I knew better than to give voice to things Francesco might not be ready to hear—whether something deeper in him had shifted. Whether he had finally, after all these years, realized that actually reaching Leonardo might break the spell entirely.

The chase had given him so much. Purpose. Identity. Stories that made him interesting, that gave him currency in circles that would otherwise have remained closed to a mere merchant. Connections to genius, to greatness, to something larger than the daily business of buying and selling silk.

But if he went to France? If he made that enormous effort, traveled those hundreds of miles, arrived at Clos Lucé and knocked on the door?

If Leonardo greeted him with that familiar distracted warmth, smiling vaguely, promising yet again to find the portrait "very soon, I'm certain it's here somewhere," would Francesco be able to bear it? Would he be able to smile and accept and travel home again knowing nothing had changed, nothing would ever change?

And if Leonardo truly had no idea where the portrait was—lost in some forgotten trunk, painted over for a study he found more interesting, traded away for a curiosity that caught his fancy, or simply left behind in some previous workshop and long since destroyed or disappeared—would my husband survive knowing that the quest had never been leading anywhere at all? That there was no treasure at the end of this particular adventure, just another empty room and another set of promises?

Sometimes I thought that was what kept him in Florence. Not age, not cost, but a kind of protective instinct. As long as he didn't go, as long as he didn't know for certain, the hope could remain alive. The possibility. The someday that never arrived but also never quite disappeared.

So he stayed in Florence. And told himself practical stories about why.

"It's too far," he'd say when someone asked if he'd heard Leonardo was in France. "At my age, such a journey would be folly."

Or: "The business needs me here. The boys are capable, but they're still learning. I can't simply vanish for months."

Or, with a knowing smile: "Kings don't like people arriving uninvited to their courts. I'm a merchant, not an ambassador. I'd probably be turned away at the border."

All perfectly reasonable explanations. All potentially true, even.

And yet.

At night, when he thought I'd fallen asleep beside him—when he assumed my breathing had evened out into the steady rhythm of rest—I'd feel him shift. Restless. Careful not to wake me but unable to settle. His breath catching slightly, the way it used to when he talked about Leonardo's workshop in those early days, or described the Battle of Anghiari sketches, or recounted the time he was dragged—accidentally, he always swore, though I had my doubts—into the papal chambers.

He never said anything. Never voiced whatever thoughts chased through his mind in those dark hours. But I knew him. Had known him for a quarter century. Could read his silences as clearly as other people read books.

He wasn't done with the chase.

He was simply... resting from it. Gathering strength, perhaps. Or gathering courage. Or waiting for some sign that the time was right, that the journey would be worth making, that there was still something to be found at the end of all this searching.

And maybe—just maybe—preparing himself for the moment when rest would no longer feel like enough. When the not-knowing would become worse than the knowing, even if what he learned was disappointing. When the pull of that unfinished thing, that dangling thread, would become too strong to resist any longer.

I watched him during those quiet years, and I waited.

Because I knew my husband.

And I knew this wasn't over.

Not yet.
