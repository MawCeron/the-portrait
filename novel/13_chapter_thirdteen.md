# CHAPTER 13 – The Final Journey

## Early 1519 – Florence to France

When the news reached us—filtered through the usual channels of traveling merchants and well-connected bankers, each adding their own details and embellishments—that Leonardo was still in France, still living at the Château du Clos Lucé near Amboise, still comfortable under the generous patronage of King Francis, I saw something change in my husband.

Not the old spark, not exactly. That had been brighter, almost boyish, burning with the particular intensity of a man who still believed he could retrieve something, or earn something, or prove something through sheer determination and persistence. This was different. Quieter. A deeper pull, steady and heavy as gravity, like the tide deciding after a long contemplation that it simply must return to shore, regardless of what stands in its way.

Francesco was in his sixties now, though he'd never admit the exact number. He'd grown vague about his age, the way older people do when the counting stops bringing satisfaction and starts bringing only uncomfortable arithmetic. A merchant's work keeps a man's back straight longer than most professions—all that standing, that reaching for bolts of fabric on high shelves, that constant movement through shop and warehouse—but age settles itself in other places first.

In the eyes, mostly. And Francesco's eyes... they'd begun to look past things more than at them. Through them, sometimes. As if the present moment was just something to see beyond, toward some other time or place.

Still, one morning in late winter—February, I think, or early March, when the worst of the cold had passed but spring hadn't quite committed itself—he rose from bed with a purpose I hadn't seen in years. Not the frantic energy of his younger quests, but something more deliberate. More final.

He laid out his best traveling clothes across our bed with careful attention. Not the newest ones—we could afford new clothes for such an important journey—but the ones he'd worn on earlier chases. The doublet he'd had made before his second trip to Milan. The cloak that had seen Rome. The boots that had walked through half of Italy. The ones with stories stitched into their wrinkles and creases, with wear patterns that told the history of his pursuit.

He moved methodically, checking everything, preparing his traveling case, counting coins with the practiced efficiency of a man who'd done this many times before.

He didn't make a speech. Didn't call me in to witness some grand declaration. Didn't justify the expense or argue the logic, as he used to do when he sensed I might resist, when he felt he needed to convince me—or perhaps himself—that this journey was reasonable, necessary, worth the cost.

He simply stood in the doorway of our bedchamber, holding his boots under his arm, already dressed for travel except for those final touches, and looked at me with quiet determination.

"I'm going to France," he said.

Just like that. Simple. Direct. No elaboration.

I was sitting at my dressing table, pinning up my hair—a morning ritual so familiar I could do it without thinking. But my hands stopped mid-motion at his words, a pin suspended between my fingers.

I stared at him for a long moment, studying his face in the mirror's reflection. I needed to understand what he really meant by those four words, what he was actually telling me beneath the simple declaration. After sixteen years of chasing, and another three years of half-chasing, half-pretending not to, the words carried weight that had nothing to do with geography.

"Why now?" I asked, keeping my voice soft, not challenging. Just asking.

He hesitated—the smallest flinch, a tiny contraction around his eyes. The truth was too big, too tangled and complex for him to push out all at once, to lay before me in its entirety. But he managed pieces of it, fragments that might assemble into something coherent if I held them right.

"He's been there three years," Francesco said, leaning against the doorframe as if he needed its support. "Longer than he stayed anywhere else in a long time. He's settled there. Comfortable. The king gives him everything he needs. If he still has the portrait..." He paused, took a breath. "He might finally be willing to let it go. To finish it properly, perhaps, and deliver it. Or at least to tell me honestly what became of it."

I almost laughed—not from mockery or cruelty, but from tenderness. From loving recognition of who this man was, even now, even after everything. After all these years, all these disappointments, all these promises that dissolved like morning mist, my husband still believed there were rules to Leonardo's behavior. Logic. Patterns. Bargains that could be struck and kept. Closure that could be achieved if only one approached the situation correctly.

"And if he doesn't have it?" I asked gently, turning to face him properly instead of speaking to his reflection. "If it's lost, or destroyed, or he's painted over it, or simply has no idea where it is after all this time?"

Another hesitation. This one longer, heavier. His jaw worked as if he were chewing words that didn't want to be spoken.

Then, very quietly, so quietly I almost didn't hear it: "Then I want to see him. One last time."

That.

_That_ was the truth.

Simple, unvarnished, impossible to argue with or reason away. Not about the portrait at all, really. About the man. About standing in the same room as Leonardo da Vinci once more before it was too late, before age or death or distance made it permanently impossible.

I felt it settle over us like dust—soft, undeniable, coating everything.

"Francesco..." I began, but stopped. What could I say? What argument could I make against such raw, honest longing?

Traveling to France from Florence is no small undertaking, even for the healthy and young. The journey north through the mountains, through territories where we had no contacts or connections, where the language changed and the customs were foreign. Weeks on the road, at minimum. Considerable expense. Real danger from bandits, accidents, simple misfortune.

And my Francesco—he was still strong, still capable in many ways, but he was no longer young. No longer the man who could bounce back from hard travel with just a day's rest. The miles had grown longer for him these last years. His body took longer to recover. He tired more easily, though he tried to hide it.

But there was no stopping him now. Whatever had been brewing in him—nostalgia, longing, unfinished business, the need for some kind of ending—it had reached a critical point where staying in Florence would hurt more than going. Where the not-knowing had become more painful than whatever truth waited in France.

I worried, of course. More than I wanted to show, more than would be helpful to voice. But I told him some of it anyway, because not saying anything felt like a betrayal of our long marriage, of the honesty we'd built between us.

"It's far," I said, crossing the room to take his hands in mine. They felt older than I remembered, though I touched them every day. When had these age spots appeared? When had the skin grown so thin and delicate? "The roads are bad this time of year. Mountain passes are still dangerous. It's expensive—not that we can't afford it, but it's not nothing. And you..." I trailed off, not wanting to say the words.

"I'll be fine," he said, too quickly. Which meant he wouldn't necessarily be fine, but would go anyway. Would accept whatever consequences came because the alternative—staying, wondering, never knowing—had become unacceptable.

I reached up and held his face between my hands, feeling the familiar contours I'd touched for nearly thirty years. The bones more prominent now, the flesh less pliant. My thumb brushed across a deep line near his eye—one I didn't remember being quite so pronounced the year before, or perhaps I'd just stopped noticing the gradual changes that accumulated into transformation.

Age sneaks in like a thief at night—quiet, efficient, taking small things so regularly that you don't notice the loss until you realize the room is nearly empty.

"When will you leave?" I asked, because there was no point in trying to stop him. We were long past that. This was happening, and my role now was simply to love him through it, whatever "it" turned out to be.

"As soon as I can arrange everything. A few days to settle affairs with the shop, make sure the boys know what to handle while I'm gone. A week at most."

"And how long will you be away?"

He met my eyes, and I saw the truth there even before he spoke it. "I don't know, Lisa. Two months? Three? However long it takes."

However long it takes to do what, I wondered. To find the portrait? To see Leonardo? To say goodbye to this thing that had defined so much of his adult life?

But I didn't ask. Some questions are better left unvoiced.

---

When he finally set out—boots laced tight, cloak clasped at his shoulder, traveling bag packed and repacked until he was satisfied, purse heavy enough for the long journey but light enough to worry me—he kissed my hand in farewell.

We stood in our courtyard, early morning light just beginning to warm the stones. Our children had come to see him off—grown now, with lives and concerns of their own, but still our children. Still worried about their father making this impossible journey at his age.

"I'll bring it home this time," Francesco said, and for a moment I wasn't sure if he meant himself or the portrait. Perhaps both. "I promise, Lisa. This time, I'll bring it home."

I didn't argue. Didn't repeat the truths we both knew—that he'd made this promise before, that Leonardo had proven unreliable at best, that the portrait had become less a real object than a ghost story we told each other, something always glimpsed in the distance but never caught, never held, never quite real.

Instead, I asked a different question: "What will you say to him, when you see him?"

He smiled a little at that. A tired smile, but genuine. Real. "I don't know. I've rehearsed half a dozen speeches on the way here—angry ones demanding what's mine by right, polite ones requesting accommodation, dignified ones about honor and obligation. But none of them sound right anymore. They're not... true. Maybe I'll just..." He shrugged, the gesture making him look younger for a moment, less burdened. "Talk to him. See where the conversation goes."

And that, more than anything else he might have said, told me what this journey really was. What it had always been, perhaps, underneath all the other justifications and excuses.

He wasn't going for the painting. Not really. Not anymore.

He was going for the man. For Leonardo. For one more conversation, one more glimpse of that extraordinary mind, one more moment of standing close enough to genius to feel its warmth.

The portrait was just the excuse that made the journey socially acceptable, that gave him something concrete to say when people asked why he was traveling to France at his age.

I reached up and kissed his cheek. "Go carefully. Come home safe."

"I will," he promised, though we both knew it was a promise he might not be able to keep. The road makes its own decisions about who returns and who doesn't.

I watched him mount his horse—we'd arranged for a guide and companion for the journey, someone younger who knew the routes—and ride out of our courtyard. Watched him turn at the corner to wave one last time before disappearing into the morning streets of Florence.

---

Later—much later, after everything that followed—he told me about those long days on the road. The speeches he practiced and discarded as he rode, testing words in the privacy of his own mind. The memories that surfaced unbidden: the first time he'd seen Leonardo work, really work, lost in concentration so complete that the world around him ceased to exist. The thrill of being near someone who made existence itself feel larger, more full of possibility.

He told me he imagined, during those endless hours of travel, walking back into our home with the portrait under his arm. The weight of it. The satisfaction of finally, _finally_ completing this quest that had consumed sixteen years of his life. The vindication.

But he also imagined—and this part he admitted only reluctantly, as if it were somehow shameful—simply sitting beside Leonardo again. Talking. Sharing a meal, perhaps, the way they had that evening in Milan so many years ago. Listening to whatever thoughts were currently occupying that restless, brilliant mind.

The way some men revisit old lovers not out of lingering desire, but out of longing for who they were when they first knew them. For the version of themselves that existed in that other person's presence.

He said he didn't know, during that journey, which mattered more. The portrait or the conversation. The object or the connection. The thing he'd paid for or the thing he'd found without meaning to.

I already knew. Had known for years.

But I let him discover it for himself.

He traveled north, mile after slow mile, through mountains and valleys, past villages whose names he never learned, across borders that existed more in custom than geography. Toward France. Toward Amboise. Toward the Château du Clos Lucé where Leonardo lived in comfortable retirement.

Toward the end of something neither of us yet understood, though we both felt its approach.

And I stayed behind in Florence, managing the household, overseeing the business, explaining to customers and neighbors and worried friends that yes, Signor del Giocondo had gone to France on important business, no, we didn't know exactly when he'd return, yes, we were confident everything would be fine.

Waiting. Hoping the road would be kind to him. Hoping his body would hold up under the strain. Hoping this final attempt—because I knew in my bones it was the final one, that there would be no more after this—would give him whatever he needed.

Whether he returned with the portrait or without it.

Whether he found Leonardo or didn't.

Whether he got answers or only more questions.

This final journey would define everything that followed.

The ending we'd been moving toward, unknowing, for sixteen years.

And all I could do was wait in Florence and hope that when he came home—if he came home, when he came home, I had to believe when—he would have found some kind of peace.

Whether with the portrait in his hands...

...or only with the truth in his heart.
