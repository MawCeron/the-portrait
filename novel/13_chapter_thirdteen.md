## **Chapter 13: The Final Journey**

When the news reached us—that Leonardo was still in France, living comfortably at the Château du Clos Lucé, under the warm wing of King Francis—I saw something change in my husband.

Not the old spark, not exactly. That had been brighter, almost boyish, back when he still believed he could _retrieve_ something, or _earn_ something, or _prove_ something. This was different. A quieter pull, steady and heavy, like the tide deciding it simply must return to shore.

He was in his fifties now, though he’d never admit it. A merchant’s work keeps a man’s back straight longer than most professions, but age settles itself in the eyes first, and Francesco’s eyes… they’d begun to look past things more than at them.

Still, one morning, he rose with a purpose I hadn’t seen in years—laid out his best traveling clothes, not the newest ones, but the ones he’d worn on earlier chases. The ones with stories stitched into their wrinkles.

He didn’t make a speech. He didn’t justify the expense or argue the logic, as he used to when he sensed I might resist. He simply stood in the doorway of our room, holding his boots under his arm, and said:

“I’m going to France.”

Just like that.

I stared at him for a long moment, because I needed to understand what he _really_ meant. After ten years of chasing, and another five of half-chasing, half-pretending not to, the words carried weight.

“Why now?” I asked, softly.

He hesitated—the smallest flinch. The truth was too big, too tangled for him to push out all at once. But he managed pieces of it.

“He’s been there three years,” he said. “Longer than anywhere in a long time. He’s settled. Comfortable. If he still has the portrait…” A breath. “He might finally be willing to let it go.”

I almost laughed—_not_ mockery, just tenderness. After everything, my husband still believed there were rules to Leonardo’s behavior. Reason. Bargains. Closure.

“And if he doesn’t?” I asked.

Another hesitation. A longer one.

Then, very quietly: “Then I want to see him. One last time.”

That… that was the truth. Simple, unvarnished, impossible to argue with. I felt it settle over us like dust, soft and undeniable.

Traveling to France from Florence is no small thing, even for the healthy. And my Francesco—he was still strong, but he was no longer fearless. The miles had grown longer for him these last years. But there was no stopping him now. Whatever had been brewing in him—nostalgia, longing, unfinished business—it had reached a point where staying would hurt more than going.

I worried, of course. I told him so.

“It’s far,” I said, taking his hands. “The roads are bad. It’s expensive. And you—”

“I’ll be fine,” he said, too quickly. That meant he wouldn’t be fine, but would go anyway.

I held his cheek for a moment. My thumb brushed a line I didn’t remember being there the year before. Age sneaks in like a thief—quiet, efficient.

“When will you leave?” I asked.

“As soon as I can.”

And he did.

When he finally set out—boots strapped, cloak clasped, purse heavy enough for France but light enough to worry me—he kissed my hand.

“I’ll bring it home this time,” he said.

I didn’t argue, didn’t dissuade, didn’t repeat the truths we both knew. The portrait had become a ghost story between us—something always glimpsed, never caught.

Instead, I asked, “What will you say to him, when you see him?”

He smiled a little at that. A tired smile, but real.

“I don’t know. I’ve rehearsed half a dozen speeches—angry ones, polite ones, dignified ones… But none of them sound right. Maybe I’ll just…” A shrug. “Talk to him.”

And that, more than anything, told me what this journey really was.

He wasn’t going for the painting.

He was going for the man.

Later—much later—he told me what passed through his mind on those long, rattling days on the road: the speeches he practiced and discarded, the memories of the first time he’d seen Leonardo work, the thrill of being near someone who made the world feel larger.

He told me he imagined walking back into our home with the portrait under his arm, yes… but he also imagined simply sitting beside Leonardo again. The way some men revisit old lovers not out of desire, but out of longing for who they were when they first knew them.

He said he didn’t know which mattered more.

I already knew.

He traveled north, mile after mile, toward France… toward the end of something neither of us yet understood.

And I stayed behind in Florence, waiting—hoping the road would be kind to him, and knowing, deep down, that this final attempt would define everything that followed.

Whether he returned with the portrait…

…or only with the truth.
