## **Chapter 14: Too Late**

Francesco always insisted he arrived in France already half-victorious.

He told me this later, when the worst of the grief had dulled, when he could speak without breaking apart. He said the morning light on the Loire Valley made everything look like a promise. Soft hills, clean air, vineyards that looked as though angels tended them. He rode through all of it sitting straighter in the saddle than he had in years.

He believed he was close—closer than ever—to ending the chase on his terms.

“Imagine it, Lisa,” he told me. “I thought I would ride into Amboise, greet Leonardo like an old friend, settle the matter once and for all. I wasn’t nervous. Not this time. I was… ready.”

And I believe him. I truly do. I can picture him trotting down the road like a merchant-knight on a holy quest, rehearsing what he’d say, imagining the surprise on Leonardo’s face, the warmth, the laugh, the inevitable, “Ah, yes, your portrait! Let me fetch it.”

He reached the town just after late morning.

He stopped a baker to ask for directions to the Château du Clos Lucé. The man blinked at him strangely, as if Francesco had asked where to find the moon.

He approached a pair of women drawing water from a well. The same look—surprise, then something like pity.

Francesco didn’t understand. Not yet. He simply thought the French had odd habits of staring.

So he kept riding.

The path to the château wound gently uphill, between trees heavy with spring leaves. Birds were singing. The sun was warm. Francesco said it felt like walking toward a reunion—one he’d earned through fifteen years of stubborn faith.

He reached the gate, dismounted, brushed the dust from his coat. I imagine him straightening his posture, smoothing his hair, preparing to greet the greatest mind of their age as an equal.

A servant approached.

Francesco smiled, confident, almost triumphant.

“I’m here,” he said in polished French, “to see Maestro Leonardo da Vinci.”

The servant hesitated. Just a beat. But enough—for a man who’d chased brilliance half his life—to sense something slipping away.

“Pardon, monsieur…”
A pause.
A softening of the eyes.

And then:

“I’m sorry, monsieur. The Maestro died this morning.”

Francesco never tried to describe the moment he heard those words. He only said it felt like the ground had been pulled out from under him—silently, completely—leaving him standing in the air with nothing to hold on to.

He didn’t speak. He couldn’t.

The birds kept singing. The sun kept shining. The château windows glowed peacefully in the light.

And my husband—after fifteen years of chasing a man who never stayed still—arrived on the only day Leonardo would never move again.

Too late to speak.
Too late to be seen.
Too late to understand what he’d really been seeking.

He stood there, he told me, long after the servant had stepped aside. Just stood, staring at nothing, feeling—for the first time in his life—not ambition or frustration or excitement.

Just emptiness.
