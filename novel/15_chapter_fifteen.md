# CHAPTER 15 – The Portrait, At Last

## May 2, 1519 – Château du Clos Lucé

Francesco would later tell me he didn't feel the cold when they let him through the gates, though the morning had a chill to it that should have made him shiver after standing still so long. He didn't feel much of anything, really—just that dull, echoing numbness people get when they've arrived too late for something they cannot name without breaking into pieces they're not sure they could reassemble.

The young servant—whose name Francesco never learned, or perhaps learned and immediately forgot in the shock—led him through the gates with gentle courtesy, as if understanding that this stranger needed to be handled carefully, that something in him had cracked and might shatter completely with rough treatment.

They crossed the courtyard. Francesco remembered noticing, with the strange clarity grief sometimes brings, the way the gravel crunched under their feet. The way the château's pale stone seemed to glow in the morning light. The way everything looked so peaceful, so normal, as if the world should have stopped turning when Leonardo's heart stopped beating but somehow hadn't received the message.

The servant led him through the entrance, through corridors that were modest compared to the palaces Francesco had visited while chasing Leonardo—no grand frescoes here, no soaring ceilings, just comfortable rooms built for living rather than impressing. The servant spoke softly as they walked, as though raising his voice might shatter whatever fragile composure my husband was desperately trying to maintain.

"Monsieur Melzi is here," the servant said quietly. "He has been... with the Maestro since early morning. He will want to speak with you, I think."

They reached a small study—Francesco described it as intimate, book-lined, with windows overlooking gardens that were just beginning their spring bloom. A fire burned low in the hearth, more for comfort than warmth.

A young man stood there, perhaps thirty years old, with refined features and intelligent eyes that were red-rimmed from weeping. A handkerchief still clutched in one hand, damp from use. Francesco Melzi—not a servant, not merely a student, but Leonardo's devoted companion. His heir. Practically a son, in all the ways that mattered.

Melzi looked up when they entered. His face showed immediate recognition of what Francesco must be—another person coming to pay respects, another life touched by Leonardo's passing. He bowed slightly, gently, like someone approaching an injured animal that might bolt if startled.

"You came a long way, Signor..." He paused, waiting for a name.

"Del Giocondo," Francesco managed. "Francesco del Giocondo. From Florence."

Something flickered in Melzi's eyes—recognition, perhaps, or just the exhausted kindness of someone who'd been receiving mourners all morning. "You came a long way, Signor del Giocondo."

"Yes," Francesco said. It was the only word he could manage without his voice breaking. Just that one syllable, confirming the obvious, filling the space where explanation should have been.

Melzi didn't ask the purpose of his visit, didn't require the story of why a Florentine merchant had traveled to France. He understood immediately, the way grief makes people understand things without words. What else brings a man across mountains and kingdoms, through foreign territories and at considerable expense, arriving by cruel chance on the very day a genius leaves the world?

Instead, he stepped forward and placed a hand on my husband's arm—a gesture of comfort, of shared loss, of recognition that they were both standing in the wreckage of something larger than either of them.

"Would you like to see where he worked?" Melzi asked quietly. "It brings... some comfort. To see the things he left behind. To understand he was here, that it was real."

Francesco nodded, not trusting himself to speak again.

Melzi led him deeper into the château, to Leonardo's workshop—the space where the Maestro had spent his final years. As soon as Francesco stepped through the doorway, he told me, it was like stepping back in time. Like walking into any of the dozen workshops he'd visited over the years in Florence, Milan, Rome.

The same beautiful chaos. Tables cluttered with sketches—some fresh, some yellowing with age. Gears and mechanisms in various states of assembly. Bits of metal, coils of wire, tools whose purposes weren't immediately clear. Half-formed ideas scattered across every surface like autumn leaves. Leonardo's world—messy, brilliant, eternally unfinished, just like everything he touched.

Francesco walked slowly through the space, his eyes taking in everything with an almost desperate attention, as if by memorizing these details he could somehow hold onto something of the man who was gone. He reached out once, fingers hovering over a geometrical diagram covered in Leonardo's distinctive mirror-writing, but pulled back before touching it, as though he were unworthy of making contact with genius even in its absence.

"He was working until the end," Melzi said softly, following behind. "His hands troubled him these last years—some paralysis, the physicians said—but his mind never stopped. Always thinking, always curious, always seeing connections no one else could see."

Francesco nodded, his throat too tight for words.

And then Melzi led him to a smaller alcove off the main workshop, a space that felt quieter, almost reverent. More private. Where the important things were kept.

"This," Melzi said, his voice dropping even softer, "I believe... is yours."

Francesco didn't understand at first. His mind, still numb with shock and grief, couldn't quite process what Melzi meant. He stood in the doorway of the alcove, confused, waiting for clarity.

Not until Melzi stepped aside, gesturing toward something leaning against the far wall, partially covered with a cloth.

And there I was.

My face. My eyes. My hands folded just so. Captured in paint and Leonardo's impossible skill at seeing beneath surfaces.

Sixteen years younger than the woman Francesco had left in Florence. Sitting patiently, eternally patient, in that pose Leonardo had positioned me in so long ago it felt like another lifetime. My hands folded in my lap, one over the other, the gesture both natural and eternal. My eyes looking out with that particular expression Leonardo had always teased me about—"too knowing," he'd said once, during one of those early sittings, and then immediately forgot he'd said it because something else had caught his attention.

The portrait wasn't finished. Of course it wasn't. Francesco could see the places where Leonardo had continued working, refining, adjusting. Areas where the paint was smoother, more resolved. Other areas still rough, still waiting for whatever touch Leonardo had planned to apply next. The background strange and dreamlike—not Florence, not anywhere real, but some landscape of imagination.

But it didn't matter that it wasn't finished.

Because in that room, in that moment of standing before it, my husband finally saw the thing he had chased for half his life. Not the painting—or not _just_ the painting—but the man behind it. Leonardo's touch in every gentle line, every subtle gradation of shadow. His humor somehow present in the faint curve of my smile, that enigmatic expression that wasn't quite happiness but wasn't anything else simple either. His devotion to beauty and truth and capturing something ineffable, even when he couldn't quite bring himself to call anything complete, to deliver it on time, to let it go and move on to the next obsession.

Francesco told me later—months later, when he could finally speak about it without his hands shaking—that he felt something inside him loosen in that moment. Or break. Or both. Some tension he'd been carrying for sixteen years, wound so tightly he'd forgotten it was there, suddenly releasing all at once.

Melzi stood beside him, patient and kind, allowing him time to simply look, to absorb, to feel whatever he needed to feel.

"It is yours, is it not?" Melzi said eventually, gently. "A commission from many years ago. Florence. I remember him speaking of it. The merchant's wife with the interesting smile. He was quite fond of it, you know. Kept working on it, year after year. He said he could never quite capture what he wanted to capture."

Francesco opened his mouth. Nothing came out. He tried again, forcing air through his throat.

He could have claimed it. Right then. He had every right—legal, moral, financial. The contract was his. The payment made in full, sixteen years ago, in good Florentine gold. The loss his to mourn, the recompense his to demand. Any court in Christendom would have agreed. This was his property, commissioned and paid for, withheld through no fault of his own.

Melzi clearly expected him to claim it. Was offering it, even, with generosity and understanding. Making it easy for Francesco to take what was rightfully his.

But Francesco just stood there, staring at the portrait. At me frozen in time, forever patient, forever waiting, forever looking out with that knowing expression. At the life he had chased around Italy, across decades, through workshops and palaces and rumors. At the thing that had defined him more than any business success, any social advancement, any achievement in his ordinary merchant's life.

Finally, after what felt like an eternity but was probably only a minute or two, he whispered, "It belongs here now."

His voice was barely audible, cracked with emotion.

Melzi looked surprised—genuinely surprised, as if this was not the response he'd expected or prepared for. "Signore, you have every right to take it. The Maestro would have wanted you to have it. He spoke of you, sometimes. The persistent Florentine who kept appearing in different cities. He was... fond of you, I think. Amused, certainly, but fond."

Francesco shook his head slowly, still looking at the portrait. "No. It was his. More his than it could ever be mine. Let it stay with his other works. Let it be part of his legacy, not..." He trailed off, unable to finish the thought.

Not a merchant's trophy. Not proof of taste or culture. Not a symbol of social climbing successfully achieved. Just Leonardo's work, where it belonged, among other things Leonardo had created and could never quite finish.

Melzi was quiet for a long moment, then nodded with understanding and respect. "As you wish, Signore. That is... very generous. The Maestro attracted such devotion. Even from those he wronged, he inspired reverence. It's a kind of grace, I suppose."

Grace. Francesco wasn't sure about that. It felt more like exhaustion. Like the ending of something that had gone on too long. Like the moment when you stop running after something and realize you're too tired to take another step, and the relief of stopping is stronger than the disappointment of not catching what you chased.

My husband turned away from the painting slowly, carefully, as though afraid it might disappear if he moved too quickly or blinked too hard. As though this might all be a dream and he'd wake to find he was still on the road to Amboise, still hoping, still believing this journey would end differently.

He looked at it one last time—at me, at Lisa captured in Leonardo's impossible skill, at the thing that had shaped sixteen years of his life—and then he walked out of the alcove.

Melzi accompanied him back through the workshop, through the corridors, to the entrance. They stood together in the courtyard for a moment, two men bound by grief for the same complicated genius, saying goodbye without quite knowing what they were saying goodbye to.

"Thank you," Francesco said. "For letting me see it. For... everything."

"Will you return to Florence now?" Melzi asked.

"Yes. There's nothing else here for me."

"The funeral will be in a few days, at the Church of Saint-Florentin. You would be welcome, if you wished to stay."

But Francesco shook his head. He couldn't bear it, he told me later. Couldn't bear watching them lower Leonardo into the ground, making it all irrevocably final. Better to leave now, while some part of him could still imagine Leonardo working in that cluttered workshop, forever thinking, forever creating, forever promising to finish things "soon."

He mounted his horse, accepted Melzi's quiet blessing for a safe journey, and rode back down the path he'd climbed with such hope just hours before.

Not running. Not defeated, exactly.

Just... emptied.

Hollowed out like a vessel that had carried something precious and then had it poured out, leaving only the shape of what had been.

He rode back through Amboise, past the people who still looked at him with that strange pity, understanding now what they had known that morning—that the man they saw was marked by loss, by arriving too late, by standing at the edge of something that had already ended.

And he began the long journey home. Alone. With nothing to show for sixteen years but stories he could tell at dinner parties, memories that would fade with time, and the realization—bitter and strange and somehow freeing all at once—that the chase, always the chase, had been all he ever truly wanted.

Not the portrait.

Not the object.

Not the proof or the trophy or the social advancement.

Just the pursuit itself. The reason to keep moving forward. The possibility that around the next corner, in the next city, at the next workshop, he might find what he was looking for.

And now that possibility was gone.

Leonardo was gone.

And Francesco would have to learn how to live without the chase that had defined him.

The road back to Florence stretched ahead of him, long and empty, and he rode into it alone.
