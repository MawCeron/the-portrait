## **Chapter 15: The Portrait, At Last**

Francesco would later tell me he didn’t feel the cold when they let him through the gates. He didn’t feel much of anything, really—just that dull, echoing numbness people get when they’ve arrived too late for something they cannot name without breaking.

A servant led him through the corridors of Clos Lucé, speaking softly, as though raising his voice might shatter whatever was left of my husband’s composure. When they reached a small study, a young man greeted him. Refined features, red-rimmed eyes, a handkerchief still clutched and damp. **Francesco Melzi.** Leonardo’s devoted student. His heir. Practically a son.

Melzi bowed, gently, like someone approaching an injured animal.

“You came a long way, Signor del Giocondo.”

“Yes,” Francesco said. It was the only word he could manage.

Melzi didn’t ask the purpose of his visit—he understood immediately. What else brings a man across mountains and kingdoms, arriving on the day a genius leaves the world? Instead, he placed a hand on my husband’s arm and said, “Would you like to see where he worked?”

Francesco nodded.

They walked through the workshop: tables cluttered with sketches, gears, bits of metal, half-formed ideas. Leonardo’s world—messy, brilliant, unfinished, just like everything he touched. My husband reached out once, almost touching a geometrical diagram, but pulled back as though unworthy.

And then Melzi led him to a smaller alcove, quiet, almost reverent.

“This,” Melzi said softly, “I believe… is yours.”

Francesco didn’t understand at first. Not until Melzi stepped aside.

And there I was.

Sixteen years younger. Sitting patiently, hands folded, eyes softened in that way Leonardo always teased me about—“too knowing,” he said once, and then immediately forgot that he’d said it.

The portrait wasn’t finished. Of course it wasn’t. But it didn’t matter.

Because in that room, in that moment, my husband finally saw the thing he had chased for half his life—not the painting, but the man behind it. Leonardo’s touch in every gentle line. His humor in the faint curve of my smile. His devotion to beauty, even when he couldn’t quite bring himself to deliver it on time.

Francesco told me later he felt something inside him loosen. Or break. Or both.

Melzi stood beside him, patient. Kind. “It is yours, is it not? A commission from many years ago.”

Francesco opened his mouth. Nothing came. He tried again.

He could have claimed it. He had every right—legal, moral, financial. The contract was his. The payment long made. The loss his to mourn.

But he just stared at the portrait. At me. At the life he had chased around Italy, across decades, through workshops and palaces and rumors.

Finally, he whispered, “It belongs here now.”

Melzi looked surprised, but he didn’t argue. Genius attracts reverence, even from those wronged by it.

My husband turned away from the painting slowly, as though afraid it might disappear if he blinked. And then he left the room, then the workshop, then the château.

Not running. Not defeated.

Just… emptied.

And he began the long journey home. Alone. With nothing to show for sixteen years but stories, memories, and the realization that the chase—always the chase—was all he had ever truly wanted.
