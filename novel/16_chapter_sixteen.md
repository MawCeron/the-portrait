## **Chapter 16: The Return**

He came back looking older than when he left. Not tired—_aged_. Something in him had been scraped out on the road between Amboise and Florence, and whatever was left had settled into a shape I did not recognize.

When he first walked through our door, I thought he was ill. He barely saw me. Barely saw the house. He moved as though his bones hurt.

Only later, when he finally spoke, did I understand: the wound wasn’t in the body.

He had arrived in France the morning Leonardo died.

He kept saying it like he still didn’t believe it.
As if repetition could undo it.
As if the right number of retellings might reverse time by a day.

“If I’d left a week earlier,” he muttered.
“If the roads had been better.”
“If the weather—if the driver—if—”

His voice kept tripping, collapsing into silence.

There are no words to answer a man arguing with fate, so I didn’t try. I just sat beside him and held his hand until he stopped shaking.

Only hours later, when he told me about seeing the portrait, did the trembling start again.

“It was there,” he said, staring past the wall, past the city, past everything. “Set aside. Waiting. As if he’d meant to finish it.”

I waited.

“I could have taken it,” he whispered. “The student said so. Melzi. Very kind, very—” His voice snagged. “He asked if it was mine.”

“And?” I asked softly.

Francesco swallowed. “I left it.”

He sounded bewildered, like he was hearing the decision for the first time.
Sixteen years of travel, expense, obsession—
and he left it.

“Why?” I asked, as gently as possible.

He opened his mouth. Closed it. Tried again. Failed.

“I don’t know,” he finally said. “I don’t know, Lisa.”

And there, at last, the dam burst.

He pressed both hands to his face and let the grief spill through his fingers.
Not quiet grief—raw, furious, bewildered grief.
The kind that comes from chasing something so long you mistake the chase for your purpose, and then suddenly the path ends and you have nothing left to run toward.

“I wasted my life chasing a dead man,” he spat, the words soaked in bitterness.

I touched his arm. He didn’t pull away, but he didn’t lean in either.

“It was right there,” he whispered. “Right in front of me. After all those years. And I walked away like a coward or a fool. What kind of man does that?”

I didn’t answer.
I knew better than to offer comfort he wasn’t ready to hear.
In moments like these, comfort is just noise.

Days passed like that—Francesco drifting through the house like a ghost. He didn’t go to the shop. Didn’t speak to the children. Barely spoke to me. He sat at the window, staring at the street as if expecting to see Leonardo wander up with the portrait under his arm.

He looked broken.
Not physically—though travel had left its mark—but in the way a dream breaks when daylight hits it.

People in Florence assumed he returned triumphant, or at least with a story. They didn’t know he’d arrived the very day the Maestro died. They didn’t know he’d stood before the painting and chosen to leave it behind. They didn’t know what that choice cost him.

But I knew.

And I knew this too:
he wasn’t angry at Leonardo.
Not truly.
He was angry at himself—for needing the man so much, for believing the chase would end in a neat, satisfying conclusion, as though life ever worked that way.

Fifteen years chasing a comet, and when it finally burned out, he had nothing but the ashes of his own longing.

The house was quiet. Too quiet.

And I waited, watching the man I married crumble under the weight of the ending he never expected.
