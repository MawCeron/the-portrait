# CHAPTER 16 – The Return

## June 1519 – Florence

He came back looking older than when he left.

Not tired from travel—though the journey had clearly exhausted him. Not weathered from weeks on the road—though his skin had darkened from sun and his clothes were stained with dust no amount of brushing could fully remove.

Aged. That was the word. Something fundamental in him had been scraped out on the road between Amboise and Florence, hollowed out and discarded somewhere along those hundreds of miles, and whatever was left had settled into a shape I did not immediately recognize as my husband.

When he first walked through our door—no warning, no message sent ahead, just suddenly there in the late afternoon—I thought he was ill. Fevered, perhaps, or suffering from some injury he was hiding. He barely saw me standing there. Barely registered the house around him, the familiar walls and furniture and the life we'd built together. He moved through the courtyard like a man whose bones hurt, each step careful and measured, as though something inside him might break if he moved too quickly.

"Francesco?" I said, and my voice sounded strange to my own ears—too bright, too hopeful, covering the fear that was already rising in my chest.

He looked at me then, really looked, and I saw it. The emptiness. The profound absence of something that had always been there before, that restless energy that had driven him forward for so long.

"I'm home," he said, and his voice was flat. Hollow. Like he was reading lines someone else had written.

I crossed to him, took his face in my hands the way I had a thousand times before, searching his eyes for the man I'd sent away. "What happened? Are you hurt? Francesco, what—"

"Later," he said quietly, pulling away gently but firmly. "I need... Later, Lisa. Please."

He walked past me, up the stairs to our bedchamber, moving like someone much older than his years. I heard the door close—not slammed, just... closed. Final.

Our children came to me with questions I couldn't answer. Where had he been? Had he found what he was looking for? Why did Father look so strange? I told them their father needed rest, that the journey had been long and difficult, that we should let him be for now.

But I knew the truth even then: the wound wasn't in his body.

---

He stayed in our room for two days. Came down only for meals he barely touched, then retreated again. I brought him wine, bread, asked if he needed anything. He just shook his head and stared at walls that held no answers.

Only on the third day, when I'd had enough of silence and grief I couldn't understand, did I go up to him with purpose rather than caution.

"Tell me," I said, sitting on the edge of our bed where he lay fully clothed, staring at the ceiling. "Whatever happened, whatever you found or didn't find, you have to tell me. This silence is worse than any truth could be."

He was quiet so long I thought he wouldn't answer. Then, very softly:

"I arrived in France the morning Leonardo died."

The words fell between us like stones into still water, creating ripples that spread outward into everything.

He kept saying it, over and over, like repetition could make it less true. Like if he told the story enough times with enough variations, eventually the ending would change.

"The same day, Lisa. The same morning. If I'd left Florence a week earlier—if the roads had been better—if the weather hadn't delayed us in the mountains—if the horse hadn't thrown a shoe outside Lyon—if—if—"

His voice kept tripping over itself, collapsing mid-sentence into silence, then starting again with another "if."

There are no words that can answer a man arguing with fate, no comfort that can ease the cruelty of such timing. So I didn't try to find them. I just moved closer and took his hand—that familiar hand I'd held for thirty years, now feeling older and more fragile than I remembered—and held it until the trembling slowed.

"I'm sorry," I whispered. "I'm so sorry, my love."

"He died that morning," Francesco repeated, as if I hadn't understood. "While I was riding toward Amboise, thinking about what I'd say to him, rehearsing my greeting, he was dying. Or already dead. I was too late for everything."

We sat in silence for a long time. Outside, Florence continued its daily business—merchants calling their wares, children playing in the street, the bells of Santa Maria marking the hours. Life continuing as it always did, indifferent to private grief.

Only later, as the afternoon light slanted through our window turning everything golden, did he tell me about the portrait.

"It was there," he said suddenly, his voice strange and distant, staring past me, past the wall, past the entire city, into some space only he could see. "Set aside in an alcove. Waiting. As if he'd meant to finish it someday. As if 'someday' was actually going to arrive."

I waited, knowing he needed to tell this at his own pace, in his own way.

"The student—Melzi, his name was Francesco too, how strange is that—he took me to the workshop. Showed me... everything. The chaos, the sketches, the projects. And then he showed me..." He stopped, swallowed hard. "Your portrait, Lisa. After sixteen years. There you were."

"And?" I asked, keeping my voice soft, neutral.

"It was beautiful," he said, and now there were tears sliding down his face though his voice remained steady. "Unfinished, but beautiful. More beautiful than I'd imagined all these years. He captured something in your expression that I... I don't even know how to describe it."

"Did you bring it home?" Though I already knew the answer.

"I could have," Francesco said, and now his voice broke completely. "Melzi offered it. Said it was mine by right. The student was generous, kind. He asked if it was mine, and I—"

He stopped, unable to continue.

"And?" I prompted gently.

Francesco swallowed, wiped his face roughly with his sleeve. "I left it there."

He sounded bewildered by his own words, as if hearing the decision for the first time and not quite believing it. Sixteen years of travel, of expenses we couldn't quite afford, of time away from home and family and business, of obsession that had shaped his entire adult life—and he'd walked away and left it there.

"Why?" I asked, as gently as possible, truly wanting to understand.

He opened his mouth. Closed it. Tried again. Failed. His face twisted with frustration and confusion and grief so tangled together I couldn't separate the strands.

"I don't know," he finally said, the words barely more than a whisper. "I don't know, Lisa. I stood there looking at it, and I thought—I should take it. It's mine. I paid for it. I've earned it. But my hands wouldn't move. My feet wouldn't move. I just... couldn't. And then I was walking out, and it was still there, and I was leaving, and I—I don't know why. I don't understand my own decision."

And there, at last, the dam that had been holding back three weeks of grief finally burst.

He pressed both hands to his face and let it spill through his fingers—not quiet, dignified grief, but raw, furious, bewildered grief. The kind that comes from chasing something so long you mistake the chase for your purpose, for your identity, for the very structure of your life, and then suddenly the path ends and you're standing in empty space with nothing left to run toward and no idea who you are without the running.

"I wasted my life chasing a dead man," he said, the words thick with bitterness, soaked in self-loathing. "Sixteen years, Lisa. Sixteen years of my life, of our money, of time with you and the children. For what? To arrive the day he died? To stand in front of the thing I'd been chasing and not even be able to take it?"

I reached for him, wrapped my arms around his shaking shoulders, held him the way I'd held our children when they were small and the world had hurt them in ways I couldn't fix.

He didn't pull away, but he didn't lean into me either. Just sat rigid in my embrace, fighting against the grief even as it consumed him.

"It was right there," he whispered against my shoulder. "Right in front of me. After all those years, all those cities, all those empty promises and missed connections. It was there, and I could have had it, and I walked away like a coward or a fool or—I don't even know what. What kind of man does that? What kind of man chases something for sixteen years and then walks away from it?"

I didn't answer. I knew better than to offer comfort he wasn't ready to hear, solutions to problems that had no solutions. In moments like these, comfort is just noise. Explanations are just excuses. Sometimes a person needs to break before they can begin putting themselves back together, and any interference only delays the necessary destruction.

So I just held him and let him grieve for the life he thought he'd been living, for the ending he'd imagined, for the man he'd believed himself to be.

---

The days that followed were difficult in ways I hadn't anticipated.

Francesco drifted through the house like a ghost, present but not present, there but not there. He didn't go to the shop, despite Marco sending increasingly worried messages about decisions that needed to be made. Didn't speak to the children beyond single-word acknowledgments when they tried to engage him. Barely spoke to me except when directly asked a question.

He sat at the window in our bedchamber for hours, staring at the street below as if expecting to see Leonardo wander up the road with the portrait under his arm, smiling that distracted smile, saying "Ah, Francesco! There you are. I was just finishing this up. Sorry for the delay."

He looked broken. Not physically—though the travel had certainly left its mark, the weight he'd lost, the new lines around his eyes, the grey that seemed to have spread through his beard like frost. But in some deeper way. In the way a dream breaks when daylight hits it and reveals all the impossibilities you'd been willfully ignoring.

People in Florence began to notice his return, of course. Merchants who knew he'd traveled, friends who'd heard about his journey, neighbors who'd seen him come back looking changed. They asked questions—politely, cautiously, but persistently.

They assumed he'd returned triumphant, or at least with a story worth telling. Expected him to have the portrait finally, or an explanation, or some kind of resolution after all these years.

They didn't know he'd arrived the very day the Maestro died. How could they? The news from France traveled slowly, and when it did arrive—Leonardo da Vinci, dead at sixty-seven, mourned by a king—Francesco didn't speak of his own presence at that moment, his own terrible timing.

They didn't know he'd stood before the portrait he'd chased for sixteen years and chosen to walk away from it. Didn't know what that choice had cost him, what it meant, what it revealed about everything he'd believed about himself.

But I knew.

And I knew this too: he wasn't angry at Leonardo. Not truly. Not anymore.

He was angry at himself. For needing the man so much. For building his entire sense of purpose and identity around this chase. For believing—despite all evidence to the contrary, despite sixteen years of proof—that the pursuit would end in some neat, satisfying conclusion, as though life ever worked that way. As though stories had actual endings rather than just places where we stop telling them.

Sixteen years chasing a comet across the Italian sky, and when it finally burned out, when the light was extinguished, he had nothing left but ashes. The ashes of his own longing. The ashes of a dream he'd never properly examined or questioned because examining it might have required him to stop chasing it.

The house was quiet during those weeks. Too quiet. The children learned to move carefully, to speak softly, to avoid their father's grief the way one avoids an open wound.

And I waited.

Waited for the anger to fade. Waited for the grief to transform into something more manageable. Waited for my husband to find some way to live in a world where Leonardo no longer existed, where the chase was permanently over, where he had to discover who Francesco del Giocondo was when he wasn't pursuing genius across Italy.

Waited, watching the man I married—the ambitious merchant who'd wanted to be more, who'd found his "more" in the strangest possible way—crumble under the weight of an ending he'd never expected because he'd never truly believed the chase would end at all.

And wondered if he would ever be whole again, or if this was simply who he was now: the man who'd arrived too late and left too soon, who'd chased for sixteen years and walked away empty-handed, who'd finally stood at the finish line and discovered there was nothing there worth finishing for.
