# CHAPTER 17 – The Letter Arrives

## Late June 1519 – Florence

In the days that followed the arrival of the letter, our home felt quieter than I had ever known it.

And we del Giocondos are not quiet people. We come from merchants and talkers, people who fill rooms with words and laughter and argument and life. Even our shadows usually have things to say, opinions to offer, grievances to air.

But not that week.

The silence that settled over our household was thick and strange, like fog that refuses to burn off even when the sun rises. Our children moved through the rooms carefully, speaking in whispers when they had to speak at all. The servants went about their work with unusual efficiency, no lingering to chat or gossip as they normally did. Everyone could feel it—the weight of something unspoken pressing down on us all.

Francesco drifted from chair to window to bed and back again, like a man trying every position in which misery might feel less humiliating, less complete. Some mornings he dressed as if he genuinely meant to go to the shop, pulling on his merchant's clothes with slow, deliberate movements. But then he'd stop halfway through buttoning his coat and just... sit back down. Stare at nothing. Stand again. Move to the window. Sit again.

He didn't even pretend to read the documents Marco kept sending—contracts that needed signing, decisions that required his attention. They piled up on his desk untouched, gathering dust like everything else in his life that required action he couldn't summon.

He just stared. At walls. At floorboards. At the ceiling. As though Leonardo himself might be hiding in the grain of the wood or the cracks in the plaster, as though if he looked hard enough he might find some answer to a question he couldn't quite articulate.

The letter stayed on my writing table where I'd placed it that first morning.

Unopened. Untouched. Avoided like a snake coiled in the corner.

It seemed to glow at the edge of my vision no matter where I stood in the house—a small moon orbiting our household, invisible but exerting its pull on everything, tugging at tides I couldn't quite see but could definitely feel.

I waited.

I've learned patience in thirty years of marriage, learned when to push and when to let things settle on their own. My husband has always been a man whose moods ran hot and quick—a flash of temper, a heavy sigh, a bit of dramatic pacing, and then a return to reason and equilibrium. Weather that passed through and moved on.

But this... this was something different. Something slower and more dangerous. Not a storm but a sinking. Not lightning but drowning. I watched him sink deeper each day, and I didn't know how to throw him a rope he'd be willing to grab.

On the fourth evening after the letter arrived—or was it the fifth? The days had started blending together in that terrible way they do during grief—after a supper he'd barely tasted despite it being his favorite dishes prepared exactly how he liked them, he finally broke the silence.

"I don't want to hear it, Lisa."

No preamble. No context. No transition from the quiet chewing and clinking of forks. He just announced it like a man resuming a conversation with himself that had been happening silently all through the meal.

I set down my wine cup carefully. "Hear what, love?"

"The letter." He gestured vaguely toward my writing room without looking that direction, as if even acknowledging its location was too much. "Whatever is in it. Whatever Leonardo had to say. I don't want to know."

He rubbed his temples with both hands, pressing hard as if he could squeeze the thoughts out through sheer force. "If it's regret, I don't need it. I have enough of my own. If it's excuses—more promises about finishing things 'soon,' more explanations about why he couldn't quite—" He stopped, breathed hard through his nose. "I've had enough of those for a lifetime."

His voice was rough, scraped raw.

"And if it's instructions," he continued, almost laughing but without any humor, "if he's somehow managed to give me orders from beyond the grave, tell me what to do with the portrait or his legacy or God knows what else—then he can give those instructions to the king. Let Francis deal with Leonardo's unfinished business. I'm done."

His voice faltered on that last word, cracked like thin ice giving way.

Then, more quietly, almost a whisper: "He always did know how to leave me with the worst timing. Even death. Especially death."

I stood and crossed to him, placed a hand on his shoulder—that shoulder that felt bonier than I remembered, as if grief had physical weight that pressed flesh away from bone. He didn't lean into my touch, seeking comfort the way he used to. But he didn't move away either.

Progress, in these circumstances.

"You loved the chase," I said softly, because it was true and needed saying. "Even when you cursed it. Even when it frustrated you. There was joy in it, Francesco. I watched you light up every time you heard his name, every time you had an excuse to travel. Don't let the ending erase all those years."

He closed his eyes, and I saw tears leak from beneath the lids. "And look how it ended, Lisa. Look how brilliantly it all ended."

The bitterness in his voice could have stripped paint.

I didn't argue. What argument could I offer? That it could have ended worse? That at least he'd seen the portrait, even if he didn't bring it home? That at least he had stories, memories, something more than nothing?

All true, perhaps. But grief doesn't listen to logic. Grief eats logic for breakfast and asks for seconds.

Instead, I reached for his hand—that same hand that had once waved contracts in the air with absolute certainty, that had knocked on Leonardo's workshop doors in half a dozen cities, that had counted out coins for yet another journey, that had held mine through births and deaths and thirty years of ordinary days.

That hand felt older now, more fragile. I held it lightly, like something that might break.

"You came so close," I said, because anything else would have felt cruel. Because sometimes acknowledging the nearness of the miss is all the comfort there is.

"So close," he repeated, and his voice was hollow, echoing with emptiness. "A morning, Lisa. A single morning. A few hours. If I'd woken earlier, ridden faster, if the weather had been different, if anything had been slightly different—" He shook his head. "I wasted half my life hunting a man I couldn't even catch before death did."

A humorless smile tugged at the corner of his mouth, ugly and self-mocking.

"He beat me even at dying. How perfect is that? Sixteen years of never quite reaching him, and in the end, death reaches him first by a matter of hours while I'm still on the road thinking about what I'll say, how I'll smile, how satisfying it will be to finally—" He stopped, the words choking off.

He pulled his hand back from mine and stood abruptly, needing to move, to pace, to do something with the energy that had nowhere productive to go.

"And that portrait," he said, starting to pace the room in that familiar pattern, back and forth, back and forth. "Right there. Right in front of me. Mine. Paid for sixteen years ago. The student offering it to me, telling me it was mine by right. And I just... walked away. Like a coward. Like a fool. Like a man who doesn't even understand his own desires."

"That's not true," I said gently, firmly. "You're not a coward."

He ignored me completely, too deep in his own spiral to hear contradictions.

"Maybe the king would've let it go if I'd asked properly. Maybe I could have explained, made a case. Or maybe Melzi could have simply given it to me quietly and no one would have known or cared. What does it matter now?" He laughed, sharp and bitter. "I came home with nothing. Empty hands. Empty heart. Nothing to show for any of it."

He stopped his pacing, turning toward me with eyes that had lost all their fire, all the spark that had driven him for so long. Just ashes now. Just the remains of something that had burned itself out.

"I wasted my life, Lisa."

He said it so quietly I almost missed it. Almost didn't catch those four words that contained everything he'd been thinking during those silent days.

I wanted to tell him it wasn't true. Wanted to list everything he had—sons who respected him, a daughter who loved him, wealth enough to live comfortably, a business that thrived, a name respected throughout Florence, friendships and connections and a life full of more than most men ever achieved.

Wanted to tell him that the chase had given him things he couldn't see yet, that he'd become more interesting, more cultured, more alive during those years than he'd ever been before them.

But none of that would reach him. Not yet. Not while the grief was still this fresh, this raw.

Grief is deaf to practicality—even mine, earned through years of managing households and businesses and the thousand daily realities that require clear-eyed assessment.

Instead, I crossed the room to where my writing table stood, picked up the letter—still sealed, still untouched, still waiting—and held it between us.

"You don't have to read it," I said softly, meeting his eyes. "But I will. If you'll let me. And I'll tell you what it says, or not tell you, depending on what you want. But Francesco—" I paused, making sure he was really hearing me. "This letter might have answers you need. Or it might have nothing. But not knowing won't make it hurt less. It'll just make it hurt differently."

Francesco's breath caught audibly. His shoulders tightened, muscles drawing up as if preparing for a blow. For a long moment I thought he might snatch the letter away from me after all—tear it in two, throw it in the fire, bury it in the courtyard, anything to keep it from being real, from having to be dealt with.

His hand actually twitched toward it.

But then he just whispered, barely audible, "Do what you want."

And he turned and left the room, his footsteps heavy on the stairs as he climbed toward our bedchamber.

I stood there alone with the letter in my hands, feeling its weight once more—heavier now than the morning it had arrived, heavier with everything unsaid between the two men whose lives had tangled together for far too long, whose friendship—if that's what it was—had shaped my husband's entire adult life without either of them fully acknowledging what it meant.

The wax seal glowed slightly in the lamplight. Leonardo's mark. Still unbroken. Still keeping its secrets.

Tomorrow, I decided. Tomorrow I would open it. Would read whatever Leonardo had left behind for the man who'd chased him across Italy for sixteen years. Would discover what wisdom or foolishness, what comfort or cruelty, what explanation or apology lay waiting inside that folded paper.

Tonight, I would keep watch over it, as if guarding the last breath of a story neither of them had known how to end because neither of them had wanted it to end at all.

I set it back on the table, smoothed my skirts, and went to check that the house was locked for the night.

Tomorrow would come soon enough.

And with it, whatever truth Leonardo had left behind.
