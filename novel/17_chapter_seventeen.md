## **Chapter 17: The Letter Arrives**

In the days that followed the arrival of the letter, our home felt quieter than I had ever known it. And we del Giocondos are not quiet people. Even our shadows usually have things to say.

But not that week.

Francesco drifted from chair to window to bed like a man trying every position in which misery might feel less humiliating. Some mornings he dressed as if he meant to go to the shop, only to stop halfway through buttoning his coat and sit back down. He didn’t even pretend to read. He just stared at the floorboards as though Leonardo himself might be hiding between them.

The letter stayed on my writing table. Unopened. Untouched. It seemed to glow at the corner of my eye no matter where I stood — a small moon orbiting our household, tugging at the tides.

I waited.

My husband has always been a man whose moods ran hot and quick. A flash of temper, a sigh, a bit of pacing, and then a return to reason. But this… this was something slower. A sinking, not a storm.

On the fourth evening, after a supper he barely tasted, he finally spoke.

“I don’t want to hear it, Lisa.” No prompt. No context. He just announced it like a man resuming a conversation with himself. “Whatever is in that letter… I don’t want to know.”

He rubbed his temples, exhausted. “If it’s regret, I don’t need it. If it’s excuses, I’ve had enough for a lifetime. And if it’s instructions—God forbid—then he can give them to the king.”

His voice faltered. Then, more quietly: “He always did know how to leave me with the worst timing.”

I crossed to him and placed a hand on his shoulder. He didn’t lean in, but he didn’t move away either. Progress, in these circumstances.

“You loved the chase,” I murmured. “Even when you cursed it.”

He closed his eyes. “And look how it ended.”

I didn’t argue. There was no winning argument to offer. Not yet.

Instead I reached for his hand — the same hand that had once waved contracts in the air with absolute certainty, the same hand that had knocked on Leonardo’s workshop doors in half a dozen cities — and I held it lightly.

“You came so close,” I said, because anything else would have felt cruel.

“So close,” he repeated, but his voice was hollow. “A morning. A single morning. I wasted half my life hunting a man I couldn’t even catch before death did.” A humorless smile tugged at his mouth. “He beat me even at dying.”

He pulled his hand back and stood, pacing again. “And that portrait… right there, in front of me. Mine. Paid for. Sixteen years. And I walked away like a… like a coward.”

“That’s not true,” I said gently.

He ignored it. “Maybe the king would’ve let it go. Maybe not. What does it matter now? I came home with nothing.”

He stopped, turning toward me with eyes that had lost their fire. “I wasted my life, Lisa.”

He said it so quietly I almost missed it.

I wanted to tell him it wasn’t true. That he had friends, sons, wealth, a name respected in Florence. But none of that would reach him just yet. Grief is deaf to practicality — even mine.

Instead, I crossed the room, picked up the letter, and held it between us.

“You don’t have to read it,” I said softly. “But I will.”

Francesco’s breath caught. His shoulders tightened. For a moment I thought he might snatch the letter away from me after all, tear it in two, burn it, bury it — anything to keep it from being real.

But he only whispered, “Do what you want.”

And he left the room.

I stood there with the letter in my hands, feeling its weight once more — heavier now than the morning it arrived, heavier with everything unsaid between the two men whose lives had tangled for far too long.

Tomorrow, I would open it.

Tonight, I kept watch over it, as if guarding the last breath of a story neither of them knew how to end.
