## **Chapter 18: The Reading**

I waited until the house was still — the kind of quiet that settles only after a day spent holding a broken man together. Francesco had gone to bed without eating, without speaking, without even pretending to try. I listened for his footsteps, for pacing, for the restless muttering he sometimes did when he thought I couldn’t hear.

Nothing.

Just silence.

It was enough.

I lit a single candle, sat at the table, and placed the letter before me. The seal had been broken when it arrived — not by us, but by the servant who’d carried it from France — yet I hesitated, as though opening it now might unleash something I wasn’t ready to face.

Then I thought of Francesco’s face when he saw the seal, the way his hope and grief had collided and collapsed in the span of a heartbeat.

I owed it to him. To both of them.

I unfolded the letter.

The handwriting was unmistakable — looping, elegant, slightly chaotic, as though the words had been chasing each other across the page.

And then I read.

---

**“My dear Francesco,”**

**“If you are reading this, then I am gone, and you have finally stopped chasing me.
I confess I always knew you would chase me to the end — it was who you were, who we both were.
Men who could not let go.”**

I felt my throat tighten.

**“I should have finished your wife’s portrait. I should have delivered it years ago,
in 1504, 1508, 1510. I had a thousand opportunities.”**

He had. Oh, he had.

**“But I kept seeing something new in it, something I hadn’t quite captured.
Her smile, yes, but also something else — the mystery of a woman who watched her husband chase a dream
and loved him anyway.”**

My breath hitched. I had never expected Leonardo to notice me at all, much less… that.

I kept reading.

**“But that’s an excuse.
The truth is I kept it because it kept you coming back.
You were never just a patron to me, Francesco.
You were a friend.
One of the few who understood what it meant to chase something you could never quite catch.”**

A hot tear slipped down onto the page. I brushed it away quickly, as if afraid it might smudge his words into nothing.

**“I have left instructions with Francesco Melzi and with King Francis.
The portrait is yours.
It was always meant to be yours.
You need only ask for it, and it will be returned.
I have vouched for your claim personally.”**

My hands trembled. He had prepared for this. Thought about it. Chosen to set things right, even if it took death to bring him clarity.

**“But I suspect — I hope — that you won’t ask for it.
Not because you can’t upset a king,
but because you understand now what I understood years ago:
some things are more valuable in the chasing than in the catching.”**

I pressed a hand to my mouth to keep the sob from escaping.

**“Thank you, my friend, for the chase.
It made me feel less alone.”**

**“Your faithful procrastinator,
Leonardo”**

---

I read it twice more. Slowly. Carefully. As though the words might vanish if I blinked.

When I finally lowered the letter, the candle had burned low, spilling warm light over a table that suddenly felt far too large.

Francesco had not been a fool.

Leonardo had not been mocking him.

The years of wandering, the frustrations, the missed meetings, the elaborate dodges — they had meant something. Not just to my husband, but to the man he’d chased across half of Italy.

There had been friendship there. A strange one, yes. Uneven. Chaotic. But real. Real enough that, in his last days, Leonardo da Vinci had taken the time to write to a silk merchant from Florence — a man no one else had ever believed was important.

I folded the letter, held it against my chest, and let the grief wash over me — not just for Leonardo, not just for Francesco, but for the final page of a story that had always been half-comedy, half-tragedy.

Tomorrow, I would tell my husband.

Tonight, I let myself cry for both men who had spent sixteen years chasing each other in circles, neither one quite realizing they’d already caught something worth keeping.
