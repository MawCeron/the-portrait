# CHAPTER 19 – The Revelation

## Morning, Florence

I found him exactly where I'd left him the night before: in that same chair by the window in our bedchamber, body turned slightly away from the world, as if the view outside—our familiar Florence, the street where we'd lived for decades—had personally offended him and he was refusing to acknowledge its existence.

Morning light edged around him, soft and forgiving in that particular way early summer light has, golden and gentle. Though he wasn't in the mood to appreciate forgiveness yet. Wasn't in the mood to appreciate anything.

He still wore yesterday's clothes. Probably hadn't slept, or if he had, it had been that fitful half-sleep that leaves you more tired than waking.

The letter felt warm in my hands—or perhaps my hands were warm from holding it too tightly, from carrying it up the stairs like something precious and fragile and potentially dangerous all at once. I had read it three times before dawn broke, sitting alone in the dark, memorizing every word, every loop of Leonardo's handwriting. I could have recited it by heart now, though I doubted Francesco would let me get through the first line before stopping me.

"Francesco," I said quietly, stepping into the room.

He didn't answer. Didn't turn. Didn't even sigh or shift position to acknowledge my presence.

But the quality of his stillness changed slightly. Which told me he was listening.

"I read it," I said simply. "The letter. Last night. After you went to bed."

Still no movement. But I saw his shoulders tense, just barely. Saw his hands tighten on the arms of the chair.

I stepped closer, until I was standing beside him, close enough to touch but not touching yet. "You need to read it."

That got him. He straightened just enough to pretend he wasn't bracing himself for impact. "Lisa, I told you—"

"I know what you told me," I interrupted, and my voice was firm now, not gentle. Not giving him room to retreat into his grief and stubbornness. "And now I'm telling you: you need to read this. Not because I say so. Not because it's polite or proper or the right thing to do. But because Leonardo wrote it for you, and you owe him—you owe yourself—the courtesy of hearing what he had to say."

He hesitated. I could see the war happening behind his eyes—the part that wanted to know fighting the part that was terrified to know, the hope and fear wrestling each other into a stalemate.

His stubbornness was a living thing in that room, practically visible, pacing the floor between us like a caged animal. But grief had worn him thin over these weeks. And silence had worn him thinner.

Finally—after what felt like an eternity but was probably only a few heartbeats—he reached out.

Took the letter from my hands.

Not snatched, not grabbed in desperation or anger. He accepted it the way one accepts communion in church—with dread and reverence and a suspicion that it might taste bitter going down but knowing you have to take it anyway because the alternative is worse.

I watched his face as he unfolded it. The paper made small sounds in the quiet room, crisp and final. Watched the lines around his mouth pull tight as his eyes found the first words. Watched him read Leonardo's greeting, his eyes moving across the page with painful slowness.

_My dear Francesco..._

The first tear fell before he reached the middle.

He tried to hide it, of course—angled his face away from me, blinked too hard, set his jaw like he was negotiating the price of wool with a difficult supplier who had him at a disadvantage. But more tears followed, slow and unwilling, betraying him. By the third one, tracking down his weathered cheek, he'd given up pretending.

I watched him read about promises broken and opportunities missed. Watched his face when he reached the part about me, about my smile, about Leonardo seeing me clearly all those years. Watched him pause there, read it again, glance up at me with something like wonder mixed with his grief.

I smiled slightly, nodded. _Yes. He saw me. He saw us both._

He kept reading, and I saw the moment he reached the heart of it—the part about friendship, about not being alone, about understanding what it meant to chase something you could never quite catch.

His breathing changed. Became ragged, uneven.

When he reached the final lines—_Thank you, my friend, for the chase. It made me feel less alone_—his breath left him in a shudder that shook his whole frame.

And then, impossibly, he smiled.

It wasn't much at first. Just the ghost of one, barely visible, like the sun trying to break through heavy clouds. But it grew, small and trembling and fragile, until I recognized it—the smile he used to wear in those early years, when he'd return home from Leonardo's workshop with nothing tangible to show for his visit but stories and shining eyes and this sense of having touched something extraordinary.

I'd missed that smile. Hadn't realized how much until I saw it again now.

He read the letter twice more, his lips moving slightly as he went through certain passages, memorizing them the way I had. Then he folded it with exquisite care, as if it might bruise, as if it were made of butterfly wings instead of paper.

I waited, giving him time to process, to feel whatever he needed to feel.

Finally, quietly, I said, "You could still claim the portrait, you know. The letter gives you the right. Leonardo arranged everything with Melzi and the king. You need only write to them. It could be here by autumn."

He shook his head gently, and there was no anger in the gesture, no bitterness. Just certainty. "As if I would be upsetting a King."

But this time—this time—the phrase carried a different weight entirely. A warmth, an ease, almost the tone of a private joke shared between old companions who understood each other perfectly. The bitterness that had soaked those words before was gone, burned away by Leonardo's letter.

It was a line he finally understood the truth of.

"You saw it, didn't you?" I asked, settling into the chair beside his. "In France. At Clos Lucé. You saw the portrait. Melzi showed it to you."

He nodded slowly.

"And you could have taken it then. Before I'd even opened the letter, before you knew what Leonardo had written. You had the right. The legal and moral right."

Another nod.

"So why didn't you?" I asked gently, needing him to say it aloud, to understand his own heart.

He looked out the window, though I doubt he saw anything out there—not the street, not the neighbors passing by, not the morning sun warming the stones. He was looking inward, or backward, or perhaps at some truth that existed in neither direction but somewhere else entirely.

"Because..." He spoke slowly, carefully, like someone discovering the truth as he said it, like someone finding words for something that had never been put into language before. "Because it wasn't mine anymore. Not really. Maybe it never was."

He paused, gathering his thoughts like scattered coins.

"Leonardo didn't just paint your face, Lisa. He painted... everything. The whole thing. The chase. The years. All of it. What it meant. What _he_ meant. The portrait wasn't about you anymore, or about me proving I had good taste or connections or culture. It was about him. About his process, his inability to let things go, his endless seeing of new possibilities. Taking it would have felt like..." He searched for words. "Like taking the past. Like trying to possess something that only existed in motion, in pursuit. And the past is... done. You can't own it. You can only carry it with you."

He swallowed hard, blinked against fresh tears. "Taking it would have ruined it somehow. Made it ordinary. Made it about possession instead of about... whatever it was really about."

I reached over and took his hand. He didn't pull away. Just let me hold it, let me anchor him to this moment, this morning, this new understanding.

He lifted the letter once more with his free hand, touched Leonardo's signature with his thumb, tracing the letters reverently. "He called me his friend, Lisa."

His voice broke on the word _friend_, but gently this time, breaking from emotion rather than from pain.

"All these years... I thought I was just some merchant he tolerated. Someone he forgot to disappoint properly because he forgot about me entirely. A pest. An inconvenience. Someone who meant nothing in his brilliant, important life full of dukes and popes and kings."

He looked at me, and his eyes were bright with tears but also with something else—relief, maybe, or vindication, or simply the profound comfort of being seen and valued by someone you admired.

"But he remembered me, Lisa. He valued me. He called me his friend and meant it. He took the time, in his last days, to write this. To tell me... to tell me it mattered. That _I_ mattered. Not as a patron or a source of money or an obligation. As a friend."

"He did," I whispered, squeezing his hand. "You mattered to him. The letter proves it."

Francesco leaned back in his chair, closing his eyes, letting the truth finally settle in the places where all the bitterness and self-recrimination and grief had been festering. Letting Leonardo's words displace the poison that had been eating at him.

For the first time since he'd returned from France—since that terrible day when he'd walked through our door looking like a ghost—he looked lighter.

Not happy yet. Happiness would come later, in small steps, in gradual healing. But unburdened. Unbroken. Like he had finally stopped carrying the weight of all those unfinished years, all that unresolved longing, all that wondering if any of it had meant anything at all.

"He wasn't perfect," Francesco said softly after a long silence, opening his eyes to look at me. And there was no accusation in it, no bitterness. Just simple truth, simply stated.

"No," I agreed. "Neither are you."

He chuckled—a real one, low and warm and surprised out of him. The first genuine laugh I'd heard from him in weeks. "Fair point."

"But you chased each other, didn't you?" I said. "In your own way. You chasing him across Italy. Him keeping just enough distance to make it interesting. Both of you needing it, though neither of you quite understood why."

"Yes," he said, and the word was soft with wonder. "We did. We chased each other. And now..."

"Now it's finished," I said gently.

He nodded, and there was peace in the gesture. Real peace, not the exhausted surrender of before but actual acceptance. "Finished," he repeated, testing the word, finding it fit properly for the first time. "At last."

And I held his hand while he finally let it all go—the portrait he would never hang on our walls, the years he'd spent in pursuit, the grief over arriving too late, the confusion over walking away empty-handed.

Let it all drain away like water finally finding its proper course.

Leaving only the strange, impossible, beautiful friendship that had survived everything else.

The friendship that had been the point all along, though neither of them had quite realized it until it was too late to say so face to face.

But not too late to know.

Not too late for Francesco to understand that he hadn't wasted anything, hadn't been a fool, hadn't chased shadows for sixteen years.

He'd been chasing connection. Understanding. Someone who saw him not as Merchant del Giocondo but as Francesco. Just Francesco. Someone worth knowing.

And he'd found it.

In the worst possible way, at the worst possible time, with the worst possible timing.

But he'd found it.

And that, I thought, watching the morning light warm his face as he sat with Leonardo's letter in his hands and peace finally settling over his features—that was worth something.

That was worth everything.
