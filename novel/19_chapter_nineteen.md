### **Chapter 19 – The Revelation**

I found him exactly where I’d left him the night before: in that same chair by the window, turned slightly away from the world, as if the view outside had offended him personally. Morning light edged around him, soft and forgiving, though he wasn’t in the mood to appreciate forgiveness yet.

The letter felt warm in my hands. I had read it three times before dawn. I could’ve recited it by heart, though I doubted he would let me.

“Francesco,” I said quietly.

He didn’t answer. He didn’t even sigh, which told me he was listening.

“I read it.”

Still no movement.

I stepped closer. “You need to read it.”

That got him. He straightened just enough to pretend he wasn’t bracing himself. “Lisa, I told you—”

“I know what you told me,” I cut in. “And now I’m telling you: you need to read this.”

He hesitated. His stubbornness was a living thing in that room, pacing the floor between us. But grief had worn him thin. And silence had worn him thinner.

Finally, he took the letter.

Not snatched, not grabbed. He accepted it the way one accepts communion — with dread, reverence, and a suspicion that it might taste bitter.

I watched his face as he broke the seal. Watched the lines around his mouth pull tight. Watched his eyes move across the page.

The first tear fell before he reached the middle.

He tried to hide it, of course — angled his face away, blinked too hard, set his jaw like he was negotiating the price of wool. But more followed, slow and unwilling. By the third, he’d given up pretending.

When he reached the final lines — _Thank you, my friend, for the chase. It made me feel less alone._ — his breath left him in a shudder.

And then… he smiled.

It wasn’t much at first. Just the ghost of one. But it grew, small and trembling, until I recognized it — the smile he used to wear in those early years, when he’d return home from Leonardo’s workshop with nothing but stories and shining eyes.

I waited until he folded the letter with care, as if it might bruise.

“You could still claim the portrait,” I murmured. “The letter gives you the right. Leonardo arranged everything.”

He shook his head gently. “As if I would be upsetting a King.”

But this time — _this_ time — the phrase carried a warmth, an ease, almost a joke between old companions. The bitterness was gone. It was a line he finally understood.

“You saw it, didn’t you?” I asked. “In France. You saw the portrait. You could have taken it then.”

He nodded.

“Why didn’t you?”

He looked out the window, though I doubt he saw anything out there. “Because… it wasn’t mine anymore. Not really.” He spoke slowly, like discovering the truth as he said it. “Leonardo painted more than your face. He painted… everything. The chase. The years. What it meant. What he meant.”

He swallowed, blinking again. “Taking it would have felt like taking the past. And the past is… done.”

I sat beside him, my hand finding his. He didn’t pull away.

He lifted the letter once more, touched the signature with his thumb. “He called me his friend, Lisa.” His voice broke on the word _friend_, but gently this time. “All these years… I thought I was just some merchant he forgot to disappoint properly. But he remembered me. He valued me.”

“He did,” I whispered.

Francesco leaned back, closing his eyes, letting the truth finally settle where all the bitterness had been.

For the first time since France, he looked lighter. Not happy yet — that would come later, in small steps — but unburdened. Like he had finally stopped carrying the weight of unfinished years.

“He wasn’t perfect,” he said softly.

“No,” I agreed. “Neither are you.”

He chuckled — a real one, low and warm. “But we chased each other, didn’t we?”

“Yes,” I said. “And now it’s finished.”

He nodded, peaceful. “Finished… at last.”

And I held his hand while he finally let it all go — the portrait, the years, the grief — leaving only the strange, impossible friendship that had survived everything else.
