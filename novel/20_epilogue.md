## **EPILOGUE — The Portrait**

Time has a way of softening the corners of a story. Sharpened edges become gentle slopes; old wounds become old anecdotes. And in the years that followed the letter, that is exactly what happened to my husband.

Francesco never was the same man after reading Leonardo’s last words — thank God for that. The bitterness evaporated first, like morning fog giving up in the face of sunlight. Then the tension left his shoulders. Then the quiet, heavy sadness that had clung to him since France finally cracked open and drifted away.

He moved differently afterward. Lighter. As if someone had finally told him he hadn’t wasted his life — because of course, someone had.

He returned to telling Leonardo stories, but they changed. Before, they had been half-complaints, half-brags, dragged out like trophies from battles he never quite won. After the letter, they became something else. Warm. Generous. Sometimes even a little proud. As if he finally understood that he had been part of something rare, even if neither he nor Leonardo knew what to call it at the time.

He never chased the portrait again — not once. Not even when travelers brought back news of its growing fame in France, or when the Medici teased him about “his painter,” or when merchants at the market whispered that the French king treasured it more than any jewel in his collection. Francesco just smiled, that new, softer smile of his, and said, “Let them have it. It brings me no sorrow there.”

And I believed him.

He grew old telling the same handful of stories, polishing them each time like silverware brought out for guests: the time Leonardo made him lift some odd contraption just to prove it _could_ be lifted; the day a pope kept him talking while Leonardo quietly escaped; the afternoon in Milan when he realized he’d spent more time admiring Leonardo’s sketches than demanding progress on the painting he’d come for.

People thought he exaggerated. I knew he didn’t. If anything, he told the gentler version.

When he died — peacefully, in his bed, his hand in mine — he looked content. That surprised me more than anything. All his life he’d chased things: status, favor, genius, friendship. And yet at the end he seemed satisfied, as if he’d finally caught what he was running after all along.

After he was gone, I found the letter folded inside his ledger book. The old one, the one he used long before Leonardo ever lifted a brush in our direction. I suppose that was his way of keeping the two halves of his life together: the man he was, and the man Leonardo’s friendship helped him become.

As for the portrait… it stayed in France. Of course it did. It had chosen its home long before any of us understood what it was becoming. Travelers say it hangs proudly, admired by nobles and visitors from far-off lands. They say the lady in the painting looks serene, amused, as if she’s enjoying a private joke no one else can quite grasp.

I like to imagine that’s true — that somewhere in that small, unfinished smile is the whole ridiculous, tender, unbelievable story.

Two men:
One who could never finish anything.
One who could never let anything go.
Both of them dreamers in their own stubborn ways.
And somehow, between them, they created something that outlasted all their chasing.

The portrait captured me, yes — my face, my eyes, whatever mystery Leonardo saw in me. But it also captured them. The odd friendship. The long pursuit. The moments of admiration and exasperation tangled together. The feeling of two very different men meeting somewhere halfway between genius and longing.

And perhaps that is as it should be.

Not every story needs a tidy ending. Not every promise must be fulfilled to matter. Some things are better left a little unfinished — like a brushstroke waiting for a hand that will never return, or a smile whose meaning shifts each time you look at it.

I sometimes imagine the portrait still resting in its place in France, surrounded by people who study it, who argue over it, who insist they can decipher every secret hidden in that smile.

Let them try.

They will never know what I know:
That the painting was more than a likeness.
It was a bridge — between a merchant and a genius, between chasing and being found, between loneliness and the one companion who made it bearable.

Leonardo wrote, _“It made me feel less alone.”_
And in the end, that was what Francesco needed too.

Perhaps that is the true masterpiece.
