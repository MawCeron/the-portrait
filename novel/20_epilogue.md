# EPILOGUE – The Portrait

## Years Later – Lisa's Reflection

Time has a way of softening the corners of a story. Sharpened edges become gentle slopes. Old wounds transform into old anecdotes, told at dinner parties with wine-smoothed voices. And in the years that followed the letter—five good years, as it turned out, though we didn't know they'd be the last—that is exactly what happened to my husband.

Francesco was never the same man after reading Leonardo's last words.

Thank God for that.

The bitterness evaporated first, like morning fog giving up in the face of strong sunlight. Then the tension left his shoulders—I watched it drain away over days and weeks, saw him stand straighter, move more easily. Then the quiet, heavy sadness that had clung to him since France, that had made him seem older than his years, finally cracked open like a shell and drifted away on winds I couldn't see but could definitely feel.

He moved differently afterward. Lighter. Freer. As if someone had finally told him the thing he most needed to hear—that he hadn't wasted his life, hadn't been a fool, hadn't spent sixteen years chasing shadows and arriving at nothing.

Because, of course, someone had told him exactly that. Leonardo. In his own way. At the last possible moment.

Francesco returned to the shop within a month of reading the letter, much to Marco's relief and our sons' delight. Returned to negotiating contracts and examining fabrics and advising clients on what colors would suit their complexions. Returned to being Francesco del Giocondo, respected merchant of Florence, instead of Francesco the broken, the lost, the man who'd chased genius and found only grief.

And he returned to telling Leonardo stories—but they changed.

Before the letter, they had been half-complaints, half-brags, dragged out like trophies from battles he never quite won. Stories with an edge to them, a bitterness underneath the humor. Look how clever Leonardo was at avoiding me. Look how I kept trying anyway. Look how it never quite worked out.

After the letter, they became something else entirely.

Warm. Generous. Affectionate, even. Sometimes tinged with pride—not the pride of possession, but the pride of having known someone remarkable, of having been trusted by someone who didn't trust easily, of having mattered to someone whose attention was sought by kings.

As if he finally understood that he had been part of something rare and strange and valuable, even if neither he nor Leonardo had quite known what to call it at the time. Even if they'd never said the words directly to each other while both were living.

Friendship. It had been friendship. Odd and unequal and complicated, but real.

He never chased the portrait again. Not once. Not even when travelers brought back news of its growing fame in France, how people traveled to see it, how scholars debated its meaning. Not even when the Medici teased him about "his painter" and asked why he'd never retrieved his commission. Not even when merchants at the market whispered that King Francis treasured it more than any jewel in his collection, that it had a place of honor, that people spoke of it with wonder.

Francesco would just smile—that new, softer smile of his that reached his eyes now—and say, "Let them have it. It brings me no sorrow there."

And I believed him. Could see the truth of it in his face, in the ease of his posture, in the way he could speak Leonardo's name without flinching.

He had let it go. Really let it go. Not with resignation but with understanding. With peace.

He grew old gracefully after that, telling the same handful of stories to anyone who would listen, polishing them each time like silverware brought out for honored guests. Making them shine.

The time Leonardo made him lift some odd contraption just to prove the physics of leverage, and Francesco had felt absurdly proud that a genius cared about his opinion.

The day Pope Leo X kept him talking about Florentine wool taxes while Leonardo quietly escaped—"Like smoke, Lisa, he simply dissolved into nothing!"—and how Francesco had been too awed by meeting the Pope to be properly angry about the dodge.

The afternoon in Milan when he'd realized, halfway through a visit, that he'd spent more time admiring Leonardo's sketches for impossible flying machines than demanding progress on the painting he'd ostensibly come for, and how he hadn't even minded.

People thought he exaggerated—I could see it in their faces, the polite smiles that said they believed he was embellishing for effect, making himself more important in Leonardo's life than he'd actually been.

I knew better. I knew he didn't exaggerate. If anything, he told the gentler version, leaving out the depths of his obsession, the desperation of his pursuit, the way it had consumed him for years. He told the version where it had all been charming and amusing rather than the version where it had been his entire identity.

When he died—peacefully, in his bed, his hand in mine, in the house we'd shared for forty years—he looked content.

That surprised me more than anything. More than his actual death, which came gently and without much struggle, as if he'd simply decided it was time and his body had agreed.

All his life he'd been a man who chased things: status, favor, recognition, respect, genius, friendship, something he couldn't quite name. Always reaching for the next thing, the better thing, the thing that would finally make him feel like enough.

And yet at the end, he seemed satisfied. As if he'd finally caught what he'd been running after all along, even though I'm not sure he could have told you what it was.

Or maybe he'd learned—perhaps the letter had taught him this—that the catching wasn't the point. That the chase itself, done with the right person for the right reasons, was valuable. Was enough.

After he was gone, after the funeral and the mourning and the slow adjustment to a house that felt too empty without his presence, I found the letter.

Folded carefully, preserved between pages, inside his old ledger book. Not the current one he'd been using, but the one from decades ago—the one he'd kept from before Leonardo ever lifted a brush in our direction, before the portrait was commissioned, before any of this strange story began.

I held the ledger, felt the weight of it, understood what he'd done.

It was his way of keeping the two halves of his life together: the man he was before Leonardo, and the man Leonardo's friendship had helped him become. The merchant and the dreamer. The practical and the impossible. Both parts of him, joined by that letter, by those words that had given him permission to be both.

I cried again, there in his study, holding his ledger and Leonardo's letter. But they were different tears than the ones I'd shed when he died. These were tears of understanding, of gratitude that my husband had found what he needed, had been given the gift of knowing he'd mattered.

I put the letter back exactly where he'd kept it. Left it there, between the pages of his former life, so the two parts of him could rest together.

---

As for the portrait...

It stayed in France. Of course it did.

It had chosen its home long before any of us understood what it was becoming. Long before anyone realized it would become perhaps the most famous painting in the world, that people would travel from distant countries just to stand before it, that scholars would spend lifetimes trying to unravel its mysteries.

Travelers who'd seen it would sometimes stop by our shop—word got around that the woman in Leonardo's painting had been Florentine, had been married to a silk merchant, and they'd come seeking the story.

They say it hangs in places of honor, admired by nobles and visitors from far-off lands. They say people stand before it for hours, transfixed. They say the lady in the painting looks serene, knowing, amused—as if she's enjoying a private joke no one else can quite grasp, as if she knows something the viewers don't.

I like to imagine that's true.

That somewhere in that small, enigmatic, forever-unfinished smile is the whole ridiculous, tender, unbelievable story of how it came to be.

Two men:

One who could never finish anything, who saw endless possibilities in every brushstroke and could never declare something complete.

One who could never let anything go, who held onto hope and pursuit and the dream of someday with a merchant's stubborn determination.

Both of them dreamers in their own ways—Leonardo dreaming of what art could be, Francesco dreaming of what he could become.

Both of them lonely in their own ways—Leonardo surrounded by admirers but understood by few, Francesco surrounded by family and business but hungry for something more.

Both of them perfectionists who couldn't quite let themselves succeed at finishing—Leonardo because finishing meant letting go of possibility, Francesco because finishing would have meant the chase was over and he'd have to find a new purpose.

And somehow, impossibly, between them, they created something that outlasted all their chasing. Something that would endure long after both their bones had turned to dust. Something that would make people wonder and argue and analyze for centuries.

The portrait captured me, yes—my face, my eyes, my hands, whatever mystery Leonardo claimed to see in my expression that day he posed me just so and told me to think of something amusing.

But it also captured them.

The odd friendship that should never have worked but somehow did. The long pursuit across Italian cities and decades. The moments of admiration and exasperation tangled together like threads in expensive fabric. The feeling of two very different men—genius and merchant, artist and patron, master and devotee—meeting somewhere in between, in that strange space halfway between brilliance and longing, between creating and appreciating, between knowing and seeking.

And perhaps that is as it should be.

Not every story needs a tidy ending. Not every promise must be fulfilled to matter. Not every commission must be delivered to be valuable.

Some things are better left a little unfinished—like a brushstroke waiting for a hand that will never return to complete it, or a smile whose meaning shifts each time you look at it, revealing something new depending on the light, the viewer, the moment.

I sometimes imagine the portrait still resting wherever it hangs now—I've heard so many rumors of its location I no longer know what to believe—surrounded by people who study it with serious faces, who argue passionately over every detail, who insist they can decipher every secret hidden in that smile, every meaning Leonardo embedded in its layers.

Let them try.

Let them debate and analyze and theorize.

They will never know what I know:

That the painting was more than a likeness, more than an image captured in oil and wood.

It was a bridge—between a merchant and a genius, between a man who wanted to be seen and a man who wanted to be understood, between chasing and being found, between loneliness and the one companion who made it bearable.

Leonardo wrote in that last letter: _"Thank you, my friend, for the chase. It made me feel less alone."_

And in the end, that was what Francesco needed too. Not the portrait. Not the proof of his taste or his connections or his place in Leonardo's legacy.

Just the knowledge that he hadn't been alone in his longing. That the chase had mattered to both of them. That the friendship had been real, however strange, however unequal, however impossible it might have seemed to anyone watching from outside.

He needed to know he'd been seen. Valued. Remembered.

And Leonardo, in dying, had given him that gift.

Perhaps that is the true masterpiece.

Not the painting of a woman with an enigmatic smile.

But the story of two men who chased each other through time and space and circumstance, who never quite caught what they were running toward, who left things beautifully, impossibly unfinished.

And who, in that unfinishing, created something that would last forever.

---

_The portrait they call La Gioconda—my portrait, though I barely recognize myself in the descriptions travelers bring—remains in France to this day. I am told it has become one of the most treasured artworks in the world, that people speak of it with reverence, that Leonardo's name has become synonymous with genius partly because of it._

_I am told my smile has become famous. That people write poems about it. That scholars debate its meaning._

_Let them._

_I know what I was smiling about._

_I was thinking of my husband, probably. Francesco, standing outside the workshop yet again, waiting patiently, hoping this time would be different._

_I was thinking: what a ridiculous man I married._

_I was thinking: what a lucky woman I am._

_And Leonardo, somehow, captured both thoughts at once._

_That was his gift. Not just seeing what was there, but what lay beneath._

_The smile wasn't mysterious. It was simply true._

_And truth, as Leonardo knew better than most, is always worth the chase._

_Even if you never quite catch it._

_Especially then._
